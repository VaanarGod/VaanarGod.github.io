Unit Overview
Where Energy Comes From & What It Costs

Unit 6 is about energy transformations — and their environmental consequences. Humans don't use energy up; we convert it from one form to another, and every conversion produces waste heat and pollution. The core question is not which energy source is "clean" but what tradeoffs each source brings across its full life cycle.

Fossil fuels dominate because of their energy density and existing infrastructure, but combustion drives climate change, acid rain, and health impacts. Renewables are growing, but no source is consequence-free. Understanding efficiency, net energy, and externalities is the key to thinking through energy tradeoffs the way the AP exam expects.

01 · Energy Fundamentals

Thermodynamics & Net Energy

Energy is the capacity to do work. Power is the rate of energy use (energy ÷ time). The sun drives almost all Earth's energy — directly (solar, wind, biomass, hydro) or indirectly via ancient photosynthesis (fossil fuels). The First Law: energy cannot be created or destroyed — only converted. The Second Law: every conversion degrades some energy to low-quality heat. No real-world process is 100% efficient.

Energy Quality
High-quality energy (electricity, concentrated fuels) can do more work. Low-quality heat is harder to use. Every conversion steps down quality — this is the Second Law in practice.
Efficiency Formula
Efficiency = useful energy out ÷ total energy in × 100%. A coal plant converting coal to electricity might be ~35% efficient — 65% is waste heat released to cooling water or the atmosphere.
Net Energy / EROI
Net energy = total energy produced − energy required to produce it. EROI (Energy Return on Investment) = energy out ÷ energy in. Higher EROI = more useful energy per unit invested. Conventional oil: ~30:1. Tar sands: ~5:1.
kWh Math
Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (h). 100 W bulb for 10 h = 0.1 kW × 10 h = 1 kWh. Multiply by cost per kWh for savings. Always convert watts → kilowatts first.
Units to Know
Joule (J): SI unit of energy. Watt (W) = 1 J/s. Kilowatt-hour (kWh): billing unit. BTU: heat energy. Quad: 10¹⁵ BTU, used for national statistics.
Energy Flow Chain
Source → extraction → conversion → transmission → end use. Each step has losses. Life-cycle analysis accounts for all steps — not just combustion at the point of use.
Exam Focus
Calculate efficiency from given inputs/outputs. Explain why the 2nd Law makes waste heat unavoidable. Distinguish net energy from gross energy. Connect high EROI to economic viability. Show unit math step-by-step — partial credit for correct setup even with arithmetic errors.
02 · Fossil Fuels Overview

Formation, Combustion & Externalities

Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) are ancient organic matter compressed over millions of years. Combustion releases CO₂, water vapor, and pollutants locked away for geologic time — dramatically raising atmospheric CO₂. They dominate because of high energy density, easy transport and storage, existing infrastructure, and historically low prices. Switching has massive inertia costs.

Combustion Products
Complete combustion: CO₂ + H₂O + heat. Incomplete combustion adds CO, unburned hydrocarbons, soot. Impurities add SO₂ (sulfur) and NOₓ. Each has different environmental outcomes: CO₂ → greenhouse; SO₂ → acid rain; NOₓ → smog and acid rain.
Externalities
Costs not reflected in market price: health damage from air pollution, climate change, water contamination from spills, habitat destruction from extraction — paid by society, not the fuel purchaser.
Life-Cycle Impacts
Extraction: land clearing, habitat loss, spill risk. Refining: air and water pollution. Transport: pipeline leaks, tanker spills. Combustion: GHG + criteria pollutants. Waste: ash, produced water, CO₂.
Why Policy Is Hard
Fossil fuel subsidies, existing infrastructure, political power of the industry, and concentrated short-term economic benefits vs. diffuse long-term harms create powerful barriers to transition. Externalities are the core market failure.
Exam Focus
Trace the full life cycle — not just combustion. Define externality and give examples. Explain WHY market prices understate the true cost of fossil fuels. Connect combustion products to specific environmental problems (SO₂ → acid rain; CO₂ → greenhouse effect).
03 · Coal

Mining, Combustion & Pollution Control

Coal Types / Ranks
Lignite (lowest energy, most moisture) → sub-bituminous → bituminous (most common for power) → anthracite (highest energy, least sulfur). Higher rank = more carbon, less water, more energy per ton.
Surface Mining
Strip mining and mountaintop removal (MTR): remove overburden to expose coal seams. MTR blasts peaks; valley fills bury headwater streams. Faster and cheaper than underground but causes severe surface disruption and AMD.
Underground Mining
Shafts and tunnels preserve surface but risk cave-ins, methane explosions, and black lung disease. Produces acid mine drainage (AMD) when water contacts pyrite (FeS₂) in exposed coal seams.
Acid Mine Drainage
FeS₂ + O₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄. Acidic water dissolves heavy metals, runs into streams, kills aquatic life. Long-lasting — continues decades after mining ends. Hard and expensive to treat.
Combustion Pollutants
CO₂ (climate), SO₂ (acid rain), NOₓ (smog, acid rain), mercury (neurotoxin, bioaccumulates), particulates (respiratory), fly ash (contains toxic metals). Coal is the dirtiest fossil fuel per unit energy.
Pollution Controls
Scrubbers remove SO₂. Electrostatic precipitators catch particulates. Low-NOₓ burners reduce NOₓ. Carbon capture and storage (CCS) can reduce CO₂ but is expensive and not widely deployed. Fly ash requires careful disposal.

Mountaintop Removal — Valley Fill Chain

MTR blasts summit → overburden fills adjacent valleys → headwater streams buried → AMD and sediment runoff downstream → fish kills and macroinvertebrate loss → Reclamation replaces forest with grass — biodiversity rarely recovered
Exam Focus
Distinguish surface vs. underground mining impacts. Explain AMD chemistry (pyrite + water + oxygen → sulfuric acid). Match each pollutant to its control technology. Coal ash contains toxic metals — it's a solid waste problem, not just an air issue. Scrubbers target SO₂, not CO₂.
04 · Oil & Natural Gas

Extraction, Transport & Fracking

Crude Oil Formation
Ancient marine organisms → buried under sediment → heat + pressure over millions of years → liquid hydrocarbons trapped in porous rock. Refining separates crude into gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, plastics feedstocks.
Oil Spill Impacts
Coats feathers and fur → hypothermia. Smothers intertidal habitats and coral. Toxic to fish gills and eggs. Persists in sediments for decades. Dispersants break oil into smaller droplets but don't remove it — ecological debate continues.
Natural Gas
Mostly methane (CH₄). Burns cleaner than coal: less CO₂ per unit energy, less SO₂ and particulates. BUT: methane is a potent GHG (~80× CO₂ over 20 years) — leaks during extraction and transport matter enormously.
Hydraulic Fracturing
High-pressure fluid injected into shale rock → fractures rock → releases trapped oil/gas. Enabled U.S. shale boom. Concerns: water use, wastewater disposal (induced seismicity), groundwater contamination risk from well casing failures.
Tar Sands / Oil Sands
Bitumen (semi-solid oil) mixed with sand. Requires strip mining or steam injection to extract. Energy-intensive processing. Higher CO₂ per barrel than conventional oil. Destroys boreal forest habitat in Alberta, Canada.
Methane Hydrates
Ice-like CH₄ on seafloor and permafrost — vast potential resource. Destabilization risk if permafrost thaws due to climate change could release enormous methane — a dangerous feedback loop.
Exam Focus
Explain how methane leakage undermines natural gas's climate advantage. Describe fracking's water concerns: high use + wastewater injection seismicity + groundwater risk. Connect tar sands to higher lifecycle emissions. Dispersants don't "clean up" spills — they change where the oil goes.
05 · Nuclear Power

Fission, Reactor Design & Waste

Nuclear fission splits heavy atoms (U-235 or Pu-239), releasing enormous heat to make steam and spin turbines — identical to fossil fuel plants, just a different heat source. One uranium pellet ≈ energy of one ton of coal.

Component Role Key Detail
Fuel rods Contain enriched uranium pellets U-235 enriched from ~0.7% to 3–5% for reactor fuel
Control rods Absorb neutrons to slow/stop reaction Made of boron or cadmium; inserted to shut down
Moderator Slows neutrons to sustain chain reaction Typically water (light or heavy) or graphite
Coolant Transfers heat from reactor to steam generator Water most common; loss of coolant → meltdown risk
Pros
Very low operational CO₂. High energy density (small fuel volume). Reliable baseload power — unaffected by sun or wind. No SO₂, NOₓ, or particulates during operation.
Cons
Radioactive waste: spent fuel remains dangerous for 10,000+ years. Thermal pollution from cooling water discharge. Meltdown risk (Chernobyl, Fukushima). Very high construction cost and long build time.
Nuclear Waste
High-level waste stored in spent fuel pools, then dry casks. No permanent U.S. repository exists. Yucca Mountain (Nevada) proposed but politically blocked. Low-level waste from medical/industrial uses — shorter half-lives.
Fusion vs. Fission
All current reactors use fission (splitting atoms). Fusion (combining atoms, like the sun) would produce far less radioactive waste — still experimental; no commercial reactors exist. Do not confuse these on the exam.
Exam Focus
Know each reactor component's function. Explain thermal pollution: hot cooling water reduces dissolved oxygen in rivers/lakes. Distinguish high-level vs. low-level waste. Nuclear is low-carbon operationally but NOT waste-free. APES focuses on tradeoffs, not engineering details.
06 · Solar & Wind

PV, CSP, Turbines & Grid Challenges

Photovoltaic (PV) Solar
Silicon cells convert sunlight directly to electricity. No moving parts, no water use during operation. Modular — rooftop to utility scale. Manufacturing requires energy and materials (cadmium, tellurium). End-of-life panel disposal is an emerging concern.
Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)
Mirrors focus sunlight onto fluid → heat → steam → turbine. Needs large areas and direct sunlight (deserts). Can store thermal energy in molten salt for after-dark generation. High water use for cooling systems.
Passive Solar Design
Building design using south-facing windows, thermal mass (brick, concrete), and insulation to capture and retain solar heat without active systems. Can reduce heating energy by 25–70% in well-designed homes.
Wind Turbines
Wind spins blades → rotates generator → electricity. Best sites: Great Plains, offshore, mountain passes. Onshore: cheap, fast. Offshore: higher and steadier winds, higher cost. Capacity factor ~25–45% — generates only when wind blows.
Wildlife Impacts
Wind turbines kill ~140,000–500,000 birds and 600,000+ bats/year in U.S. — serious for raptors and migratory bats. Careful siting away from migration corridors and use of radar-curtailment systems reduces mortality.
Intermittency Problem
Solar and wind only generate when sun shines or wind blows. Solutions: grid storage (batteries, pumped hydro), geographic diversification, demand response, backup from dispatchable sources (gas, hydro), long-distance transmission.
Exam Focus
Distinguish PV (direct electric) from CSP (thermal → electric). Explain intermittency and at least two solutions. Wind: pros (no combustion, low water use) vs. cons (bird/bat mortality, visual, noise, land use). Both have manufacturing footprints — never call them zero-impact.
07 · Hydropower & Geothermal

Dispatchable Renewables with Local Tradeoffs

Hydroelectric power converts the kinetic energy of flowing water into electricity. Dams store water in reservoirs, releasing it through turbines on demand — the most dispatchable renewable. Generates ~17% of global electricity.

Factor Hydropower Geothermal
How it works Falling water spins turbines Steam/hot water from Earth's interior drives turbines
Dispatchable? Yes — release water on demand Yes — steady baseload, 24/7
Geographic limit Rivers with adequate elevation drop Geologically active zones (Ring of Fire, Iceland)
Main impacts Fish migration blocked, habitat flooded, sediment trapped H₂S gas, land subsidence, local seismicity
Water use High — evaporation from large reservoirs Moderate — closed-loop systems reuse water
Dam Ecological Impacts
Blocks salmon migration → population collapse. Floods terrestrial habitat upstream. Traps sediment → starves downstream deltas and coastal habitats. Alters water temperature and dissolved oxygen. Social impact: displacement of communities.
Pumped Storage Hydro
Uses surplus electricity to pump water uphill → releases through turbines during peak demand. Acts as a giant battery. Most common grid-scale energy storage technology. Critical for integrating intermittent solar and wind.
Run-of-River
Smaller reservoirs, lower ecological disruption — generates less power but more environmentally compatible than large dams. Micro-hydro viable for remote communities without large grid infrastructure.
Geothermal Pros/Cons
Pros: continuous 24/7 power, small footprint, low GHG. Cons: limited to volcanic/tectonic zones, hydrogen sulfide gas emissions, can deplete if overused, occasional induced seismicity from enhanced geothermal systems.
Exam Focus
Know the dam impact chain: migration blocked → sediment trapped → delta erosion → habitat loss. Distinguish geothermal from hydropower on geographic availability. Pumped storage is the key link between intermittent renewables and grid reliability — a high-yield exam concept.
08 · Biomass, Biofuels & Hydrogen

Organic Energy & Energy Carriers

Biomass is biological material (wood, crop waste, animal dung, energy crops) burned or converted for energy. It is renewable only if harvested sustainably — if forests are burned faster than they regrow, it is effectively a fossil fuel in slow motion.

Carbon Neutrality (Conditional)
Biomass is carbon-neutral IF regrowth captures as much CO₂ as burning releases over a relevant timescale. Burning old-growth forests is NOT carbon-neutral — regrowth takes centuries. Crop residues or fast-growing grasses are better.
Biofuels
Ethanol: fermented from corn (U.S.) or sugarcane (Brazil — better energy return). Biodiesel: from vegetable oils or algae. Cellulosic ethanol from crop wastes — more efficient but technically harder. Food vs. fuel tradeoff: corn ethanol raises food prices.
Biogas / Methane
Anaerobic digestion of organic waste (manure, landfill, sewage) produces methane — burned for electricity or heat. Captures methane that would otherwise escape to atmosphere (potent GHG), so net benefit relative to doing nothing with the waste.
Hydrogen as Carrier
Hydrogen is not an energy source — it must be produced using energy. "Green hydrogen" (electrolysis via renewables) = near-zero emissions. "Grey hydrogen" (from natural gas) = significant CO₂. Fuel cells convert H₂ + O₂ → electricity + water.
Food vs. Fuel
Diverting corn/sugar to biofuels raises food prices, pressures land conversion, and may increase net GHG emissions if tropical forests are cleared for plantations. Life-cycle analysis must include land-use change emissions.
Air Pollution from Biomass
Burning wood/biomass releases particulates, CO, and volatile organics — can cause worse local air quality than natural gas. Disproportionately affects low-income communities where wood stoves are the primary heat source.
Exam Focus
Biomass is only carbon-neutral under specific conditions — always qualify. Know the food-vs-fuel tradeoff for corn ethanol. Hydrogen is a carrier, not a source — always ask where the hydrogen comes from. Biogas from waste is usually an environmental win relative to letting methane vent uncontrolled.
09 · Energy Efficiency & Conservation

Demand Reduction: The Fastest Resource

Reducing energy demand is often cheaper and faster than building new supply. Efficiency delivers the same service with less energy input (LED vs. incandescent). Conservation is using less of the service itself (turning lights off). Both matter — and they work together.

Rebound Effect
Efficiency improvements reduce cost per unit → people use more → some efficiency gains are erased. Efficient cars → more driving. Efficient appliances → larger appliances. Policy must account for rebound to realize projected savings.
Building Efficiency
Buildings use ~40% of U.S. energy. Insulation, double-pane windows, HVAC upgrades, cool roofs, passive solar design. LEED certification standard. Retrofitting existing buildings is the largest near-term efficiency opportunity.
Transportation
CAFE standards raise fleet fuel economy. EVs shift emissions to power plant — only better if grid is clean. Mass transit, cycling infrastructure, and walkable urban design reduce vehicle miles traveled (VMT) per capita.
Cogeneration (CHP)
Combined heat and power: captures waste heat from electricity generation for space heating or industrial processes. Can raise overall efficiency from ~35% to 80–90%. Common in universities, hospitals, and industrial plants.
Smart Grids
Digital two-way communication between utility and users → dynamic pricing, demand response (shift loads to off-peak), faster outage detection, better integration of distributed rooftop renewables.
Industrial Efficiency
Industry uses ~30% of U.S. energy. Motor efficiency standards, waste heat recovery, process redesign, and material substitution reduce energy intensity. Iron, steel, cement, and chemicals are most energy-intensive sectors.
Exam Focus
Always distinguish efficiency from conservation with concrete examples. Calculate energy and cost savings step-by-step (watts → kW → kWh → $ saved). Explain the rebound effect and how policy counteracts it. Cogeneration is a high-yield concept — know how it boosts overall system efficiency.
10 · Energy Policy & Transition

Economics, Subsidies & Environmental Justice

Fossil Fuel Subsidies
Governments worldwide subsidize fossil fuel extraction — keeping prices artificially low and discouraging alternatives. Removing subsidies levels the playing field. IMF estimates global fossil fuel subsidies at $5+ trillion/year when health and climate externalities are counted.
Carbon Tax vs. Cap-and-Trade
Carbon tax: fee per ton of CO₂ — simple, predictable price signal. Cap-and-trade: government caps total emissions; companies buy/sell permits. Both internalize the externality of carbon. BC carbon tax cut emissions while growing GDP — a real-world test case.
CAFE Standards
Corporate Average Fuel Economy: sets minimum fleet fuel efficiency for automakers. Has significantly reduced gasoline consumption and CO₂ per mile since 1975. Tightening standards drives automaker innovation.
Renewable Portfolio Standards
State/national mandates requiring utilities to source a percentage of electricity from renewables by a target year. Drives investment in solar and wind. 30+ U.S. states have RPSs; some target 100% clean electricity by 2050.
Environmental Justice & Energy
Fossil fuel infrastructure (refineries, power plants, pipelines) disproportionately located near low-income and minority communities — "sacrifice zones." Energy poverty: inability to afford adequate heat/cooling. A "just transition" must address historic siting inequities.
Net Metering
Lets rooftop solar owners sell excess electricity back to the grid, reducing energy bills. Expands renewable adoption but utility companies resist as it disrupts the traditional business model. Equity issue: mainly benefits homeowners, not renters.
Exam Focus
Explain how carbon pricing internalizes externalities — a key FRQ pattern. Connect environmental justice to energy infrastructure siting. Distinguish carbon tax (per-ton fee) from cap-and-trade (permit market). APES expects policy reasoning — propose a solution and explain its mechanism, not just its name.

Exam Prep

Key Concepts & Strategies

High-yield topics · Pattern recognition · Quick-reference

Efficiency Math

kWh & Savings Calculations

  • Step 1: Convert watts → kilowatts (÷ 1000)
  • Step 2: Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (h)
  • Step 3: Cost savings = kWh saved × $/kWh
  • Step 4: Payback period = upfront cost ÷ annual savings
  • Always label units — partial credit if setup is correct even with arithmetic errors
Externalities Pattern

Market Failure → Policy Fix

  • Externality = cost not paid by buyer/seller → market underprices harmful goods
  • Fossil fuels: pollution, climate, health costs imposed on society
  • Fix: carbon tax or cap-and-trade → raises price → reduces use → drives alternatives
  • Subsidies for renewables correct for underpriced fossil alternatives
  • FRQ: always explain the mechanism, not just name the policy
Source Comparison

Tradeoff Framework

  • Compare on: energy density, CO₂, other pollutants, water use, land use, reliability
  • Coal: highest CO₂ + pollutants, dispatchable, mining destruction, cheap
  • Natural gas: lower CO₂ than coal, methane leak risk, fracking water issues
  • Nuclear: low-carbon operation, radioactive waste, meltdown risk, expensive
  • Wind/Solar: no combustion, intermittent, manufacturing and land impacts
Renewable Nuance

No Free Lunch

  • Solar: semiconductor manufacturing, land use, panel disposal — not zero impact
  • Wind: bird/bat mortality, noise, aesthetics — site carefully away from corridors
  • Hydro: fish migration, sediment, flooded habitat — dispatchable but impactful
  • Biomass: only carbon-neutral if regrowth matches harvest rate
  • Never call any source "clean" without acknowledging at least one tradeoff
Common Mistakes

Avoid These Errors

  • Calling nuclear "100% clean" — it has radioactive waste and thermal pollution
  • Saying biomass is automatically carbon-neutral — depends on regrowth timescale
  • Ignoring methane leaks when praising natural gas — leaks can negate coal's advantage
  • Forgetting life-cycle impacts — solar panels require energy and materials to make
  • Treating efficiency = conservation — they are different concepts with different mechanisms
FRQ Writing

Energy Scenario Tips

  • Name the specific pollutant and its specific impact: SO₂ → acid rain → pH drop → aquatic harm
  • Propose a solution AND explain its mechanism — not just what it is called
  • For efficiency: calculate step-by-step, then explain the environmental benefit
  • For policy: identify the externality first, then show how policy internalizes it
  • Environmental justice: name who lives near the facility and what they're exposed to
