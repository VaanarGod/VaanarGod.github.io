Core Frame
What Unit 1 Is Really About

Unit 1 is the conceptual foundation of APES. Nearly every environmental problem you'll study — pollution, climate change, resource depletion — can only be analyzed after you understand how ecosystems normally work. That means mastering energy flow, nutrient cycling, limiting factors, and trophic dynamics.

The big organizing idea: energy flows one-way through ecosystems, while matter cycles repeatedly. Get this right and eutrophication, biomagnification, and productivity questions become predictable.

Common Mistakes

Avoid These

  • Saying energy cycles like nutrients — energy flows one-way and is lost as heat at every transfer.
  • Saying phosphorus has an atmospheric phase — it doesn't; it cycles through rocks and sediments only.
  • Stopping eutrophication at "algae grow" — always continue: algae die → decomposers consume O₂ → hypoxia → fish kill.
  • Confusing habitat (address) with niche (role/occupation).
  • Calling all deserts "hot" — tundra and some high-altitude zones are cold deserts with very low precipitation.
  • Using GPP as energy available to consumers — it's NPP (GPP − respiration) that consumers can access.
  • Treating positive feedback as "good" — it means self-amplifying, not beneficial.
Study Order

Best Way to Review

  • Start with ecosystem structure: biotic/abiotic, levels of organization, habitat vs. niche.
  • Learn the energy rules: one-way flow, 10% rule, GPP vs. NPP, ecological pyramids.
  • Master all four biogeochemical cycles — water, carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus — with processes and human impacts.
  • Study species interactions and limiting factors: symbiosis, tolerance, carrying capacity.
  • Cover terrestrial biomes using climate graphs: temperature + precipitation → biome.
  • Finish with aquatic systems: zones, stratification, wetland services, coral reefs, eutrophication.

01 · Ecosystem Structure & Species Interactions

How Ecosystems Are Organized

An ecosystem is a defined area where biotic components (living organisms) interact with abiotic components (sunlight, temperature, water, soil, pH, dissolved oxygen) through exchanges of energy and matter. Abiotic conditions set the rules — low dissolved oxygen can kill fish even when food is plentiful.

Organism → Ecosystem
Organism → Species → Population (one species, one area) → Community (multiple species) → Ecosystem (community + abiotic). Adding abiotic factors is what makes a community an ecosystem.
Habitat vs. Niche
Habitat = where an organism lives (its "address"). Niche = its role: how it gets energy, what it eats, when it's active, and how it interacts with biotic/abiotic factors.
Generalists vs. Specialists
Generalists (cockroaches, humans) tolerate wide conditions; resilient to disturbance. Specialists (giant pandas, koalas) thrive in stable, narrow niches but are vulnerable to any change.
Law of Tolerance
Each species has a range of tolerance for each abiotic factor. Outside that range, survival and reproduction decline. Limiting factors restrict population growth — they can be density-dependent (predation, disease) or density-independent (drought, fire).
Carrying Capacity (K)
Maximum sustainable population size given available resources. When limiting factors tighten, K drops. When resources increase, K can rise.
Feedback Loops
Negative feedback: counteracts change → stabilizes systems. Positive feedback: amplifies change → self-reinforcing. "Positive" means amplifying, not beneficial.

Six Species Interaction Types

Interaction Species A Species B Example
Mutualism + + Rhizobium bacteria in legume roots
Commensalism + 0 Barnacles on whales
Parasitism + – Tapeworm in host
Predation + – Wolf eating deer
Competition – – Two plants competing for light
Amensalism 0 – Black walnut releasing juglone

Resource Partitioning

When species overlap in resource use, competition is reduced through morphological partitioning (different structures), spatial partitioning (different areas), or temporal partitioning (different times). This allows similar species to coexist.
02 · Matter Cycling vs. Energy Flow

The Most Important Distinction in Ecosystem Science

Energy flows through ecosystems — it enters as sunlight, moves through trophic levels, and exits as heat. It does not cycle back. Matter cycles within and among ecosystems; the same atoms are reused over and over.

Why Energy Doesn't Cycle
The Second Law of Thermodynamics: energy transfers increase entropy (disorder). Each metabolic step releases some energy as heat — permanently unavailable for biological work.
Why Matter Does Cycle
Atoms (C, N, P, H₂O) are conserved — they're not destroyed. They move among living organisms and abiotic reservoirs (atmosphere, ocean, soil, rock) indefinitely.
Role of Decomposers
Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) and detritivores (earthworms, pill bugs) break down dead organic matter. Without them, nutrients become locked in dead biomass and primary production collapses.
03 · Biogeochemical Cycles

Water · Carbon · Nitrogen · Phosphorus

A biogeochemical cycle tracks a chemical through living organisms (bio), Earth's rocks/soil (geo), and air/water (chemical). Know the major reservoirs, key processes, and human impacts for each.

💧 Water (Hydrologic) Cycle

Key Processes
Evaporation · Transpiration · Evapotranspiration · Condensation · Precipitation · Infiltration · Percolation · Runoff
Distribution
Oceans hold ~97% of Earth's water. Only ~3% is freshwater — most locked in glaciers. Rivers renew in ~16 days; deep groundwater takes ~10,000 years.
Aquifers
Geologic formations storing water. Overextraction causes depletion, land subsidence, and saltwater intrusion. Recharge zones must be protected.
Human Impacts
Urbanization → impervious surfaces → more runoff, less infiltration. Deforestation → less transpiration, more erosion. Agriculture → nitrate/phosphate runoff → eutrophication.

🌿 Carbon Cycle

Key Processes
Photosynthesis (removes CO₂) · Respiration (releases CO₂) · Decomposition · Combustion · Ocean–atmosphere exchange · Sedimentation (long-term storage)
Major Reservoirs
Marine sediments/rocks (largest) → Oceans → Fossil fuels → Soil organic matter → Atmosphere → Terrestrial biomass
Ocean Acidification
CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid → lowers ocean pH. Disrupts coral reef formation and shell-building organisms. Already measurably acidifying oceans.
Human Impacts
Burning fossil fuels (geologic → atmospheric carbon). Deforestation (reduces uptake, releases stored carbon). Both increase atmospheric CO₂ driving climate change.

🌱 Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is ~78% of the atmosphere as N₂, but most organisms cannot use N₂ directly. Nitrogen is often a limiting nutrient for terrestrial plant growth.

Nitrogen Fixation
N₂ → NH₃/NH₄⁺. Done by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in legume roots) and lightning. Industrial Haber-Bosch process also fixes N at massive scale.
Nitrification
NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ by nitrifying bacteria. Makes nitrogen available for plant uptake as nitrate.
Denitrification
NO₃⁻ → N₂ (gas) by anaerobic bacteria. Returns nitrogen to atmosphere. Can also produce N₂O — a potent greenhouse gas.
Human Impacts
Synthetic fertilizers, livestock waste, fossil fuel combustion → excess reactive nitrogen → eutrophication, acid rain, NOx smog, N₂O emissions (GHG).

🪨 Phosphorus Cycle

No Atmosphere
Unlike N and C, phosphorus has no significant atmospheric phase. It cycles only through rock weathering → soil/water → organisms → sedimentation. This makes it slow and often limiting.
Key Processes
Weathering releases phosphate from rocks. Plants absorb phosphate ions. Decomposition returns P to soil/water. Sedimentation locks P in sediments for long periods.
Human Impacts
Phosphate fertilizers and detergents → runoff → eutrophication in freshwater (P is often limiting in lakes). Slow recovery because sediments can continue releasing P.

Eutrophication: The Full Chain

Human input: Nitrogen/phosphorus fertilizer runoff into a lake Step 1 → Nutrients increase → algae and cyanobacteria bloom rapidly Step 2 → Algae die in large quantities Step 3 → Decomposers (bacteria) break down dead algae Step 4 → Decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen (hypoxia) Step 5 → Fish and other aerobic organisms suffocate → die-off Step 6 → "Dead zone" — low-oxygen water with little aquatic life Key: ALWAYS include the oxygen-depletion step. Stopping at "algae grow" = incomplete.
04 · Energy in Ecosystems & Trophic Dynamics

Producers, Consumers & the 10% Rule

Ecosystems run on energy captured by producers through photosynthesis. As energy moves up trophic levels, ~90% is lost as heat at each transfer. This inefficiency explains why top predators are rare and food chains are short.

Photosynthesis
CO₂ + H₂O + light → glucose + O₂. Producers (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) capture solar energy. Rate is limited by CO₂, light, water, and temperature.
Cellular Respiration
Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP. All organisms respire, releasing energy for metabolism. Often described as the reverse of photosynthesis.
Trophic Levels
Producers (1st) → Primary consumers/herbivores → Secondary consumers → Tertiary consumers. Arrows in food webs show direction of energy transfer (prey → predator).
10% Rule
Only ~10% of energy at one trophic level transfers to the next as new biomass. The other ~90% is lost as heat through metabolism, movement, incomplete digestion, and waste.
Energy Pyramid
Always upright — energy decreases at higher levels. Biomass pyramids can be inverted in aquatic systems (fast-reproducing phytoplankton support heavier zooplankton).
Bioaccumulation vs. Biomagnification
Bioaccumulation: toxin builds up in one organism over time. Biomagnification: toxin concentration increases at each higher trophic level because predators eat many contaminated prey.

10% Rule — Worked Example

Producers store: 20,000 kJ Primary consumers receive: 20,000 × 0.10 = 2,000 kJ Secondary consumers receive: 2,000 × 0.10 = 200 kJ Tertiary consumers receive: 200 × 0.10 = 20 kJ → Tertiary consumers receive only 0.1% of original producer energy → This is why large predators need vast territories and are rare

Trophic Cascade & Keystone Species

A trophic cascade is a ripple effect across trophic levels from a change at one level. A keystone species has a disproportionately large impact relative to its abundance — losing one can restructure an entire ecosystem.

Classic example: Sea otters → sea urchins → kelp forests. Remove otters → urchin population explodes → kelp is overgrazed → kelp forest collapses → loss of habitat for hundreds of species.

05 · Primary Productivity

GPP, NPP & What Controls Them

Primary productivity is the rate at which producers convert solar energy to chemical energy (biomass). It sets the entire energy budget for food webs above it.

GPP
Gross Primary Productivity — total energy captured by producers through photosynthesis before any is used for respiration.
NPP
Net Primary Productivity = GPP − Respiration. Energy actually available to consumers and decomposers. Always use NPP, not GPP, for available-energy questions.
Terrestrial Controls
Temperature and water availability dominate. Tropical rainforests (warm + wet) = highest NPP. Tundra and deserts (cold or dry) = lowest NPP.
Aquatic Controls
Nutrients (N, P), light depth, temperature, and upwelling. Open ocean is nutrient-poor at surface → low NPP per area despite covering ~70% of Earth.
NPP Calculation: GPP = 2,500 units · area⁻¹ · time⁻¹ Respiration (R) = 1,100 units · area⁻¹ · time⁻¹ NPP = GPP − R = 2,500 − 1,100 = 1,400 units → 1,400 units available to herbivores, decomposers, and higher trophic levels
06 · Terrestrial Biomes

Temperature + Precipitation → Biome

Biomes are large ecological regions defined by climate and characteristic plant communities. The two key variables are temperature and precipitation. Identify biomes from climate graphs on the exam.

Tropical Rainforest
Near equator. High, year-round rainfall (>200 cm). Highest biodiversity and NPP. Soils nutrient-poor (nutrients in biomass). Rapid decomposition.
Temperate Deciduous Forest
Four seasons, moderate precip (75–150 cm). Fertile soils (leaf litter). E. North America, Europe, NE Asia. Trees shed leaves in winter.
Temperate Grassland
Moderate precip, insufficient for trees. Deep fertile soils. Maintained by fire and grazing. Great Plains, steppes, pampas, veldts.
Desert
<50 cm precip/year. Can be hot or cold (tundra is a cold desert). Plants: succulents, deep or shallow roots, CAM photosynthesis, waxy coatings.
Boreal Forest (Taiga)
Largest terrestrial biome. Cold, short summers. Evergreen conifers. Thin, acidic soils. Slow decomposition. Permafrost possible at northern edge.
Tundra
Arctic or alpine. Permafrost limits roots and drainage. Very low NPP. Short growing season (~50 days). Simple food webs. Cold desert precipitation levels.
Savanna
Tropical grassland with scattered trees. Pronounced wet/dry seasons (~50–130 cm precip). Seasonal fires maintain structure. Africa, Australia, S. America.
Chaparral (Shrubland)
Hot dry summers, mild wet winters. Fire-adapted plants. Mediterranean climates. Periodic fire is part of natural disturbance regime.
07 · Aquatic Systems

Freshwater · Wetlands · Marine · Estuaries

Aquatic biomes are shaped by salinity, depth/light, nutrients, temperature, turbidity, and dissolved oxygen. Cold water holds more DO than warm water — critical for eutrophication questions.

Lake Zones
Littoral (shallow, rooted plants) → Limnetic (open, phytoplankton) → Profundal (deep, dark, low O₂) → Benthic (bottom, decomposers).
Lake Stratification
Summer: warm epilimnion over cold hypolimnion, separated by thermocline. Fall/spring turnover mixes layers, redistributing O₂ and nutrients.
Lake Trophic State
Oligotrophic (young, clear, low nutrients) → Mesotrophic → Eutrophic (old, murky, nutrient-rich, low O₂). Fertilizer pollution accelerates this transition.
Wetlands
Flood control, carbon sinks, water filtration, groundwater recharge, nursery habitat. Degraded by agriculture, drainage, development, logging, invasive species, and overgrazing.
Estuaries
Where freshwater meets saltwater. Highly productive. Critical nursery for fish/shellfish. Vulnerable to nutrient runoff and pollution.
Coral Reefs
Warm, shallow, clear water. Highest marine biodiversity. Corals + zooxanthellae (photosynthetic algae). Sensitive to temperature (bleaching), acidification, and physical damage.
Thermohaline Circulation
Global ocean "conveyor belt" driven by temperature and salinity differences. Distributes heat globally (e.g., Gulf Stream warms N. Europe). Climate change could disrupt it.
Dissolved Oxygen
Essential for aerobic aquatic life. Increases with cold temperature, turbulence, photosynthesis. Decreases with warm temperature and decomposition. Eutrophication creates hypoxic dead zones.
08 · Biodiversity & Ecosystem Services

Why Life's Variety Matters

Biodiversity — the variety of life — exists at three levels: genetic, species, and ecosystem. Higher biodiversity generally increases resilience (the ability to recover after disturbance) through functional redundancy and genetic variation.

Species Richness vs. Evenness
Richness = number of species. Evenness = how equally individuals are distributed. A community can have high richness but low evenness (one species dominates) — both matter for diversity assessment.
Habitat Fragmentation
Breaking large habitats into smaller patches reduces population size, gene flow, and interior habitat. Edge effects (changed temperature, humidity, predators) make fragment edges ecologically different from interiors.
Provisioning Services
Tangible products: food, fresh water, timber, fiber. These are what people most easily recognize as nature's "goods."
Regulating Services
Climate regulation (carbon storage), flood control, water purification, disease regulation. Often invisible until lost.
Supporting Services
Nutrient cycling, soil formation, primary productivity — underlying functions that enable all other services.
Cultural Services
Recreation, tourism, spiritual and educational value. Often undervalued in economic analyses but important for human well-being.

FRQ Example: Wetland Conversion

Scenario: City replaces a wetland with a shopping center. Chain of consequences: Impervious surface replaces permeable wetland → More stormwater runoff (less infiltration) → Higher flood risk downstream → Loss of water filtration capacity → More pollutants entering waterways → Loss of nursery habitat → decline in local fish populations → Loss of carbon sink → increased atmospheric CO₂ For full credit: identify the service lost AND explain the ecological mechanism.

AP Exam Strategies

FRQ patterns, traps, and the cause-and-effect method that earns points

The FRQ Chain

How to Structure Any Ecosystem Answer

  • Identify the human action and which cycle/process it disrupts.
  • Trace the disruption through the cycle (which reservoir changes, what process is altered).
  • Predict the ecological consequence — population, community, or ecosystem level.
  • Extend to a human consequence if asked (water quality, fisheries, health).
  • Propose a mitigation — connect it back to the disrupted process (not just "use less fertilizer").
Cycle Traps

Biogeochemical Cycle Pitfalls

  • Phosphorus has NO atmospheric reservoir — it cycles only rock → soil/water → organisms.
  • Nitrogen fixation ≠ nitrification. Fixation: N₂→NH₃. Nitrification: NH₄⁺→NO₃⁻.
  • Eutrophication requires the oxygen depletion step — don't stop at algae growth.
  • Groundwater renews slowly — deep aquifers take ~10,000 years, not days or decades.
  • Ocean acidification is from CO₂ dissolving, not acid rain (that's sulfur/nitrogen oxides).
Energy Rules

Trophic Level Questions

  • Always use NPP (not GPP) as the energy available to consumers.
  • Arrows in food web diagrams point from prey to predator (direction of energy flow).
  • Biomass pyramids can be inverted in aquatic systems — fast phytoplankton turnover means low standing biomass despite high production rate.
  • Bioaccumulation = one organism over time. Biomagnification = across trophic levels.
  • Endotherms lose more energy as heat than ectotherms → lower transfer efficiency.
Biome ID

Reading Climate Graphs

  • High, year-round precip + warm = Tropical rainforest.
  • Cold all year + very low precip = Tundra or Boreal forest.
  • Hot dry summer, mild wet winter = Chaparral.
  • Moderate precip, four seasons = Temperate deciduous forest.
  • Low precip (any temperature) = Desert. Don't assume deserts are hot.
  • Latitude alone doesn't determine biome — altitude and ocean currents also matter.
Aquatic Systems

Water Ecosystem Patterns

  • Cold water holds more dissolved oxygen than warm water.
  • Eutrophication → algae bloom → decomposers spike → O₂ drops → hypoxia → die-off.
  • Oligotrophic = young, clear, low nutrients. Eutrophic = old, murky, high nutrients.
  • Estuaries are highly productive because of nutrient mixing — critical nursery habitat.
  • Coral reefs can have inverted biomass pyramids and are sensitive to temperature & pH.
Ecosystem Services

Services FRQ Template

  • Always name the category of service (provisioning, regulating, supporting, cultural).
  • Connect the service loss to a specific ecological mechanism — not just "it's important."
  • Wetland conversion → impervious surface → less infiltration → more flooding + less groundwater recharge.
  • Distinguish provisioning (water supply) from regulating (flood control) — both come from wetlands but through different mechanisms.
