Unit Overview
Shared Resources & Human Impact

Unit 5 explores how humans extract, manage, and degrade land and water resources. Every topic connects to a central tension: individual benefit vs. shared cost. Garrett Hardin called this the Tragedy of the Commons — when a shared resource is overused because no single user bears the full cost of depletion.

Whether it's fishing in the open ocean, pumping groundwater, clearing forests, or paving farmland, the same pattern repeats: short-term incentives undermine long-term sustainability. Unit 5 asks you to identify the pattern and propose realistic solutions.

Erosion, salinization, and waterlogging reduce productivity. No-till, contour plowing, terracing, and cover crops protect topsoil.

Fisheries

Urbanization

Impervious surfaces increase runoff, reduce infiltration, and create heat islands. Smart growth uses density, mixed-use, and green infrastructure to reduce impact.

01 · Shared Resources

Tragedy of the Commons

Common-pool resources are rival (your use reduces what's left) but non-excludable (hard to keep others out) — fisheries, aquifers, the atmosphere, grazing land.

In 1968, Garrett Hardin's Tragedy of the Commons described how rational individuals overuse shared resources: each user captures the full benefit of one more unit of use but shares only a fraction of the cost.

Regulation (top-down)
Catch limits, grazing permits, groundwater pumping caps. Requires monitoring and enforcement to work.
Privatization
Owner has incentive to maintain future value. Works for land; harder to apply to fish or air.
Community Governance
Local rules, monitoring, enforcement. Works when group is well-defined and compliance is verifiable.
Commons Examples
Fisheries, groundwater aquifers, open grazing land, the atmosphere (CO₂), old-growth forests.
Exam Focus
Explain WHY a resource is vulnerable (rival + hard to exclude). Propose and justify a management strategy. Identify the "individual benefit vs. shared cost" mechanism in scenarios.
02 · Forestry & Deforestation

Clear-Cutting, Edge Effect & Mitigation

Clear-Cutting
All trees cut at once. Creates habitat loss, more sunlight → hotter/drier ground, increased runoff & erosion, loss of carbon sinks, reduced biodiversity.
Edge Effect
Environment changes at habitat boundaries. Canopy removal shifts microclimate: hotter, drier, windier — harms interior-forest species.
Deforestation Impacts
Runs off into aquatic systems → erosion → drier soils → 80% of land species threatened → habitat fragmentation → CO₂ emissions from burning/decay.
Mitigation
Uneven-aged management, longer rotations, reduced road-building, enforcement of logging laws, sustainable methods, reducing fragmentation.
Exam Focus
Outcomes of clear-cutting: erosion, habitat loss, carbon sink reduction, microclimate change. Connect edge effect to biodiversity. Always include the hydrology: runoff and erosion follow canopy removal.
03 · Agriculture

Systems, Revolutions & the Green Revolution

Subsistence vs. Commercial
Subsistence: feeds local community, low inputs, locally adapted. Commercial: market-oriented, mechanized, fertilized, irrigated — maximizes yield and profit.
Monoculture
Single crop over large area — efficient for machinery but low diversity makes pest/disease spread easy. Relies heavily on pesticides and fertilizers.
Polyculture / Intercropping
Multiple crops simultaneously on same land. Reduces pest outbreaks, improves soil health, increases ecological resilience.
Agricultural Revolutions
1st: domestication (2000+ BCE). 2nd: industrialization (1700–1900). 3rd: mechanization + biotech (1900–present).

Green Revolution

Component What Changed Environmental Cost
High-Yield Varieties (HYVs) More food per hectare — reduced hunger risk Reduced genetic diversity of crop plants
Synthetic Fertilizers Consistent nutrient supply, higher yields Runoff → eutrophication downstream
Expanded Irrigation Farming in dry regions becomes possible Groundwater depletion, salinization
Pesticides Reduced crop losses Non-target species harm, resistance, bioaccumulation
GMOs (2nd wave) Bt corn (insect resistance), Golden Rice (Vitamin A) Ecological concerns, monoculture risk
Common Mistakes
The Green Revolution is NOT just "new seeds" — include fertilizers + irrigation + pesticides + mechanization. Do NOT confuse it with organic farming. Tradeoffs exist in all directions.
04 · Rangelands & Land Degradation

Overgrazing, Desertification & Slash-and-Burn

Rangelands
Native grasslands, wetlands, woodlands, and deserts managed via grazing and prescribed fire rather than intensive cultivation.
Overgrazing
Plants grazed faster than roots recover — root growth can drop by up to 90%. Removes vegetation, increases erosion and compaction.
Desertification
Productive potential of arid/semiarid land declines by ≥10% from human activity and/or climate. NOT simply "desert expanding naturally."
Slash-and-Burn
Clear-cut and burn wild land — provides short-term ash nutrients, but drives deforestation and CO₂ emissions; soil degrades if fallow periods are too short.
Exam Focus
Diagnose overgrazing/desertification from a scenario. Explain how slash-and-burn gives short-term benefits but causes long-term degradation. Always emphasize human drivers.
05 · Soil Degradation & Conservation

Erosion, Salinization, Waterlogging & Solutions

Degradation Type Cause Key Detail
Water Erosion Bare soil + rain + slope Loses nutrient-rich topsoil; sediment pollutes waterways
Wind Erosion Bare, dry, exposed fields Fine fertile particles removed; prevented by windbreaks
Salinization Irrigation water evaporates, leaves salts Common in arid regions — water balance problem, NOT fertilizer
Waterlogging Over-irrigation saturates soil O₂ depleted in root zone; co-occurs with salinization
Nutrient Depletion Harvest removes nutrients without replenishment Requires compost, manure, or fertilizer to restore

Conservation Practices

Contour Plowing
Plow along slope contours → ridges hold water → slows runoff. Best for hilly cropland (water erosion).
Terracing
Flat steps on steep slopes drastically reduce runoff speed. High upfront cost, very effective long-term.
No-Till / Cover Crops
No-till leaves structure intact, reduces erosion. Cover crops protect soil between seasons and add organic matter.
Windbreaks & Strip Cropping
Windbreaks: trees perpendicular to wind (wind erosion). Strip cropping: alternating crop rows slow both wind and water erosion.
Exam Focus
Match practice to erosion type (wind vs. water, slope vs. flat). Salinization ≠ "too much fertilizer" — it's salt accumulation from evaporation. Erosion is also a water-quality problem downstream.
06 · Irrigation

Methods, Efficiency & Environmental Consequences

Method How It Works Water Efficiency Main Risk
Flood Irrigation Fields flooded; water flows among crops Lowest High water loss, waterlogging
Furrow / Ditch Water flows down small channels by gravity Low–moderate Infiltration loss at head; erosion
Spray / Sprinkler Overhead sprinklers distribute water Moderate Wind drift and evaporation losses
Drip Irrigation Water drips directly to root zone via tubes Highest Higher cost; tube clogging
Salinization from Irrigation
Especially in dry climates — irrigation water evaporates, leaving dissolved salts behind. Reduces yield over time. Hard to reverse once severe.
Waterlogging
Over-irrigation raises water table, saturating root zone. Oxygen depleted → plants suffer. Often co-occurs with salinization.
Aquifer Depletion
Pumping exceeds recharge → water table drops → wells fail → connected wetlands/streams shrink. "Invisible" until crisis.
Exam Focus
Compare irrigation methods by efficiency and environmental impact. Explain WHY dry climate irrigation causes salinization (evaporation leaves salts). Propose drip irrigation + scheduling as solutions.
07 · Pest Control & IPM

Pesticides, Resistance & Integrated Pest Management

Pesticide Types
Insecticides (insects), herbicides (weeds), fungicides (fungi). Also: organophosphates (toxic, short-lived), POPs (persistent, bioaccumulate), biological pesticides (living organisms).
Pesticide Treadmill
Resistance builds → farmers use more → more resistance. Natural selection favors resistant individuals already in the population — the pesticide doesn't "make" pests resistant.
Bioaccumulation & Biomagnification
Persistent fat-soluble pesticides concentrate in fatty tissue and increase up the food web. Top predators receive the highest doses.
Non-Target Impacts
Pollinators, natural predators, and aquatic organisms all at risk. Runoff carries pesticides into waterways. Spray drift affects nearby habitats.

IPM Components

  • Regular monitoring (visual inspection + traps + record-keeping)
  • Prevention first: crop rotation, resistant varieties, habitat for beneficial insects
  • Biological controls: natural predators, parasites, pathogens of the pest
  • Mechanical/physical controls: barriers, traps, sterilized insect release
  • Intercropping and polyculture to slow pest spread
  • Pheromones and hormone interrupters
  • Chemical pesticides as a LAST RESORT — targeted, rotated to slow resistance
IPM Reduces: → Bioaccumulation and biomagnification → Chance of resistance to any single pesticide → Destruction of beneficial/non-target organisms → Overall pesticide use and associated runoff
Exam Focus
Resistance evolves because resistant individuals already exist and survive — it's selection, not adaptation. Biological control is NOT always safe (invasive risk). IPM is a SYSTEM, not a single tactic.
08 · Meat Production & CAFOs

Grazing, Feedlots & Environmental Impacts

Rangeland Grazing
Uses land unsuitable for crops. Risk: overgrazing degrades vegetation and soil. Compaction and habitat fragmentation are concerns.
Feedlots / CAFOs
CAFO = Concentrated Animal Feeding Operation (confined >45 days/year). Efficient meat production but concentrates waste. Depends on cropland for feed (indirect land/fertilizer impacts).
Water Pollution
Manure contains soluble nitrogen and phosphorus. Spills and runoff contaminate surface and groundwater. States with high CAFO concentration average 20–30 serious water-quality problems per year.
Air Pollution
Manure decomposition releases ammonia, hydrogen sulfide, methane, and particulate matter. Methane is a significant greenhouse gas from livestock systems.
Exam Focus
Compare grazing vs. CAFOs with one advantage and one disadvantage each. Connect meat production to land use change, nutrient runoff, and water pollution. Waste concentration is the central CAFO issue.
09 · Fisheries & Aquaculture

Overfishing, Bycatch & Management

Coastal waters make up less than 10% of the world's ocean area yet contain about 90% of all marine species — making coastal habitat especially critical. Fisheries are classic commons: exclusion is difficult, and harvesting directly reduces the breeding population.

Overfishing
Harvest exceeds reproduction. Breeding population drops → recovery slow or impossible. Improved technology (sonar, large nets, refrigeration) increases pressure.
Bycatch
Unintentional capture of non-target species (turtles, dolphins, juvenile fish). Some gear physically damages seafloor habitat.
Aquaculture (Mariculture)
Fish farming reduces wild fishery pressure but creates waste/nutrient pollution, disease spread, escape risks, and still depends on wild-caught fishmeal for feed.
Management Tools
Catch limits/quotas, size limits, seasonal closures, gear restrictions, marine protected areas, licenses, trade sanctions, elimination of fishing subsidies.
Exam Focus
Aquaculture does NOT automatically solve overfishing. Bycatch is a major impact even when target catch is within limits. Propose solutions beyond biology: subsidies, labeling, import restrictions, sanctuaries.
10 · Mining

Extraction Methods & Environmental Damage

Method Description Key Impact
Open Pit Large pit dug where deposit is near surface Massive land disturbance, acid drainage
Strip Mining Soil removed over each coal seam Habitat destruction, erosion, sediment runoff
Mountaintop Removal Mountaintops blasted to expose coal; debris in valley fills Aquatic habitat destruction from valley fill runoff
Contour Mining Follows ridge contours along a hillside seam Slope erosion, acid drainage
Dredging Material scooped from water body bottom Habitat disturbance in aquatic systems
In Situ / Leaching Chemical solvents injected through small holes Groundwater contamination risk
Room & Pillar / Longwall Underground; rotating drum or half coal left as pillars Land subsidence, dust, worker health
Exam Focus
Match method to likely land disturbance pattern. Water-quality threats (acid mine drainage, in situ leaching) are high-yield topics. AP often gives descriptions rather than names — read carefully.
11 · Urbanization & Sprawl

Impervious Surfaces, Runoff & Smart Growth

Impervious Surfaces
Roads, rooftops, parking lots prevent infiltration → more runoff → flooding, stream bank erosion, groundwater recharge decreases.
Urban Heat Island
Dark surfaces absorb heat; reduced vegetation lowers evapotranspiration cooling. Cities are measurably warmer than surrounding rural areas.
Urban Sprawl
Low-density, car-dependent development. Single-family homes on large lots, single-use zoning, job sprawl, loss of agricultural land.
Smart Growth
Compact, walkable, transit-oriented, mixed-use development. Greenbelts around cities, mass transit subsidies, incentives to locate in urban centers.
Urban Runoff Pollution
Carries oil, metals, fertilizers, pesticides, sediment, trash into waterways. Thermal pollution as urban heat transfers to streams — harms fish and aquatic wildlife.
Green Infrastructure Solutions
Bioswales, rain gardens, permeable pavement, green roofs, constructed wetlands, retention basins. Mimic natural hydrology, filter pollutants.
Exam Focus
Explain the mechanism: impervious surfaces → less infiltration → more runoff (not "cities are bad"). Per-capita land use can be LOWER in dense cities than suburbs. Hydrology is heavily tested — don't focus only on air.
12 · Sustainable Agriculture

Long-Term Resource Management

Soil Health Practices
No-till, cover crops, compost, green manure, crop residues, perennial crops. Build organic matter, improve water retention, reduce erosion.
Biodiversity Strategies
Polyculture, intercropping, crop rotation, agroforestry, habitat for beneficial insects. Reduce pest outbreaks and improve ecosystem services.
Organic Agriculture
Natural inputs; reduced synthetic pesticides and fertilizers. Tradeoff: lower yields in some contexts may require more land. NOT pesticide-free — some natural pesticides allowed.
Ecological Footprint
Biologically productive land + sea needed to supply resources and absorb waste. Connected to the IPAT equation: I = P × A × T.
Planetary Boundaries (Selected): Biodiversity loss: Boundary = 10 extinctions/million/yr | Current: >100 | EXCEEDED Climate change: Boundary = 350 ppm CO₂ | Current: ~420 | EXCEEDED Freshwater use: Boundary = 4000 km³/yr | Current: 2600 | Not exceeded Land use: Boundary = 15% cropland | Current: 11.7% | Not exceeded
Exam Focus
Sustainability requires combining strategies — no single practice fixes everything. "Organic" isn't automatically more sustainable on all dimensions. Use IPAT as a conceptual framework, not a precise calculator.

Key Concepts & Strategies

High-yield topics · Pattern recognition · Quick-reference

Commons Pattern

Why Resources Get Overused

  • Individual captures FULL benefit of one more unit of use
  • Cost shared across ALL users → each bears only a fraction
  • Result: every individual is incentivized to overuse
  • Solutions: regulation (caps), privatization, community governance
  • Easy to control: land, lakes, forests. Hard: open ocean, atmosphere
Irrigation Consequences

Salinization vs. Waterlogging

  • Salinization: water evaporates → salts left behind → arid regions
  • Waterlogging: over-irrigation raises water table → saturated roots
  • Both can come from irrigation but through DIFFERENT mechanisms
  • Fix: drip irrigation + scheduling + improved drainage
  • Aquifer depletion: "invisible" until wells start failing
Pesticide Resistance

How the Treadmill Works

  • Resistant individuals already EXIST in the population
  • Pesticide kills susceptible ones → resistant ones reproduce
  • Pesticide did NOT "create" resistance — it selected for it
  • IPM slows resistance by rotating modes of action + using as last resort
  • Biological control ≠ always safe — invasive potential
Urbanization Hydrology

Impervious Surface Chain

  • Impervious surface → less infiltration → more runoff → more flooding
  • Less infiltration → less groundwater recharge → wells decline
  • Runoff carries oil, metals, nutrients, pesticides → water pollution
  • Green infrastructure mimics natural hydrology to reduce these
  • Dense cities often have lower per-capita land use than suburbs
CAFO Impacts

Concentrated Waste Problem

  • CAFO: confined animals >45 days/year in large numbers
  • Water: nitrogen + phosphorus in manure runoff → eutrophication
  • Air: ammonia, H₂S, methane, particulates from manure decomposition
  • Indirect: feedlots need cropland for feed (secondary land/fertilizer use)
  • ~20–30 serious water-quality problems/year in high-CAFO states
Green Revolution Summary

Yield ↑ + Environmental Cost

  • HYV seeds + synthetic fertilizer + expanded irrigation + pesticides
  • Dramatically raised food production, reduced famine risk
  • Cost: fertilizer runoff, groundwater depletion, reduced genetic diversity
  • 2nd wave: GMOs (Bt corn, Golden Rice) + biotechnology
  • Do NOT confuse with organic farming — nearly opposite approach
