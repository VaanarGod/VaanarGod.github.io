Unit 5 · Voting Rights & Behavior

VOTING RIGHTS & BEHAVIOR

AMENDMENTS · VRA 1965 · TURNOUT · RATIONAL-CHOICE · RETROSPECTIVE

Constitutional Expansion of Voting Rights
Constitutional Amendments
THE FOUR AMENDMENTS ⚡ AP

The right to vote was expanded through four constitutional amendments, each responding to political exclusion.

15th Amendment (1870): Prohibits denying the vote based on race. Required the Voting Rights Act (1965) to actually enforce.
19th Amendment (1920): Prohibits denying the vote based on sex. Women's suffrage movement — decades of organized protest.
24th Amendment (1964): Bans poll taxes in federal elections. Poll taxes were a classic tool of disenfranchisement.
26th Amendment (1971): Lowers voting age to 18. Driven by Vietnam era: "old enough to fight, old enough to vote."
HIGH FREQ
Federal Legislation
VOTING RIGHTS ACT 1965 ⚡ AP

The most significant civil rights law protecting voting, the VRA targeted systematic racial discrimination — especially in the South.

National standards: Banned discriminatory practices like literacy tests nationwide.
Federal oversight (Section 5): Certain jurisdictions needed pre-clearance before changing election rules — a "preclearance" requirement.
Shelby County v. Holder (2013): Supreme Court struck the coverage formula. Pre-clearance still exists but lost its enforcement teeth.
HIGH FREQ
Voting Models · Behavior
HOW VOTERS DECIDE ⚡ AP

AP Gov tests four main voting behavior models. Know each, apply each to a scenario.

Rational-Choice: Self-interest — vote for the candidate whose policies benefit you. Uses party label as a shortcut.
Retrospective: "Are we better off?" Judge incumbents on past performance, especially the economy.
Prospective: Forward-looking — choose who will handle future problems best, based on promises and competence.
Party-Line: Vote for your party across all races. Reflects polarization and party identification.
HIGH FREQ
Election Administration
TURNOUT & BARRIERS

Even eligible voters face structural barriers. Costs of participation shape who shows up.

Voter ID laws: Supported to prevent fraud; criticized as burdening lower-income and minority voters.
Motor Voter (NVRA 1993): Expanded registration opportunities at DMVs and government agencies.
Political Efficacy: Internal (I can participate) vs. External (government will respond). Low efficacy → disengagement.
Socialization: Family, education, media, peers all shape political values — family is the most influential.

Unit 5 · Political Parties

POLITICAL PARTIES

TWO-PARTY SYSTEM · PRIMARIES · POLARIZATION · PARTY FUNCTIONS

Party Structure & Function
Functions
WHAT PARTIES DO ⚡ AP

Parties are linkage institutions — they connect citizens to government. They perform five core functions.

Recruit candidates: Identify, support, and train potential officeholders.
Mobilize voters: GOTV efforts — canvassing, phone banks, voter registration drives.
Organize government: Parties structure Congress — committee assignments, leadership, floor scheduling.
Set policy agenda: Party platforms translate ideology into legislative priorities.
Provide voting cues: Voters use party label as a shortcut when uninformed about specific candidates.
HIGH FREQ
System Structure
TWO-PARTY SYSTEM ⚡ AP

The U.S. two-party system is structurally reinforced — not accidental. Winner-take-all elections make third parties nearly impossible.

Duverger's Law: Plurality (winner-take-all) elections tend to produce two-party systems — third parties waste votes.
Primary elections: State-run elections for party nominees. Opens selection but can boost ideological extremes.
Electoral College: Favors major parties — no third party has won electoral votes since 1968 (Wallace).
Caucus vs. Primary: Caucuses are party-run meetings; primaries are government-run. Front-loading moved both earlier.
HIGH FREQ
Polarization
PARTY SORTING & POLARIZATION

The parties have become more ideologically distinct and more geographically sorted — a major shift from the mid-20th century.

Party sorting: Liberals moved to Democrats; conservatives to Republicans. Fewer moderates in either party.
Affective polarization: Parties don't just disagree on policy — they increasingly dislike and distrust each other.
Divided government: President and Congress from different parties — leads to gridlock or more moderate compromises.
MED FREQ
Elections & Redistricting
ELECTIONS & GERRYMANDERING ⚡ AP

District lines shape who wins and who's represented. Gerrymandering is a key AP topic.

Reapportionment: Redistribution of House seats after the census. States gain or lose based on population shifts.
Gerrymandering: Drawing district lines to favor one party. Packing (concentrate opponents) and cracking (split them).
Majority-minority districts: Districts drawn to give minority groups majority representation — raises both VRA and equal protection questions.
Incumbency advantage: Name recognition, franking, casework, and PAC access all benefit incumbents.
HIGH FREQ
Unit 5 · Interest Groups

INTEREST GROUPS

LOBBYING · PACS · IRON TRIANGLES · FREE-RIDER · CITIZENS UNITED

How Groups Influence Policy
Organization Theory
THE FREE-RIDER PROBLEM ⚡ AP

If benefits are collective (clean air, lower taxes), why would anyone pay to organize? This is the fundamental challenge all interest groups face.

Free-rider problem: Rational individuals will benefit from group success without paying dues or participating. Hard to overcome.
Selective benefits: Groups solve this by offering exclusive perks to members — magazines, discounts, access, social solidarity.
Material, purposive, solidary: Three types of benefits — tangible goods, policy goals, social belonging.
HIGH FREQ
Influence Tactics
LOBBYING & ACCESS ⚡ AP

Lobbying is about information and access, not just money. Groups that can provide useful expertise get the most influence.

Direct lobbying: Meeting privately with officials to provide information, arguments, and research.
Amicus curiae briefs: "Friend of the court" filings — groups not party to a case can submit legal arguments.
Grassroots lobbying: Mobilize members to contact officials — legislators care about constituent contacts.
Revolving door: Former officials become lobbyists using insider relationships — now subject to waiting period rules.
HIGH FREQ
Campaign Finance
PACS, SUPER PACS & MONEY ⚡ AP

Campaign finance law has been rewritten by two court decisions that changed the role of money in elections.

PACs: Political Action Committees — give limited amounts directly to candidates. Regulated by FECA (1974).
Buckley v. Valeo (1976): Spending money on campaigns = First Amendment free speech. Expenditure limits struck down.
Citizens United (2010): Corporations and unions can spend unlimited independent money on elections — Super PACs born.
Independent expenditures: Spending not coordinated with a candidate — allowed to be unlimited.
HIGH FREQ
Policy Models
IRON TRIANGLES & NETWORKS

Interest groups don't just lobby — they embed in the policymaking process through stable relationships with agencies and committees.

Iron triangle: Three-way stable relationship — congressional committee + bureaucratic agency + interest group. Self-reinforcing.
Issue networks: Broader, more fluid coalitions of groups, experts, agencies, and officials — less stable but more dynamic.
Pluralism vs. elitism: Pluralists say many groups compete; elitists say wealthy organized interests dominate. Both capture something true.

Unit 5 · Media & Communication

MEDIA & COMMUNICATION

AGENDA-SETTING · FRAMING · PRIMING · SELECTIVE EXPOSURE · SOCIAL MEDIA

How Media Shapes Politics
Media Effects
AGENDA-SETTING ⚡ AP

Media doesn't tell you what to think — it tells you what to think about. Coverage drives salience.

Agenda-setting: Issues that receive sustained media coverage become the issues the public considers most important.
Framing: How a story is presented activates different values and interpretations. "Tax relief" vs. "tax cuts" frames the same policy differently.
Priming: Media coverage of certain issues shapes what criteria citizens use to evaluate candidates and the president.
HIGH FREQ
Media Consumption
SELECTIVE EXPOSURE & SILOS

Audiences increasingly choose media that confirms prior beliefs — a structural feature of fragmented media markets.

Selective exposure: People seek out information that confirms what they already believe, avoiding dissonant information.
Algorithms: Social media platforms personalize feeds to maximize engagement — which reinforces existing views.
Affective polarization: Information silos where different groups inhabit different factual realities drives mutual distrust.
MED FREQ
Media Industry
STRUCTURAL BIAS

Media bias is often structural (driven by incentives) more than ideological (driven by reporter views).

Negativity bias: Bad news gets more coverage than good. Conflict, crisis, and scandal attract more viewers.
Horse-race coverage: Campaign news focuses on polling and strategy rather than policy substance.
Watchdog function: Free press investigates government corruption — essential for accountability but weakened by local news collapse.
MED FREQ
Linkage Institutions
MEDIA AS LINKAGE INSTITUTION

Media is one of four linkage institutions connecting public opinion to government action.

Four linkage institutions: Political parties, interest groups, elections, and media. Each translates public preferences into policy pressure.
Social media & mobilization: Lowers costs of organizing — enables grassroots movements (#MeToo, BLM, Tea Party) to emerge rapidly.
Misinformation: False information spreads faster than corrections, amplifying extreme voices that generate more engagement.
HIGH FREQ
Unit 5 · Economic & Domestic Policy

POLICY

FISCAL · MONETARY · ENTITLEMENTS · TRADE · DOMESTIC SAFETY NET

Economic Policy Framework
Fiscal vs. Monetary
TWO TOOLS OF ECONOMIC POLICY ⚡ AP

Economic policy operates through two distinct levers — fiscal (Congress/President) and monetary (Federal Reserve).

Fiscal policy: Government taxing and spending. Controlled by Congress and the President. Stimulus = increase spending/cut taxes. Austerity = opposite.
Keynesian economics: Government should counteract recessions through deficit spending and stimulate demand.
Supply-side economics: Cut taxes, especially on investment, to grow the economy. "Trickle-down" — favored by conservatives.
Monetary policy: Federal Reserve controls money supply and interest rates. Independent from elected government.
HIGH FREQ
The Federal Reserve
THE FED'S THREE TOOLS ⚡ AP

The Federal Reserve uses three tools to manage the money supply and control inflation vs. unemployment.

Reserve requirement: Minimum fraction banks must hold. Lower = banks lend more = money supply grows.
Discount rate: Rate at which banks borrow from the Fed. Lower rates → cheaper credit → more lending.
Open market operations: Buy/sell U.S. bonds. Buying = more reserves in banking system = more money supply.
HIGH FREQ
Entitlement Programs
SOCIAL SAFETY NET

Mandatory spending programs — funded by law rather than annual appropriations — dominate the federal budget.

Social Security: Major entitlement. Funded through payroll taxes. Politically difficult to reform because of elderly voters.
Medicare / Medicaid: Medicare for 65+; Medicaid for low-income (joint federal-state). Both are mandatory spending.
Mandatory vs. discretionary: Mandatory required by law (entitlements + debt payments); discretionary set annually by Congress.
Social insurance vs. public assistance: Social insurance earned through contributions; public assistance for people in need regardless.
MED FREQ
Budget Process
BUDGETING & TRADE POLICY

The federal budget process involves multiple actors; trade policy involves competing domestic interests.

OMB: Office of Management and Budget initiates the budget; helps write the president's request to Congress.
Government shutdown: Failure to pass spending bills by Oct. 1 fiscal year start; Congress passes continuing resolutions as stop-gap.
Trade deficit/surplus: Imports exceed exports = deficit (wealth "outflow"). Political debate: free trade vs. protectionism.
NAFTA/USMCA: North American free trade — reduced tariffs, controversial: labor unions feared job loss; economists debated net benefit.
MED FREQ

UNIT 5 SECTIONS

Voting Rights, Election Rules & Voting Behavior

Political participation starts with a basic question: who is allowed to participate, and on what terms? In the United States, voting is the most visible form of participation, but it has never been equally accessible to everyone. Strong answers connect constitutional rules, federal laws, court decisions, and real-world political strategies.

Voting rights: what they are and why they matter

Voting rights are the legal rules that determine who can vote and how voting is carried out. They matter because elections translate public preferences into governmental power. If access to voting is unequal, then representation becomes unequal.

At the national level, four amendments set broad boundaries: 15th (1870, race), 19th (1920, sex), 24th (1964, poll taxes), 26th (1971, age 18). A common misconception is that these amendments "guaranteed" full and immediate voting access — in practice, states used workarounds for decades.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965

The Voting Rights Act (VRA) of 1965 is one of the most significant civil rights laws in U.S. history. It targeted racial discrimination in voting, especially in places where exclusion was systematic. Two essential features: national standards banning discriminatory practices, and federal oversight requiring certain jurisdictions to pre-clear election rule changes.

In Shelby County v. Holder (2013), the Supreme Court struck down the VRA's coverage formula that determined which jurisdictions required federal oversight. This did not eliminate the VRA entirely, but it changed how certain protections operated.

Voting behavior models

Rational-choice voting assumes self-interest: voters weigh costs and benefits of candidates. Retrospective voting judges past performance ("Are we better off?"). Prospective voting looks forward to future promises. Party-line voting uses party identity as a consistent cue across all races.

EXAM FOCUS
  • Explain how a constitutional amendment expanded voting rights — and why enforcement still required additional law.
  • Apply a voting behavior model to a short scenario.
  • Analyze how a change in election rules could affect participation among specific groups.
  • Do NOT assume voting restrictions are automatically unconstitutional — the key is discriminatory impact.

Voter Turnout & Forms of Participation

Voting is only one form of participation, and even voting varies dramatically across groups and elections. Political participation is best understood as a mix of motivation, resources, and mobilization.

What turnout is and why it changes

Voter turnout is the percentage of eligible voters who cast ballots. Turnout matters because it affects representation — if only certain groups vote, elected officials will be more responsive to those groups. Turnout changes for two broad reasons: differences in people (interest, education, age, income, political efficacy) and differences in the election environment (competitiveness, mobilization, barriers, salience).

Political efficacy

Political efficacy is the belief that you can influence politics. Internal efficacy: "I can understand and participate." External efficacy: "Government will respond to citizens." Low external efficacy causes disengagement because the system feels unresponsive.

Political socialization

Political socialization is how people form political values. Key agents: family (most influential), school, peers, media, religious institutions, and life experiences. Political socialization explains why political values tend to persist across generations.

Forms of participation beyond voting

Donating, volunteering, protesting, contacting officials, and social media activism all count. Linkage institutions — parties, interest groups, elections, and media — connect public opinion to government by providing information, mobilizing participation, and translating preferences into policy.

Political Parties & the Party System

Political parties are essential linkage institutions. They perform multiple functions that connect citizens to government and structure political competition.

Party functions

Parties recruit and nominate candidates, mobilize voters (GOTV), organize government (committee assignments, legislative leadership), set policy agendas, and provide voting cues. Party identification ("I think of myself as a Democrat") is one of the strongest predictors of vote choice.

Why we have a two-party system

Structural features reinforce two parties: plurality (winner-take-all) elections, the Electoral College, ballot access laws, and campaign finance rules. Duverger's Law argues single-member districts with plurality rules tend to produce two-party competition because voters fear "wasting" votes on third parties.

Primary elections and front-loading

Primary elections are state-run elections in which voters choose party nominees. Open primaries allow any registered voter; closed primaries restrict to party members. Front-loading moved primaries earlier — "Super Tuesday" can effectively determine the nominee before most states vote.

Polarization and party sorting

The parties have become more ideologically distinct and geographically sorted. Affective polarization — disliking the opposing party — has increased as much as policy polarization. Divided government occurs when different parties control different branches.

EXAM FOCUS
  • Explain why the U.S. has a two-party system using structural features (winner-take-all, Electoral College).
  • Compare open vs. closed primaries and their effects on nominee ideology.
  • Apply party identification as a voting cue in a scenario.

Interest Groups

Interest groups are organizations that try to influence government policy on behalf of their members. They are neither political parties nor electoral organizations — they do not nominate candidates.

The free-rider problem and selective benefits

The free-rider problem: if benefits are collective, rational individuals will benefit without contributing. Groups solve this through selective benefits — exclusive perks available only to members (magazines, discounts, professional networking, social solidarity).

Lobbying strategies

Lobbying is about information, access, and expertise — not just money. Tactics include direct lobbying (private meetings), congressional testimony, amicus curiae briefs, grassroots mobilization, and endorsements. The revolving door — former officials becoming lobbyists — is regulated by waiting period requirements.

Campaign finance and court cases

FECA (1974) established limits and disclosure requirements. Buckley v. Valeo (1976) held that spending money on campaigns is protected speech — expenditure limits struck down. Citizens United v. FEC (2010) allowed corporations and unions to spend unlimited amounts on independent political expenditures, creating Super PACs.

Iron triangles and issue networks

Iron triangles: stable three-way relationships among a congressional committee, a bureaucratic agency, and an interest group. Each benefits the others. Issue networks: broader, more fluid coalitions — less stable but more representative.

Elections: Rules, Cycles & Outcomes

Elections structure participation by determining when people vote, what choices are available, and what incentives candidates face.

Federal election timing

House elected every two years (all seats). Senate six-year terms, ~1/3 up every two years. Presidential elections every four years; between them are midterm elections. Presidents typically lose House seats in midterm elections — a structural pattern driven by the "in-party penalty."

Electoral College

Each state gets electors = Representatives + Senators. 270 needed to win. Mostly winner-take-all by state. Encourages focus on "swing states." Critics argue it distorts representation; defenders argue it forces candidates to build broad geographic coalitions.

Redistricting and gerrymandering

Reapportionment: census every 10 years redistributes House seats. Redistricting: states redraw district lines. Gerrymandering: manipulating lines to favor one party via packing (concentrate opponents in one district) and cracking (split opponents across multiple districts). Majority-minority districts can comply with VRA by ensuring minority representation.

Incumbency advantage

Incumbents benefit from name recognition, franking privilege, constituent service (casework), access to PAC money, and gerrymandered districts. House incumbency re-election rates often exceed 90%.

Campaigns, Polling & Public Opinion

Modern campaigns require both money and information — they use polling to track opinion and target messaging.

Campaign finance

Presidential campaigns use matching funds (public financing system), but most major candidates opt out to raise more privately. Citizens United allowed outside spending to explode. Dark money — spending by nonprofits that don't disclose donors — has grown dramatically.

Public opinion and polling

Public opinion polling methodology affects results: sampling (random vs. non-random), question wording, question order, and response scales. Sampling error is reduced by larger representative samples. Horse-race polling covers who's winning rather than policy positions.

Political socialization and opinion formation

Family is the single most influential agent of political socialization. Media, education, religion, and life experience also shape opinion. Demographic variables (age, race, gender, income, education, region) correlate with political preferences — but no variable perfectly predicts vote choice.

Mandate theory

A mandate is a claimed public endorsement of the winner's agenda. Presidents routinely claim mandates after winning. But mandates are empirically hard to verify — voters choose for many reasons. A 52–48 margin doesn't show agreement on each specific policy position.

Media & Political Communication

Mass media is simultaneously a socialization agent, an agenda-setter, a watchdog, and a commercial industry. The commercial incentives shape coverage regardless of ideological intent.

Three core media effects

Agenda-setting: media determines which issues become salient — what people think about, not necessarily what they think. Framing: how a story is presented activates different values (a tax cut "framed" as relief vs. as benefiting the rich). Priming: heavy coverage of an issue shapes what criteria voters use to evaluate the president.

Selective exposure and polarization

Selective exposure: audiences choose confirming media. Social media algorithms accelerate this. Creates information silos where different groups inhabit different factual realities. Major driver of affective polarization.

Structural bias

Structural bias (commercial incentives: conflict, negativity, simplicity, celebrity) may be more consequential than ideological bias. Politicians actively shape their media coverage through staged events, press releases, and social media. The relationship is two-way: politicians need coverage; media needs access.

EXAM FOCUS
  • Distinguish agenda-setting, framing, and priming — three related but distinct concepts.
  • Apply selective exposure to explain media's role in polarization.
  • Don't claim media "controls" opinion in all cases — explain conditions under which effects are strongest.

Policymaking Process

Policymaking involves multiple stages and actors — from agenda-setting through implementation to evaluation.

The policy process

Stages: agenda-setting (what problems become policy priorities), policy formulation (developing solutions), adoption (passing law or issuing regulation), implementation (bureaucracy executes), evaluation (assessing effectiveness). Problems can fail at any stage.

Why popular policies can fail

Veto points multiply — bicameralism, presidential veto, filibuster (60 Senate votes), and judicial review each require additional hurdles. Well-organized minority interests often defeat majority opinion through lobbying, litigation, and campaign finance. Salience matters: if most people weakly support a policy but intense minorities oppose it, intensity wins.

Linkage institutions and responsiveness

Parties, interest groups, elections, and media translate public opinion into policy — imperfectly. Policy feedback: policy shapes future public opinion (Social Security creates beneficiaries who defend it politically). Visible, widely used programs entrench constituencies.

Economic Policy

Economic policy operates through two distinct tools: fiscal policy (taxing and spending, controlled by Congress and the President) and monetary policy (money supply, controlled by the Federal Reserve).

Fiscal policy

Fiscal policy is government taxing and spending decisions. Expansionary: increase spending or cut taxes (Keynesian stimulus). Contractionary: cut spending or raise taxes. The OMB drafts the president's budget; House Ways and Means handles revenue; Appropriations committees set spending levels.

Monetary policy and the Fed

The Federal Reserve is independent — insulated from electoral pressure. Tools: reserve requirements (how much banks must hold), discount rate (rate at which banks borrow from Fed), open market operations (buying/selling Treasury bonds to inject or withdraw money). Low rates stimulate borrowing; high rates control inflation.

Mandatory vs. discretionary spending

Mandatory spending is required by existing law — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, veterans' pensions, interest on debt. Discretionary spending is set annually by appropriations — defense, education, research, infrastructure. Mandatory spending dominates the budget and is politically difficult to change.

EXAM FOCUS
  • Distinguish fiscal vs. monetary policy in a scenario — who controls each?
  • Explain how a Fed tool (reserve requirement, discount rate, open market operations) affects interest rates.
  • Identify mandatory vs. discretionary spending in a budget question.

Trade Policy

Trade policy affects domestic jobs, prices, and economic growth. It involves competing interests: exporters (support free trade), import-competing industries (support protectionism), and consumers (benefit from lower prices under free trade).

Key terms

Balance of trade: imports vs. exports. Trade deficit: imports exceed exports. Trade surplus: exports exceed imports. Tariffs: taxes on imported goods. Trade wars occur when countries retaliate with counter-tariffs.

Major agreements

GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade): multilateral framework to reduce tariffs, evolved into the WTO. NAFTA (1994): reduced tariffs among U.S., Canada, and Mexico — controversial among labor unions worried about job loss to lower-wage production. Revised as USMCA (2018).

Domestic Policy & the Social Safety Net

Domestic policy debates often reflect ideological disagreements about the role of government. Liberals generally favor government provision of social welfare; conservatives generally favor individual responsibility and limited government.

Social insurance vs. public assistance

Social insurance programs are funded through taxes paid by employees and employers — beneficiaries view benefits as "earned" (Social Security, unemployment insurance). Public assistance programs are targeted aid for people in need — recipients do not "pay in" directly (Medicaid, SNAP).

Major programs

Social Security provides retirement and disability income. Medicare provides health coverage for 65+. Medicaid provides health services for low-income individuals — jointly funded by federal and state governments. SSI (Supplemental Security Income) supports disabled people near poverty level. All are entitlements: eligibility is set by law, not annual appropriations.

Great Society and welfare reform

Johnson's Great Society (1960s) dramatically expanded federal welfare programs. Reagan administration (1980s) sought to reduce or restructure programs. The 1996 welfare reform act converted AFDC to TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) — block grants to states with work requirements and time limits.
