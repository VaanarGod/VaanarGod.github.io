Core Frame
What Unit 3 Is Really About

Unit 3 is where biology meets human society. You'll study how populations grow, what stops them, and how human population dynamics drive environmental change. The thread connecting every section: population size isn't the only driver — per-capita consumption and technology matter too.

The two big models to master: J-curve (exponential growth) — no limits, grows forever until it crashes — and S-curve (logistic growth) — growth slows as it approaches carrying capacity. Everything else (r/K strategies, survivorship curves, DTM) builds on these.

Common Mistakes

Avoid These

  • Forgetting to divide by 10 when converting CBR−CDR (per 1,000) to growth rate (%).
  • Confusing CBR (births per 1,000) with TFR (average children per woman). They measure different things.
  • Thinking K-selected species can't go extinct — they're MORE vulnerable because they recover slowly.
  • Calling weather events (drought, frost) density-dependent — they are density-independent.
  • Assuming a low TFR immediately stops growth — population momentum from a young age structure sustains growth for decades.
  • Thinking Stage 2 of the DTM has high death rates — Stage 2 is when death rates drop while birth rates stay high.
  • Treating carrying capacity as fixed — it can change with drought, habitat loss, invasives, or climate.
  • Saying lower population solves environmental problems — per-capita consumption matters just as much.
Calculation Cheatsheet

Key Formulas

  • Growth Rate (%) = (CBR − CDR) ÷ 10  [global, no migration]
  • National Growth Rate (%) = (CBR + immigration − CDR − emigration) ÷ 10
  • Doubling Time = 70 ÷ growth rate (%)
  • CBR = (births ÷ population) × 1,000
  • CDR = (deaths ÷ population) × 1,000
  • Population Density = total population ÷ total area
  • % Rate of Change = (new − old) ÷ old × 100
  • Pop. Growth Rate (raw counts) = (births + immigration − deaths − emigration) ÷ population × 100

Sections
01 · Population Basics: Definitions, Niches & Dispersal

What a Population Is & How It Spreads

A population is a group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time. Population size is always linked to resource availability, habitat conditions, predation, disease, and — for humans — economics and culture. Never treat population growth as happening in isolation.

Generalists vs. Specialists

Trait Generalist Species Specialist Species
Resource use Wide variety of resources Specific, narrow resource set
Tolerance range High (temperature, salinity, etc.) Low — narrow environmental window
Adaptability Handles change well; can switch foods/habitats Vulnerable if specific resource/habitat disappears
Extinction risk Less likely to become extinct More likely to become extinct
Examples Humans, raccoons, crows Pandas, koalas, specialist birds

Dispersal Patterns

Clumped Dispersal
Organisms cluster in patches. Common where resources are patchy or where grouping offers protection (prey herds, social families). Most common dispersal pattern in nature.
Random Dispersal
Uniform conditions + little interaction among individuals → random placement. Wind-dispersed plants (dandelions) often show this. Relatively rare.
Uniform Dispersal
Individuals evenly spaced, often to minimize competition. Shaped by territorial behavior or resource spacing. Example: nesting territories, some desert shrubs.

Biotic Potential & Environmental Resistance

Biotic potential is the maximum reproductive capacity under ideal conditions. Environmental resistance is any factor inhibiting population increase (disease, predation, limited resources, harsh weather). The interplay of these two determines actual population size.

02 · Life History Strategies: r-Selected vs. K-Selected

How Species Allocate Energy to Survive & Reproduce

r = intrinsic rate of increase (max growth rate under ideal conditions). K = carrying capacity (long-term sustainable population size). These letters anchor the two life history extremes. In reality, it's a spectrum — species are not strictly "either/or."

Trait r-Strategists K-Strategists
Offspring number Many; tend to overproduce Few
Parental care Low to none High — invest heavily per offspring
Maturation speed Rapid Slow
Lifespan Short Long
Body size Small Tend to be larger
Main limiting factor Density-independent (weather, disasters) Density-dependent (competition, predation, parasitism)
Population pattern Wide fluctuations; can crash Stabilizes near carrying capacity K
Survivorship type Type III (high early mortality) Type I or II (survival to near max lifespan)
Extinction risk Less likely to be endangered More likely to be endangered — slow recovery
Examples Insects, bacteria, algae, rodents, annual plants Humans, elephants, sharks, whales, cacti
Exam Key
After a major disturbance (cleared field, fire), r-strategists colonize first and dominate early succession. K-strategists recover slowly after overharvesting — this is why overfishing large, slow-reproducing fish is so damaging.
03 · Survivorship Curves

When Do Individuals Die?

A survivorship curve shows the proportion of a cohort (same age group) that survives over time. It reveals when mortality is concentrated — early in life, evenly, or late. The shape directly reflects life history strategy.

Type I — Late Loss
High survival through early and middle life; steep drop in old age. Few offspring, high parental care, long-lived. Deaths concentrated near biological limit. Examples: humans, sheep, elephants. Associated with K-strategies.
Type II — Constant Loss
Roughly constant mortality at every age → steady decline. Predation often drives death at all ages. Organisms reach adult stages quickly. Examples: rodents, many songbirds, some perennial plants.
Type III — Early Loss
Very high mortality early in life; survivors live long. Many offspring, little parental care, early death from predation/environment. Examples: sea turtles, trees, fish, oysters, marine invertebrates. Associated with r-strategies.
Reading a survivorship curve on the exam: Steep early drop, then flattens = Type III → many offspring, low parental care, r-strategist Stays high, then drops steeply at old age = Type I → few offspring, high care, K-strategist Steady diagonal decline = Type II → constant mortality; predation at all ages
04 · Carrying Capacity, Limiting Factors & Feedback

What Stops Populations from Growing Forever

Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population size an environment can sustain long-term given available resources. K is NOT fixed — it changes with rainfall, seasons, land use, nutrient inputs, invasive species, and climate. A useful guiding principle: Liebig's Law of the Minimum — even if most factors are favorable, the factor in shortest supply determines growth.

Density-Dependent Factors
Effects intensify as population density increases. Competition for food/nesting sites, disease transmission, predation, parasitism, intraspecific competition. These create negative feedback that pushes population back toward K.
Density-Independent Factors
Effects unrelated to population density. Drought, floods, hurricanes, temperature extremes, pollution, volcanic eruptions. Can cause large crashes regardless of population size. Never call these density-dependent on the exam.
Bottom-Up Control
Population regulated by resource availability (food, nutrients, habitat). If resources increase → population can increase. Nutrient inputs to an ecosystem can raise carrying capacity.
Top-Down Control
Population regulated by predators, parasites, or disease. Predation pressure keeps prey low even when resources are plentiful. Removing a predator can trigger prey population explosion.
Overshoot & Dieback
Population temporarily exceeds K → overbrowse/deplete resources → habitat damage → K drops → dieback (crash). Example: deer after predator removal → overbrowse → starvation → crash.
Predator-Prey Cycles
Prey increases → predators increase (more food) → prey decreases (more predation) → predators decrease. Predators lag behind prey in population peaks. Classic: Lynx-snowshoe hare cycle.
05 · Population Growth Models

J-Curves, S-Curves & the Rule of 70

Two models describe how populations change over time. Knowing which curve a graph shows — and why — is a core exam skill.

Exponential Growth (J-Curve)
Growth proportional to current size: dN/dt = rN. Occurs when resources are unlimited. Produces a J-shaped curve — rapid increase then abrupt stop or crash when a limiting factor hits. Examples: early invasive spread, post-disturbance colonization, early human history.
Logistic Growth (S-Curve)
Growth slows as population approaches K: dN/dt = rN(1 − N/K). The S-curve shows fast early growth, inflection point, then leveling off at K. Most real populations follow something like this in stable environments.

Rule of 70 — Doubling Time

Doubling Time = 70 ÷ growth rate (%) Examples: Growth rate 1% → doubles in 70 years Growth rate 2% → doubles in 35 years Growth rate 3.5%→ doubles in 20 years Growth rate 7% → doubles in 10 years Remember: larger growth rate = FASTER doubling (smaller number) Populations cannot double forever — limiting factors always apply.

J-Curve vs. S-Curve: Key Distinctions

Feature J-Curve (Exponential) S-Curve (Logistic)
Resource assumption Unlimited resources Finite resources; limiting factors apply
Growth rate Constant; accelerates with size Decreases as N approaches K
Endpoint Crash or abrupt stop Levels off at carrying capacity K
When it occurs New habitat, early invasion, post-disturbance, r-strategists Most natural populations in stable environments
Equation dN/dt = rN dN/dt = rN(1 − N/K)
06 · Human Population Metrics & Formulas

Measuring How Human Populations Change

Human demographic measures link population change to resource demands and environmental impacts. Exam questions frequently require calculations — show your work clearly and state the units.

Core Definitions

CBR — Crude Birth Rate
Births per 1,000 people per year. "Crude" = not adjusted for age structure. A young population can have a high CBR even with moderate fertility.
CDR — Crude Death Rate
Deaths per 1,000 people per year. An aging population may have a high CDR even with good healthcare. Not a direct measure of healthcare quality.
TFR — Total Fertility Rate
Average number of children a woman is expected to have over her lifetime. A clearer family-size indicator than CBR because it's not distorted by age structure. Replacement level ≈ 2.1 in developed countries.
IMR — Infant Mortality Rate
Deaths of infants under age 1 per 1,000 live births per year. Strongly linked to clean water, sanitation, nutrition, vaccination, and maternal health. Key development indicator.

Key Formulas

Global Growth Rate (%) = (CBR − CDR) ÷ 10 National Growth Rate (%) = (CBR + immigration rate − CDR − emigration rate) ÷ 10 Raw-count version: Growth Rate (%) = (births + immigration − deaths − emigration) ÷ population × 100 Doubling Time = 70 ÷ Growth Rate (%) CBR = (births ÷ population) × 1,000 CDR = (deaths ÷ population) × 1,000 Population Density = population ÷ area % Rate of Change = (new − old) ÷ old × 100

Worked Example

Given: CBR = 30, CDR = 10 (per 1,000/year), no migration data Step 1 — Growth Rate: (30 − 10) ÷ 10 = 2%/year Step 2 — Doubling Time: 70 ÷ 2 = 35 years Interpretation: At 2%/year, this population doubles in 35 years. If CBR = 28, CDR = 8: growth = (28−8)÷10 = 2% → same result.

Factors That Reduce TFR Over Time

Access to women's education, economic opportunities for women, affordable contraception and reproductive healthcare, declining child mortality (reduces pressure to have extra children for "insurance"), social safety nets reducing dependence on children in old age, and urbanization (higher costs, less need for farm labor). These are the mechanisms the exam expects you to explain — not just list.

07 · Age-Structure Diagrams & Population Momentum

Who Is in the Population Matters as Much as How Many

An age-structure diagram (population pyramid) shows the distribution of a population across age groups, often separated by sex. It reflects birth rate, death rate, generation time, migration, and past events (wars, pandemics, baby booms). A key concept: population momentum — growth continues after fertility drops if large cohorts are moving into reproductive years.

Pyramid Shape (Rapid Growth)
Wide base: many individuals in younger age groups, high birth rates. Predicts continued growth. Puts strain on schools, childcare, and job creation. Common in developing nations early in the DTM.
Bell Shape (Stable/Slow Growth)
Pre-reproductive and reproductive groups roughly equal; post-reproductive smaller due to mortality. Characteristic of stable populations, often DTM Stage 3–4.
Urn Shape (Declining)
Narrow base: birth rates below death rates. Aging population; healthcare and retirement systems under strain. Common in parts of Europe and East Asia today.
Population Momentum
Even if TFR drops to replacement level today, the population can keep growing for decades because a large young cohort is still entering their reproductive years. A wide-base pyramid = strong momentum.
FRQ Example: Country: CBR = 28, CDR = 8, wide-base pyramid, expanding girls' education Step 1: Current growth = (28−8)÷10 = 2%/year Step 2: Doubling time = 70÷2 = 35 years Step 3: Wide base → strong population momentum even if TFR falls soon Step 4: Education + contraception → TFR declines over time → CBR falls Prediction: Growth rate declines long-term but positive for decades due to momentum
08 · Demographic Transition Model, Worldviews & Policy

How Societies Move From High to Low Birth & Death Rates

The Demographic Transition Model (DTM) describes the shift from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates as a country industrializes. It is a model — not a law — and countries vary in timing and pathway. Death rates typically fall before birth rates because public health improvements (sanitation, vaccines) are faster than social/economic changes.

Stage Birth Rate Death Rate Growth Context
Stage 1 Pre-Industrial High High Low/stable Food scarcity, poor sanitation, limited healthcare. High births replace high mortality.
Stage 2 Transitional High ↓ Falling Rapid increase Industrialization begins. Hygiene, sanitation, vaccines → death rates drop. Birth rates stay high. Population surges.
Stage 3 Industrial ↓ Falling Low Slowing Urbanization, female education, contraception, economic incentives for smaller families → birth rates decline. Growth slows.
Stage 4 Post-Industrial Low Low Near zero Both rates low. Population stabilizes. Higher standard of living.
Stage 5 (optional) Below replacement Low-rising Declining Birth rates fall below death rates. Aging population; immigration may sustain size. Parts of Europe/East Asia.

Historical Population Surges

Tool Use (~3.5 Ma)
First major expansion of human range and resource access. Estimated 1–3 million humans as hunter-gatherers.
Agricultural Revolution (~10,000 BCE)
Shift from hunting/gathering to crop-growing and animal domestication → sustained food surpluses → ~50 million humans by 8000 BCE.
Industrial & Medical Revolutions (~1650–present)
Vaccines, antibiotics, sanitation, improved nutrition, mechanized agriculture → death rates plummeted. Growth rate 0.1% in 1650 → ~1.2% today. Population: ~600 million → 8+ billion.
Environmental Worldviews
Frontier: exploit resources as quickly as possible. Planetary Management: humans manage Earth; technology will solve resource limits. Earth Wisdom: resources are limited; sustain natural cycles for all species, not just humans.

Environmental Impacts of Population Growth

Growing populations strain freshwater (finite supply, rising demand), forests (≈16 million hectares lost annually), food supply (25% of world malnourished; 2 billion hectares of arable land degraded), fisheries and coasts (estuaries drained, fish stocks declining), and public health (12+ million die/year from dirty water and poor sanitation). Remember: population size alone doesn't determine impact — per-capita consumption multiplies the effect.
