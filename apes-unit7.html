01 · Introduction

Air Pollution Basics

Air pollution occurs when harmful substances enter Earth's atmosphere in concentrations that damage health, ecosystems, or materials. Concentration is typically expressed in parts per million (ppm) or ppb. The atmosphere is not just a container — it's a chemical reactor where pollutants are emitted, transported by wind, transformed by sunlight, and removed by deposition.

Point vs. Non-Point
Point source: identifiable single source (e.g., a smokestack). Non-point source: many diffuse sources not attributable to one location (e.g., citywide vehicle emissions). Most urban smog is non-point.
Six Criteria Pollutants (CAA)
CO · NO₂ · SO₂ · Particulate Matter (PM) · Tropospheric Ozone · Lead (Pb). These are regulated by National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS). Know each pollutant's major source and health impact.
Measuring Concentration
ppm = parts per million by volume. ppb = parts per billion. Higher concentration = worse exposure. Exam prompts often give concentration and ask you to identify source, conditions, and impacts.
Emission vs. Exposure
Emission is what leaves the source. Exposure is what reaches the receptor (person/ecosystem). Wind, inversions, and distance affect how much emitted pollution becomes actual exposure.
Exam Focus
Classify a source as point vs. non-point. Know the six criteria pollutants and match each to sources and impacts. Interpret ppm concentrations in a scenario. Don't confuse "criteria pollutants" with "any important pollutant."
02 · Atmospheric System

Layers, Inversions & Dispersal

The atmosphere has multiple layers. For APES, two matter most: the troposphere (lowest, where weather and smog occur) and the stratosphere (above, where the protective ozone layer sits). Same ozone molecule — very different consequences depending on altitude.

Thermal Inversion
Normally warm air rises and mixes. In a temperature inversion, a warmer air layer sits atop cooler surface air, capping vertical mixing. Pollutants accumulate near the ground. Common at night, in valleys, during high-pressure systems, and near cold ocean currents.
Wind & Precipitation
Wind dilutes pollutants by mixing with cleaner air. Precipitation removes them through wet deposition (rain/snow carrying dissolved pollutants). Calm, dry conditions = worst air quality days.
Residence Time
How long a pollutant stays in the atmosphere before removal. Short-lived → local problem. Long-lived (like CFCs) → regional or global. PM10 settles in hours; PM2.5 persists weeks; CFCs last decades in the stratosphere.
Urban Heat Island
Cities are warmer than surroundings due to impervious surfaces, waste heat from buildings/vehicles, and lack of vegetation. Warmer air speeds smog chemistry and raises health risks. Rainfall can be ~30% higher downwind of cities.
Street Canyon Effect
Streets flanked by tall buildings reduce air circulation, concentrating pollutants at street level where people breathe. A key environmental justice issue — pedestrians and residents bear the highest exposure.
Tropospheric vs. Stratospheric Ozone
Tropospheric O₃: harmful ground-level pollutant from NOx + VOCs + sunlight. Stratospheric O₃: beneficial UV shield. Same molecule, opposite effects. Never mix these up on the exam.
Exam Focus
Explain why inversions increase pollution concentration (less mixing, not more wind). Predict when ozone will be worst (hot, sunny, calm, downwind of urban areas). Distinguish tropospheric vs. stratospheric ozone by location, cause, and impact.
03 · Pollutant Classification

Primary vs. Secondary Pollutants

Primary pollutants are emitted directly from a source. Secondary pollutants form in the atmosphere through reactions, often driven by sunlight. This distinction matters because controlling secondary pollutants means targeting their precursors, not the end product.

Pollutant Primary / Secondary Major Sources Key Impacts
Carbon monoxide (CO) Primary Incomplete combustion, vehicles, fires Blocks blood oxygen; dangerous indoors
NOx (NO, NO₂) Primary + precursor High-temp combustion: vehicles, power plants Forms ozone + nitric acid; respiratory irritation
SO₂ Primary + precursor Coal/oil combustion, metal smelting Acid deposition; respiratory harm; sulfate PM
PM2.5 / PM10 Both Combustion, road dust, wildfires; secondary from SO₂/NOx Deep lung penetration; cardiovascular disease
Tropospheric Ozone (O₃) Secondary NOx + VOCs + sunlight Lung irritation; crop damage; materials degradation
Lead (Pb) Primary Industrial processes, legacy contamination Neurotoxin; developmental harm
VOCs Primary (precursor) Fuels, solvents, paints, some plants Smog formation; some toxic/carcinogenic
PANs Secondary NOx + VOCs + sunlight (smog) Eye irritation; plant damage; inhibit photosynthesis
Exam Focus
Classify pollutants as primary or secondary and justify. Identify precursors (NOx, VOCs, SO₂) and connect them to secondary products (ozone, acids, secondary PM). Control secondary pollutants by reducing precursors — you can't just "filter" ozone.
04 · Photochemical Smog

NOx + VOCs + Sunlight → Smog

Photochemical smog is the brownish haze typical of sunny, car-dense cities. It is nitrogen-based and UV-driven, forming when NOx and VOCs react in sunlight to produce ozone, PANs, and other oxidants. Worst conditions: high emissions + strong sunlight + stagnant air.

Daily Smog Timeline

6–9 AM
Rush hour ↑ NOx & VOCs build up
9–11 AM
Traffic eases; NO₂ increases
11 AM–4 PM
Peak sunlight → O₃ and PANs peak
4 PM–sunset
Sunlight fades → ozone production stops
Why Ozone Is Harmful
Strong oxidant — aggressively damages lung tissue. Worsens asthma and bronchitis, reduces lung function, increases infection susceptibility. Also damages plant leaves, reducing crop yields.
PANs
Peroxyacyl nitrates form in photochemical smog, decompose slowly, and travel far from cities. Cause eye irritation, respiratory problems, inhibited photosynthesis, and reduced crop yields.
Downwind Ozone Peak
Photochemical reactions take time. Winds transport NOx/VOC mixtures as they "cook" in sunlight, so ozone often peaks miles downwind of the urban emission source — not directly over the city.
Controls
Reduce NOx + VOCs: catalytic converters, reformulated gasoline, vapor recovery, low-NOx burners, public transit, and walkable urban planning. Targeting precursors is the only effective strategy.
Exam Focus
Ozone peaks AFTER rush hour — not during it. Explain why: chemistry takes time + sunlight needed. Ozone is secondary — you can't scrub it, you must reduce NOx and VOCs. Smog worst when hot + sunny + calm + downwind.
05 · Industrial Smog

Gray Smog: SO₂, Soot & Particulates

Industrial smog (gray smog) is sulfur-based, historically associated with coal-burning cities. The dominant pollutants are SO₂, particulate matter (soot, ash), and CO from incomplete combustion — plus secondary sulfate aerosols.

Industrial Smog Formation Chain

Carbon in coal burns → CO₂ + CO (incomplete) + soot (PM) Sulfur in coal + O₂ → SO₂ SO₂ + O₂ → SO₃ SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) H₂SO₄ + NH₃ → (NH₄)₂SO₄ (ammonium sulfate — brown haze)
PM2.5 vs. PM10
PM10 reaches upper respiratory tract. PM2.5 penetrates deep into lungs and can enter the bloodstream. Fine PM linked to cardiovascular disease, stroke, and premature death. Size determines health risk — treat PM as a size category, not a single substance.
SO₂ Direct Effects
Irritates throat and lungs; dissolves in airway moisture forming acid. Toxic to plants — reduces crop yields. Combines with moisture at ground level to dissolve stonework. Major contributor to acid deposition.
Visibility & Haze
PM and sulfate/nitrate aerosols scatter and absorb light, reducing visibility. Haze is not just smoke — secondary aerosols from SO₂ and NOx chemistry are a major contributor.
PM Source Comparison
Natural: dust storms, wildfires, sea spray, volcanoes. Anthropogenic: fossil fuel combustion, waste incineration, vehicle exhaust, soil erosion from land clearing. Wildfires can spike PM2.5 regionally with no sunlight required (unlike smog ozone).
Exam Focus
Trace SO₂ → sulfuric acid → sulfate PM chain. Compare PM2.5 vs. PM10 penetration and health risk. Industrial vs. photochemical smog differ in precursors (sulfur/soot vs. NOx/VOC) and conditions (cold/cloudy vs. hot/sunny).
06 · Acid Deposition

From Emissions to Ecosystem Damage

Acid deposition occurs when SO₂ and NOx in the atmosphere are converted to sulfuric and nitric acids, which fall as wet deposition (acid rain, snow, fog) or dry deposition (acidic particles and gases). It links atmospheric chemistry to soil, freshwater, and forest health — often hundreds of miles from emission sources.

Main Precursors
SO₂: coal/oil combustion, metal smelting. NOx: vehicle engines, power plants, some natural sources. Natural rain is slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) from CO₂ → carbonic acid. Acid deposition adds sulfuric and nitric acid, lowering pH further.
Wet vs. Dry Deposition
Wet deposition: acids dissolved in rain, snow, or fog falling to the ground. Dry deposition: acidic gases and particles settling onto surfaces; rainstorms later wash these into waterways as acidic runoff.
Aquatic Ecosystem Impacts
Lakes and streams become acidic → fish eggs fail to hatch → food web collapses. Acid mobilizes aluminum ions from soil, which are toxic to fish gills. Sensitive ecosystems: thin-soil granite watersheds (Adirondacks, Scandinavia).
Forest & Soil Damage
Acid leaches calcium and magnesium from soils → nutrient deficiency → tree weakness → increased susceptibility to pests, disease, and drought. High-elevation forests (fir, spruce) bathed in acidic fog suffer most.
Human Infrastructure
Acid dissolves limestone, marble, and metal in buildings and monuments. Corrodes bridges and pipelines. Economic costs from material degradation are significant and ongoing.
Transboundary Problem
Emissions in one country cause acid deposition hundreds of miles away (e.g., U.S. Midwest → Canadian lakes; UK/Germany → Scandinavian forests). Requires international cooperation. Addressed by 1990 Clean Air Act amendments and cap-and-trade for SO₂.

Acid Deposition Formation Pathways

SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ → oxidized → H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) → wet/dry deposition NOx (from combustion) → atmospheric reactions → HNO₃ (nitric acid) → wet/dry deposition Both acids lower pH of rain below 5.6 → "acid rain"
Exam Focus
Distinguish wet vs. dry deposition. Explain why aquatic systems are most vulnerable (buffering capacity, aluminum mobilization). Acid deposition is a regional problem — emissions travel hundreds of miles before depositing. SO₂ scrubbers and NOx controls are the primary solutions.
07 · Stratospheric Ozone Depletion

CFCs, UV-B & the Ozone Layer

The stratospheric ozone layer absorbs harmful UV-B radiation. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) — stable in the troposphere — drift into the stratosphere where UV radiation breaks them apart, releasing chlorine atoms that destroy ozone catalytically. One Cl atom can destroy ~100,000 ozone molecules.

CFC Sources
Refrigerants (freon), aerosol propellants, foam-blowing agents, dry-cleaning solvents. Non-reactive at ground level → long atmospheric lifetime → accumulate and rise into stratosphere where UV radiation breaks the C-Cl bond.
The Antarctic Ozone Hole
Severe seasonal thinning over Antarctica each Southern Hemisphere spring. Cold polar stratospheric clouds provide surfaces for ozone-destroying reactions to occur. First observed 1985; at worst by ~2000; slowly recovering under the Montreal Protocol.
Effects of UV-B Increase
Increased skin cancer and cataracts in humans. Damage to phytoplankton (base of marine food webs). Reduced crop yields. UV-B penetrates water up to 20 m — aquatic productivity threatened.
Montreal Protocol (1987)
International treaty to phase out CFC production. Considered the most successful international environmental agreement. CFC levels in the stratosphere peaked ~1999 and are slowly declining. Ozone layer projected to recover to 1980 levels by ~2060-2070.
N₂O and Ozone Depletion
Nitrous oxide (N₂O) is now the largest ozone-depleting substance emitted by humans. Comes from microbial processes (nitrification/denitrification) in soils and agriculture. Atmospheric N₂O has risen ~15% since 1750.
Ozone vs. Climate
Ozone depletion ≠ greenhouse effect (though they interact). CFCs are also potent greenhouse gases. HFCs (CFC substitutes) are not ozone-depleting but are powerful GHGs — addressed by the Kigali Amendment (2016).
Exam Focus
CFCs are stable → long residence time → reach stratosphere. One Cl atom destroys many ozone molecules (catalytic). Montreal Protocol is the success story for international cooperation. Don't confuse ozone depletion with the greenhouse effect — different mechanisms, different molecules.
08 · Indoor Air Quality

Hidden Hazards: Radon, VOCs & More

Indoor air can be more polluted than outdoor air. People spend ~90% of time indoors. Poor ventilation concentrates pollutants from building materials, combustion, and consumer products. Indoor air quality is a major public health concern — especially in tight, energy-efficient buildings.

Radon
Radioactive gas from natural uranium decay in soil and rock. Seeps into basements through cracks. #1 cause of lung cancer among nonsmokers in the U.S. Detected by test kits; mitigated by sub-slab depressurization and ventilation.
Carbon Monoxide (CO)
Odorless, colorless gas from incomplete combustion of fuels (gas stoves, heaters, fireplaces, attached garages). Binds to hemoglobin, blocking O₂ transport. CO detectors and proper ventilation are essential preventions.
VOCs (Indoor)
Emitted from paints, carpets, furniture, cleaning products, adhesives. Benzene (carcinogen) from some building materials. "Sick building syndrome" linked to high indoor VOC concentrations and inadequate ventilation.
Asbestos
Fibrous mineral used in insulation and building materials before 1980. Inhalation of fibers → mesothelioma (lung cancer) and asbestosis. Banned in many applications; abatement (removal or encapsulation) required in older buildings.
Lead Paint
Lead-based paint in pre-1978 buildings. Peeling paint creates lead-contaminated dust. Major risk for children — neurological and developmental damage. Abatement programs and disclosure laws address the legacy problem.
Mold & Biological Pollutants
Moisture promotes mold, bacteria, and dust mites. Triggers asthma and allergic reactions. Pets and cockroach allergens contribute. Solution: moisture control, HEPA filtration, and ventilation. Environmental justice: low-income housing has higher mold rates.
Exam Focus
Radon is natural but manageable — know its source (uranium decay), pathway (soil gas), and fix (ventilation). CO is from combustion — needs a detector. VOCs need fresh air dilution. Asbestos and lead are legacy materials — abatement, not just cleanup.
09 · Noise & Light Pollution

Overlooked Pollutants

Pollution extends beyond chemicals. Noise pollution and light pollution are physical disturbances that alter ecological and human health — yet are rarely the focus of traditional pollution policy.

Noise Pollution Sources
Traffic, aircraft, construction, industry, and recreational noise. Decibels (dB) scale logarithmically: 10 dB increase = 10× sound intensity. Prolonged exposure above 85 dB causes hearing damage. Ocean noise pollution from shipping disrupts whale communication and navigation.
Wildlife Impacts of Noise
Disrupts animal communication (mating calls, alarm signals). Birds in noisy urban areas shift call frequency or timing. Marine mammals rely on sound for navigation and feeding — noise pollution is a serious threat. Alters animal behavior and reduces reproductive success.
Human Health & Noise
Chronic noise exposure raises stress hormones, blood pressure, and cardiovascular risk. Disrupts sleep, which has cascading health effects. Disproportionately affects communities near highways, airports, and industrial zones — an environmental justice issue.
Light Pollution
Excess artificial light at night (ALAN) from cities, streetlights, and advertising. Disrupts circadian rhythms in humans and wildlife. Reduces melatonin production, affecting sleep. Sea turtle hatchlings disoriented by beach lights. Migrating birds collide with lit buildings.
Exam Focus
Noise and light pollution are physical, not chemical. Both disrupt ecological systems (communication, migration, reproduction) and human health (sleep, stress, hearing). Solutions include sound barriers, dark-sky ordinances, shielded lighting, and traffic management.
10 · Policy & Control Technologies

Clean Air Act, NAAQS & Controls

The Clean Air Act (CAA), originally passed in 1970 and significantly amended in 1990, is the primary U.S. federal law regulating air quality. It established National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) for the six criteria pollutants and required states to develop implementation plans.

NAAQS
Primary standards protect human health; secondary standards protect public welfare (crops, ecosystems, visibility, materials). EPA sets the standards; states must meet them through State Implementation Plans (SIPs). Non-attainment areas face stricter controls.
Catalytic Converters
Required on cars since 1975. Convert CO → CO₂, unburned hydrocarbons → CO₂ + H₂O, and NOx → N₂ + O₂. Dramatically reduced vehicle emissions. Trade-off: CO₂ output increases (a greenhouse gas).
Scrubbers
Wet scrubbers spray alkaline solution (lime/limestone) into smokestack exhaust to absorb SO₂ → gypsum (calcium sulfate). Target SO₂, not CO₂. Required under the CAA for large coal plants. Creates solid waste (scrubber sludge) requiring disposal.
Electrostatic Precipitators
Electrically charge particulates so they stick to oppositely charged plates — then shaken off for disposal. Very effective at removing PM from power plant exhaust. Does not remove SO₂, NOx, or CO₂.
Cap-and-Trade (SO₂)
The 1990 CAA established an SO₂ cap-and-trade system. Total emissions capped; firms with low abatement costs reduce more and sell permits to firms where reductions are more expensive. Reduced U.S. SO₂ emissions ~90% from 1990 to 2020.
Vehicle Emission Standards
EPA and California (via CARB) set progressively tighter tailpipe emission standards. CAFE standards reduce fuel consumption, indirectly cutting air pollutants and CO₂. Zero-emission vehicle (ZEV) mandates push electric vehicle adoption.
Exam Focus
Match each technology to the pollutant it controls: scrubbers → SO₂; electrostatic precipitators → PM; catalytic converters → CO, NOx, hydrocarbons. Cap-and-trade is a market-based incentive — not a direct regulation. NAAQS has primary (health) and secondary (welfare) standards.

GAP FILL · High Priority · Air Pollution Trap

Thermal Inversion — Mechanism & Historical Events

A temperature inversion reverses the normal atmospheric lapse rate (temperature decreasing with altitude), trapping cold, polluted air near the surface. This creates dangerous smog events and is a must-know mechanism for the AP exam.

Normal Atmosphere
Temperature normally decreases with altitude (environmental lapse rate ~6.5°C/km). Warm air near surface is less dense → rises → mixes with upper atmosphere → pollutants disperse. This vertical mixing is the mechanism that normally dilutes urban air pollution.
Temperature Inversion
A layer of warm air forms ABOVE cooler air at the surface. The warm air acts as a lid — cool, dense air (and all its pollutants) cannot rise through the warm layer. Pollutants accumulate near the surface. Can last hours (overnight radiation cooling) or days (synoptic-scale high pressure systems).
Formation Mechanisms
Radiation inversion: clear nights → surface cools rapidly → air near ground cools below upper air → inversion forms by morning. Subsidence inversion: sinking air in high-pressure systems warms as it descends → creates persistent warm layer. Advection fog: warm air moves over cold surface. Mountains (valley inversions): cold dense air drains into valleys at night, trapping pollution.
Donora, PA (1948)
Steel mill and zinc smelter emissions + temperature inversion → five days of toxic fog → 20 deaths, 6,000 hospitalized in a town of 14,000. A turning point in US air quality awareness. Led directly to early air pollution legislation. First major documented US air pollution disaster.
London Great Smog (1952)
High-pressure system + cold weather (Londoners burned extra coal) + temperature inversion → 5 days of industrial smog → estimated 4,000 deaths immediately, 12,000 excess deaths over following months. Led to the UK Clean Air Act (1956). "Industrial smog" or "London smog" type: SO₂ + coal particles + fog in cold conditions — distinct from LA photochemical smog.
Los Angeles Geography
Mountains to the north and east + frequent subsidence inversions from Pacific high-pressure systems + sunny climate + enormous vehicle fleet = chronic photochemical smog problem. Geographic "bowl" traps air. LA smog is photochemical type (NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight → ozone), not London type. Morning rush-hour emissions → afternoon ozone peak.

Acid Deposition: Wet vs. Dry

Acid deposition is broader than just "acid rain" — the exam distinguishes two forms and expects you to understand chemistry, ecological impacts, and policy responses.

Wet Acid Deposition
SO₂ and NOₓ emissions → react with water vapor in atmosphere → form H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) and HNO₃ (nitric acid) → fall as rain, snow, sleet, fog with pH typically 4.2–4.4. Normal rain pH ≈ 5.6 (slightly acidic from natural CO₂ dissolution). Acid rain is anything below 5.6, but most damage below pH 5.0.
Dry Acid Deposition
SO₂ and NOₓ gases and particles settle directly onto surfaces without precipitation. When these dry deposits contact moisture (dew, fog), they form acids on surfaces. Damages buildings, statues (limestone is especially vulnerable — H₂SO₄ dissolves CaCO₃). Can be significant in dry climates or areas downwind of industrial sources.
Lake Acidification
Acid rain lowers lake pH → aluminum leaches from soil into water → toxic to fish gills (prevents oxygen exchange). Many Adirondack and Scandinavian lakes became fishless in the 1970s–80s. Buffering capacity varies: granite bedrock lakes acidify easily; limestone bedrock lakes are buffered by carbonate. Measures: pH monitoring, lime addition (emergency treatment).
Forest Damage
Acid rain leaches calcium, magnesium, and potassium from soil → nutrient deficiency in trees → weakened, stunted growth. High-elevation forests most affected (bathed in acid fog). Mobilizes aluminum → toxic to fine roots. Acid rain damage to Appalachian and European forests was severe in the 1970s–80s before SO₂ reduction.
Policy Success: 1990 CAA Amendments
Established SO₂ cap-and-trade system (Acid Rain Program) — set declining total cap, issued tradeable permits. SO₂ emissions from power plants fell ~90% from 1990 to 2015. Acid rain in northeastern US measurably declined. This is the classic example of cap-and-trade successfully solving an environmental problem.
GAP FILL · Medium Priority · Smog Precursors & Policy

VOCs, Radon & Cap-and-Trade vs. Carbon Tax

Three additional air quality topics that appear regularly but are often undertreated in study guides.

VOCs — Volatile Organic Compounds
Carbon-containing compounds that evaporate easily at room temperature. Precursors to ground-level ozone (smog): NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight → O₃. Sources: vehicle exhaust and crankcase emissions, industrial solvents (dry cleaning, paint), petroleum vapors, adhesives, and natural vegetation (isoprene from trees). BTEX compounds (benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, xylene) are toxic VOCs from petroleum.
Radon — Indoor Air Quality
Naturally occurring radioactive gas from uranium decay in soil/rock (particularly granite, shale, phosphate rock). Seeps through foundation cracks into buildings. Colorless, odorless, tasteless. Decays into radioactive particles that lodge in lung tissue → lung cancer. Second leading cause of lung cancer in US (~21,000 deaths/year) after smoking. Testing: EPA-approved radon kits. Mitigation: sub-slab depressurization (pipe + fan removes radon before it enters).
Cap-and-Trade
Government sets a total cap (maximum permitted emissions). Issues permits (allowances) equal to the cap. Companies may buy and sell permits — companies that reduce emissions cheaply can sell excess permits to companies for whom reduction is expensive. Market finds cheapest overall reductions. Proven success: SO₂ Acid Rain Program reduced SO₂ at lower cost than predicted. Current use: California's cap-and-trade for CO₂.
Carbon Tax
Direct price placed on each unit of CO₂ (or carbon equivalent) emitted. Provides certainty about price but uncertainty about total emission reduction. Revenue can be returned to citizens (carbon dividend) or fund clean energy. Simpler to administer than cap-and-trade. Critics: industry moves to unregulated countries ("carbon leakage"). In use: British Columbia, parts of Europe, some US states.
London vs. LA Smog
London/Industrial smog: SO₂ + coal dust particles + fog → sulfurous, grey smog in cold, damp conditions. Peaks in morning, winter. Photochemical/LA smog: NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight → O₃ + PAN (peroxyacetyl nitrate) → brownish haze. Peaks in afternoon on hot, sunny days. Completely different chemistry, different conditions, different primary controls (scrubbers for London type; catalytic converters for LA type).

Common Mistakes & Traps

High-yield patterns · Things the AP loves to test

Trap #1

Ozone Confusion

Ground-level ozone = harmful smog pollutant. Stratospheric ozone = helpful UV shield. Same molecule, completely different consequences. Know the layer, know the impact. Never say "the ozone layer is coming down."
Trap #2

Primary vs Secondary

Ozone and PANs are secondary — you can't scrub them. To control ozone, reduce NOx and VOCs. Thinking you can install a filter to remove ozone from city air is a classic wrong answer.
Trap #3

Inversions Reduce Mixing

Inversions are NOT more wind — they suppress vertical mixing. A warm air cap traps cooler, polluted surface air. Smog builds for days during inversions, not because emissions increase.
Trap #4

Ozone Peaks After Rush Hour

Traffic provides the ingredients, sunlight does the cooking. Ozone peaks in the afternoon — sometimes downwind of the city. Rush hour peaks ≠ ozone peaks. Always link smog timing to photochemistry.
Trap #5

Scrubbers ≠ CO₂ Control

Scrubbers remove SO₂. Electrostatic precipitators remove PM. Catalytic converters reduce CO, NOx, hydrocarbons. None of these control CO₂ — that requires switching fuel or carbon capture.
Trap #6

Acid Deposition Is Regional

Acid deposition falls hundreds of miles from the emission source. A country or state burning coal can damage forests and lakes across a border. Always describe as a transboundary issue requiring multi-jurisdictional solutions.
Trap #7

CFC ≠ Greenhouse Effect

Ozone depletion and the greenhouse effect are different problems with different mechanisms. CFCs do both, but ozone depletion works via UV chemistry in the stratosphere; greenhouse warming works via IR absorption in the troposphere.
Trap #8

Radon Is Natural but Harmful

Radon comes from natural uranium decay — but that doesn't make it safe. It's the #1 cause of lung cancer among non-smokers. Natural source, serious indoor hazard. The fix is ventilation, not eliminating the mineral.
