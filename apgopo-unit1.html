Core Frame
What Unit 1 Is Really About

The Constitution is best understood as a response to two simultaneous problems: the new nation needed a government strong enough to govern, but the Framers also feared concentrated power and designed the system to slow it down.

That tension runs through everything in Unit 1: natural rights, limited government, separation of powers, checks and balances, and federalism.

Major Buckets
01 · Founding Ideas

Enlightenment Ideas and Models of Democracy

Unit 1 begins with a tension: the Founders wanted a government strong enough to solve national problems, but limited enough to prevent tyranny. Enlightenment ideas helped them imagine government as something people create for a purpose, not something rulers own by divine right.

John Locke is the biggest influence for natural rights, social contract, consent of the governed, and revolution against abusive government. Montesquieu is the key thinker for separation of powers. Hobbes helps explain why people accept government for order, while Rousseau, Voltaire, and Diderot connect to public good, expression, religious liberty, and reason.

Natural Rights
Rights exist because people are human; government protects them but does not create them. The Declaration uses this logic to justify independence.
Social Contract
People consent to government for order and rights protection. If government breaks the contract, people may alter or abolish it.
Democracy Models
Participatory democracy emphasizes broad engagement, pluralist democracy emphasizes group competition, and elite democracy argues power is concentrated among wealthy or educated elites.
Republicanism
The U.S. is primarily representative. Citizens choose leaders, and those leaders filter public views into policy.

AP Move

When a prompt names a Founding idea, connect it to a structure: Locke to the Declaration, Montesquieu to separated branches, and republicanism to elected representation.
02 · First Government and Constitution Making

Articles of Confederation and the Constitutional Convention

The Articles of Confederation created the first national government, but they reflected deep fear of central power. Congress was unicameral, each state had one vote, there was no president, no national court system, no power to tax directly, weak commerce power, and amendments required unanimous state approval.

The Articles were not useless. They helped the nation survive the Revolution, handled western lands through the Northwest Ordinance, and kept states loosely joined. But they failed at collective action: debt, trade conflict, military weakness, and inability to enforce national decisions.

Shays' Rebellion
The uprising exposed the inability of the national government to maintain order and convinced many leaders that a stronger central government was necessary.
Virginia vs. New Jersey
The Virginia Plan favored population-based representation and large states. The New Jersey Plan favored equal state representation and small states.
Great Compromise
Created a bicameral Congress: House representation by population and Senate representation equal by state.
Three-Fifths Compromise
Counted enslaved people as three-fifths for representation and taxation, increasing Southern political power and embedding slavery into constitutional bargaining.
Electoral College
Balanced distrust of direct democracy, state influence, and executive selection by using electors allocated through congressional representation.
03 · Ratification Debate

Federalists, Anti-Federalists, and the Bill of Rights

Ratification forced Americans to choose between a stronger national government and fear that such a government would endanger liberty. Federalists supported the Constitution because the Articles were too weak and because structure could restrain power. Anti-Federalists feared distant centralized authority, a strong executive, federal courts, a standing army, and the absence of explicit rights.

Federalist 10
Madison argues factions are inevitable. A large republic controls faction effects by multiplying interests and making majority domination harder.
Federalist 51
Madison explains checks and balances: because people are not angels, ambition must counteract ambition.
Brutus No. 1
Warns that a large republic will be too distant, too powerful, and too likely to swallow state authority and individual liberty.
Federalist 70 and 78
Hamilton defends a single energetic executive in 70 and an independent judiciary with judicial review logic in 78.
Bill of Rights
The promise of explicit rights helped answer Anti-Federalist concerns and made ratification politically possible.

AP Move

Do not just name a paper. State the principle and apply it to the constitutional design in the prompt.
04 · Constitutional Structure

Principles Embedded in the Constitution

The Constitution is short, but it creates a durable system through broad principles. Articles I, II, and III create Congress, the presidency, and the federal courts. The system is designed to be powerful enough to govern while forcing institutions to share authority.

Popular Sovereignty
Authority comes from the people, expressed through elections, representation, and the Preamble's "We the People."
Limited Government
Officials are bound by constitutional rules and enumerated powers. Limited government means restricted authority, not automatically small government.
Separation of Powers
Congress makes laws, the president enforces them, and courts interpret them. Different branches have distinct jobs.
Checks and Balances
Branches share tools: vetoes, confirmations, treaties, impeachment, judicial review, and overrides. Shared power prevents domination.
Amendment Process
Change is possible but difficult: usually two-thirds of Congress and three-fourths of states. This balances stability and adaptability.

Growth of Power

Executive orders, executive agreements, judicial review, the Necessary and Proper Clause, and the Supremacy Clause all show how constitutional power grows through practice, interpretation, and institutional conflict.
05 · Federalism

Dividing Power Between Nation and States

Federalism divides authority between national and state governments. It is a compromise between a unitary system and a confederation. National government handles powers like defense, currency, interstate commerce, naturalization, and treaties. States control areas like elections, local governments, public health, education, and policing.

Delegated Powers
Powers listed for the national government, especially in Article I, Section 8.
Reserved Powers
Powers kept by states under the Tenth Amendment. They are defined by what is not delegated to the national government.
Concurrent Powers
Shared powers, including taxation, borrowing, courts, roads, and eminent domain.
Fiscal Federalism
Categorical grants are narrow with rules; block grants are broader; mandates require states to act, sometimes without funding.

Federalism Over Time

Dual federalism imagines separate national and state spheres. Cooperative federalism features shared policy work. Federalism disputes often turn on the Necessary and Proper Clause, Commerce Clause, Supremacy Clause, and Tenth Amendment.

Foundations

Enlightenment and Founding Ideas

Natural rights · social contract · consent of the governed

Core Claim

Reason Over Tradition

The Enlightenment emphasized reason as the best tool for organizing society. That pushed political thinkers to question monarchy, inherited authority, and unaccountable power.

For AP Gov, this matters because the Founders borrowed the idea that legitimate government must be justified, limited, and structured to protect liberty.

Thinkers

Who Matches Which Idea

  • Hobbes: order, strong ruler, social contract for stability.
  • Locke: natural rights, consent, right to revolution.
  • Montesquieu: separation of powers.
  • Rousseau: public good over narrow self-interest.
  • Voltaire and Diderot: freedom of thought and criticism of concentrated authority.
Declaration Logic

Natural Rights and Consent

The Declaration of Independence applies Enlightenment ideas as a political argument. Government exists to protect rights, derives authority from the consent of the governed, and may be altered or abolished if it violates that purpose.

That same logic explains why American constitutional design emphasizes both effective government and structural limits.

Models of Democracy

Direct, Representative, Participatory, Pluralist, Elite

Know the definition and apply it to a scenario

Direct
Citizens Vote on Policy

Direct democracy means citizens vote directly on laws and policies. In the United States, this appears mostly through state and local initiatives and referendums, not national policymaking.

Representative
The Republic Model

Representative democracy means citizens choose leaders to make policy decisions. The Framers preferred this model because they feared instability, factions, and mob rule.

Participatory
Broad Public Engagement

Participatory democracy argues that broad involvement by ordinary citizens creates a more responsive system.

Pluralist
Competition Among Groups

Pluralist democracy sees policy as the result of bargaining among many groups. It does not mean every group has equal power.

Elite
Power Concentrates at the Top

Elite democracy argues that the wealthy and educated tend to dominate political outcomes and that mass participation is often limited in practice.

First Government

The Articles of Confederation

Weak national structure · strong state sovereignty · Shays' Rebellion

Weaknesses
Why the Articles Failed
  • No national executive to enforce laws.
  • No national judiciary to interpret laws.
  • Congress could not tax directly.
  • Congress had weak power over interstate commerce.
  • Major action required supermajorities; amendments required unanimity.
Accomplishments
What Still Worked

The Articles were not useless. They kept the new nation functioning after independence, helped establish a federal relationship between states and the national government, and shaped western expansion through the Northwest Ordinance.

Turning Point
Shays' Rebellion

Shays' Rebellion showed many leaders that the national government lacked the power to maintain order. That crisis helped build support for a stronger central government at the Constitutional Convention.

Philadelphia 1787

The Convention and the Big Compromises

Representation · slavery · executive power · Electoral College

Representation

Virginia vs. New Jersey

The Virginia Plan favored large states with representation by population. The New Jersey Plan favored small states with equal state voting.

The Great Compromise combined both: a population-based House and an equal-representation Senate.

Slavery and Power

Three-Fifths Compromise

The Three-Fifths Compromise counted enslaved people as three-fifths of a person for representation and taxation. It was a political compromise over power, not a moral settlement.

Institutional Fixes

Executive, Judiciary, Electoral College

The Convention created a single president to enforce laws, a national judiciary to resolve disputes, and an Electoral College rather than direct national popular election.

The Ratification Fight

Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

Factions · checks and balances · strong executive · Bill of Rights

Federalists

Case for Ratification

Federalists argued the Articles were too weak and that internal structure would prevent tyranny even under a stronger national government.

Federalist 10 explains how a large republic controls faction. Federalist 51 explains how ambition checks ambition through separated institutions.

Anti-Federalists

Case Against Ratification

Anti-Federalists feared the Constitution would overpower states, endanger liberty, and create a powerful executive and judiciary. Brutus No. 1 warns that a large republic could become unresponsive and dangerous.

Ratification Solution

Bill of Rights

The promise of a Bill of Rights was critical to winning ratification. Anti-Federalists wanted explicit protections because they did not trust structural limits alone.

Instrument of Government

Constitutional Structure and Core Principles

Articles I-III · implied powers · judicial review · supremacy

Purpose
Why the Constitution Was Created

The Constitution was written to fix the weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation. The goal was to build a government strong enough to govern but limited enough to protect liberty.

The Constitution does not rely on trust. It relies on structured conflict between branches and between levels of government.

Branches
Articles I, II, and III

Article I creates Congress, Article II creates the presidency, and Article III creates the judiciary. Their powers are distinct by design.

Growth
Implied Powers

The necessary and proper clause allows Congress to make laws needed to carry out enumerated powers, which explains how the national government created institutions not explicitly named in the Constitution.

Judiciary
Marbury and Review

Marbury v. Madison established judicial review, giving the Supreme Court the power to strike down laws that conflict with the Constitution.

Separation of Powers

  • Distinct roles for each branch.
  • Legislative makes law, executive enforces, judicial interprets.
  • Prevents one institution from holding every function.

Checks and Balances

  • President vetoes bills.
  • Senate confirms appointments and ratifies treaties.
  • Courts review laws for constitutionality.

Major Constitutional Powers

  • Congress makes laws, collects taxes, regulates trade, and declares war.
  • The president enforces laws, commands the military, and handles foreign policy.
  • The judiciary interprets laws and reviews constitutionality.

Bill of Rights Snapshot

  • Speech, religion, press, assembly, and petition.
  • Search and seizure protections, fair trial rights, due process.
  • Protection from cruel and unusual punishment.
  • Unlisted rights can still belong to the people.
Divided Sovereignty

Federalism Basics

Enumerated powers · reserved powers · concurrent powers · grants

Simple Definition

Federalism in Plain English

Federalism is the division of power between the national government and state governments. Its purpose is to keep too much authority from concentrating in one place.

Most major political conflict in the United States can be framed as a struggle between national unity and state independence.

Power Types

Enumerated, Reserved, Concurrent

Enumerated powers belong to the national government. Reserved powers remain with the states under the 10th Amendment. Concurrent powers are shared.

  • Enumerated: declaring war, coining money, regulating interstate commerce.
  • Reserved: education, local policing, elections.
  • Concurrent: taxation, road building, law enforcement.
Constitutional Hooks

Supremacy, Commerce, Necessary and Proper

The supremacy clause makes valid federal law supreme over conflicting state law. The commerce clause and implied powers often justify national action.

The necessary and proper clause is the foundation of implied powers, which is why it appears repeatedly in federalism disputes.

Policy Tools

Grants and Mandates

The federal government often influences states through categorical grants, block grants, and mandates. Funding can shift power even without changing the Constitution.

Court Landmarks

Federalism Cases You Must Know

Clause, holding, and whether national or state power grew

Judicial Review

Marbury v. Madison (1803)

This case established judicial review, meaning the Supreme Court can declare laws unconstitutional.

Constitutional principle: separation of powers. Impact: strengthened the national judiciary. Big idea: courts are the final interpreters of the Constitution.

Implied Powers

McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)

The Court ruled that Congress could create a national bank under the necessary and proper clause, and states could not tax federal institutions.

Constitutional principle: implied powers plus the supremacy clause. Impact: expanded national government power. Big idea: federal law overrides conflicting state law.

Commerce

Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)

This case expanded the commerce clause, giving Congress broad power to regulate interstate trade.

Constitutional principle: commerce clause. Impact: strengthened federal economic power. Big idea: interstate commerce is controlled by the federal government.

Limits

United States v. Lopez (1995)

The Court limited Congress's power under the commerce clause by ruling that gun possession in schools was not interstate commerce in that case.

Constitutional principle: limits on federal power. Impact: strengthened state authority. Big idea: not everything can be regulated by Congress.

AP Writing

How Unit 1 Appears in FRQs

Use evidence to support reasoning, not to decorate an answer

Use This Pattern

Evidence + Reasoning

Claim: The Constitution limits tyranny by dividing power.

Evidence: Federalist 51 argues that ambition must counteract ambition.

Reasoning: Because each branch has institutional tools to resist the others, no branch can dominate easily.

Typical Prompt Move

Explain the Tradeoff

High-scoring answers explain how the same feature can solve one problem while creating another. For example, checks and balances protect liberty, but they also slow policymaking.

What To Avoid

Term Dumping

Do not list ideas without linking them. The AP prompt usually wants a causal chain, a comparison, or an applied explanation.
