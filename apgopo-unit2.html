Institutions, Power, and Interbranch Conflict

Core Strategy
How the Constitution Prevents Domination

The constitutional system divides governing authority so that no single person or institution can control the whole state. Separation of powers assigns different jobs to the branches, while checks and balances lets each branch limit the others.

That design makes conflict normal. Major change usually requires bargaining across institutions, not just a single election result or one powerful leader.

Major Buckets
01 · Separation of Powers

Powers, Checks, and Institutional Friction

Unit 2 is about institutions that share power because the Constitution tries to prevent domination. Separation of powers gives Congress, the president, and courts different jobs. Checks and balances lets each branch limit, supervise, or reshape what the others do.

Conflict is not a glitch. The design expects disagreement over lawmaking, appointments, funding, war powers, executive privilege, judicial review, and implementation.

Power Types
Enumerated powers are written in the Constitution, implied powers are inferred through necessary and proper logic, and inherent powers are often claimed by presidents in foreign affairs or national security.
Congress Checks
Funding, oversight, subpoenas, confirmations, treaty ratification, impeachment, statutes, and jurisdiction rules.
President Checks
Vetoes, agenda-setting, bargaining, executive orders, signing statements, and implementation choices.
Courts Check
Judicial review lets courts invalidate unconstitutional laws or executive actions through actual cases.

AP Move

Name the specific power, explain the mechanism, then show how another branch responds.
02 · Congress

Representation, Committees, and Lawmaking

Congress writes laws, represents citizens, controls funding, confirms major officials through the Senate, and oversees executive implementation. Many Unit 2 scenarios begin in Congress because lawmaking and appropriations shape what the executive branch can actually do.

House vs. Senate
The House is population-based, has two-year terms, starts revenue bills, and impeaches. The Senate has equal state representation, six-year terms, confirmations, treaty ratification, and impeachment trials.
Districting
The census leads to reapportionment and redistricting. Gerrymandering uses packing and cracking, while Baker and Shaw connect districting to Equal Protection limits.
Leadership
The Speaker, majority/minority leaders, whips, committee chairs, and ranking members shape what gets attention and what reaches the floor.
Committees
Standing committees handle most work. Hearings, markup, and pigeonholing decide whether bills advance or disappear.
House Rules
The Rules Committee controls floor debate and amendments. Open rules allow amendments; closed rules block them.
Senate Delay
Filibuster and cloture let Senate minorities slow or block action. Riders can be attached more freely than in the House.

Bill to Law

Bills are introduced by members, referred to committees, shaped through hearings and markup, debated under chamber rules, reconciled through conference committees if needed, and sent to the president for signature, veto, or possible pocket veto.
03 · Presidency

Formal Powers, Informal Powers, and Limits

The presidency gives energy and unity to law execution, but the president is checked by Congress and courts. Formal powers come from Article II; informal power comes from visibility, party leadership, bargaining, and control of the executive branch.

Formal Powers
Veto, Commander in Chief, appointments, treaties, pardons, State of the Union, and calling Congress into special session.
Informal Tools
Bully pulpit, going public, agenda-setting, party leadership, bargaining, and electoral pressure.
Executive Orders
Direct executive operations but must rest on constitutional or statutory authority and can be reversed or challenged.
Executive Privilege
A limited confidentiality claim. U.S. v. Nixon shows it is not absolute in a criminal investigation.
Foreign Policy
Treaties require Senate ratification; executive agreements do not. War powers are divided between Congress and the president.
EOP and Cabinet
OMB, NSC, chief of staff, policy councils, and cabinet departments help presidents manage administration.
04 · Federal Bureaucracy

Implementation, Discretion, and Accountability

The bureaucracy turns laws into real policy. Congress often writes broad goals, then agencies define rules, enforce standards, administer programs, and apply expertise. This creates democratic tension because unelected officials make choices that affect policy outcomes.

Discretion
Agencies make choices inside statutory boundaries because Congress cannot legislate every technical detail.
Rulemaking
Agencies propose rules, gather comments, issue final regulations, and enforce them. Regulated groups may challenge rules in court.
Agency Types
Cabinet departments, independent agencies, independent regulatory commissions, government corporations, and the Senior Executive Service.
Control Tools
Presidents use appointments, directives, and OMB. Congress uses funding, statutes, confirmations, and oversight. Courts review legality.
Iron Triangles
Stable relationships among agencies, congressional committees, and interest groups. Issue networks are broader and less stable.
Civil Service
Pendleton created merit hiring; Hatch limits partisan activity to protect neutrality.
05 · Judiciary

Federal Courts, Judicial Review, and Appointments

Federal courts interpret laws and the Constitution through cases. The judiciary is powerful because of judicial review, but it depends on cases, standing, implementation by other actors, appointments, and institutional legitimacy.

Structure
District courts are trial courts, courts of appeals review lower decisions, and the Supreme Court has limited original jurisdiction plus broad appellate influence.
Access
Cases must be justiciable, litigants need standing, lower options are usually exhausted, and certiorari follows the rule of four.
Opinions
Majority opinions control, concurrences agree with the result but not reasoning, dissents explain disagreement, and unanimous opinions carry special force.
Appointments
Presidents nominate; the Senate confirms. Ideology, party control, interest groups, ABA ratings, and senatorial courtesy can matter.
Philosophy
Judicial restraint favors deference; judicial activism is more willing to overturn laws or precedents.
06 · Conflict, Cooperation, and Statutes

Institutions in Practice

Unit 2 ultimately tests interaction. A law might begin with congressional bargaining, face a veto threat, become law through signature or override, be implemented by agencies, be overseen through hearings, and then be challenged in court.

Youngstown
Presidential power is weakest when acting against Congress's expressed will.
Impeachment
House impeaches by majority; Senate removes by two-thirds. Impeachment is political and constitutional.
Unified vs. Divided
Unified government may make lawmaking easier; divided government increases veto threats, investigations, gridlock, and unilateral action.
Key Laws
War Powers Resolution, Budget and Impoundment Control Act, FOIA, Pendleton, Hatch, ADA, VRA, NCLB, Patriot Act, and campaign finance laws show Congress shaping implementation.

AP Paragraph Model

Claim: identify a power. Mechanism: explain how it works. Interaction: explain how another branch can check, influence, or respond.

Foundational Powers

Separation of Powers and Checks

Enumerated · implied · inherent · vetoes · oversight · judicial review

Power Sources

Enumerated, Implied, Inherent

Enumerated powers are written directly into the Constitution. Implied powers are inferred from listed powers, usually through the Necessary and Proper Clause. Inherent powers are claimed as powers of a sovereign nation and often appear in foreign policy or national security.

Checks in Practice

Where Branches Touch

  • Congress uses appropriations, hearings, confirmations, treaties, and impeachment.
  • The president uses the veto, bargaining, agenda-setting, and sometimes signing statements.
  • Courts use judicial review through actual cases, not direct commands.
Misconception Check

Co-Equal Does Not Mean Identical

The branches are co-equal in legitimacy, but not interchangeable in powers. Presidents cannot rewrite statutes, Congress cannot order a court to decide a case a certain way, and the Supreme Court rules on constitutionality rather than “overruling” Congress as if it were a legislature.

Congress Basics

Representation, Elections, and Districting

Bicameralism · census · reapportionment · gerrymandering

Two Chambers

House vs. Senate

Bicameralism creates a House built for responsiveness and a Senate built for stability. The House starts revenue bills and impeaches; the Senate confirms appointments, ratifies treaties, and conducts impeachment trials.

Districting

Census, Reapportionment, Redistricting

The census determines how House seats are divided among states. After apportionment, states redraw districts, which opens the door to gerrymandering through packing and cracking.

Representation Styles

Delegate vs. Trustee

Under the delegate model, members follow district preferences closely. Under the trustee model, members use personal judgment even when constituents disagree.

Congress Internals

Leadership, Committees, and Oversight

Speaker · majority leader · whips · standing committees · subpoenas

House

Structured Chamber

The Speaker of the House controls agenda flow and committee influence inside the majority party. Majority and minority leaders shape strategy, while whips count votes and pressure members.

Senate

Individual Leverage

The Senate majority leader is the key agenda broker, but the chamber’s looser rules give individual senators more room to delay or reshape legislation.

Committees

Gatekeepers of Congress

Most bills live or die in committees. Hearings, expert testimony, and markup sessions determine whether legislation is rewritten, advanced, or pigeonholed.

Oversight

Continuous, Not Rare

Congress supervises the executive through hearings, investigations, subpoenas, budgets, and statutory revisions. Oversight is one of its strongest ongoing checks.

Lawmaking

The Legislative Process as a System of Veto Points

Rules Committee · filibuster · cloture · conference committee · pocket veto

Committees First

Where Bills Usually Change or Die

Bills are introduced by members, referred to committees, and usually shaped there before the public sees them. Many never escape committee unless pressure, leadership, or in the House a discharge petition changes that.

House vs. Senate Rules

Tight House, Loose Senate

The House Rules Committee tightly manages floor debate and amendments. The Senate allows extended debate, making the filibuster and cloture central to minority leverage.

Presidential Stage

Sign, Veto, or Pocket Veto

After bicameral passage, the president may sign or veto. If the president does nothing for ten days and Congress adjourns, the result is a pocket veto, which cannot be overridden.

Executive Power

The Presidency: Formal Powers, Informal Powers, and Limits

Commander in Chief · executive orders · executive privilege · unified vs divided government

Article II

Formal Powers

  • Veto legislation.
  • Serve as Commander in Chief.
  • Negotiate treaties and appoint judges, ambassadors, and executive officers, often with Senate confirmation.
  • Issue pardons and reprieves for federal offenses.
Political Tools

Informal Powers

Modern presidents rely on agenda-setting, bargaining, party leadership, and the bully pulpit. Under unified government, bargaining may be easier; under divided government, veto threats and unilateral tools become more important.

Unilateral Action

Executive Orders and Privilege

Executive orders direct executive operations but must rest on constitutional or statutory authority. Executive privilege exists, but U.S. v. Nixon shows it is not absolute.

Implementation State

The Federal Bureaucracy

Discretion · rulemaking · OMB · independent agencies · iron triangles

Core Job

Implementation and Regulation

Bureaucracies translate statutes into real policy through implementation, regulation, and expertise. Agencies matter because Congress cannot legislate every technical detail.

Agency Power

Discretion

Discretionary authority means agencies make choices inside legal boundaries. The same law can produce different outcomes depending on enforcement priorities and rule design.

Control

Presidents, Congress, Courts

Presidents influence agencies through appointments, directives, and the OMB. Congress uses funding and oversight. Courts review whether agency action stays within statutory and constitutional limits.

Policy Networks

Iron Triangles and Issue Networks

An iron triangle links an agency, a congressional committee, and an interest group in a mutually beneficial relationship. Issue networks are broader and more fluid.

Judicial Branch

Structure, Judicial Review, and Legal Process

Standing · certiorari · rule of four · majority opinions · criminal vs civil

Access to the Court

Justiciability and Standing

The Supreme Court only hears justiciable disputes. Litigants need standing, lower court paths are usually exhausted, and the Court normally takes a case by granting certiorari under the rule of four.

Review Power

Marbury and Judicial Review

Marbury v. Madison established judicial review, making the Court the final interpreter of constitutionality in cases that come before it.

Types of Cases

Criminal vs. Civil

Criminal law requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt and may end in punishment. Civil law usually uses a preponderance standard and often ends with damages or equitable relief.

Institutional Interaction

Interbranch Conflict and Cooperation

Appointments · treaties · Youngstown · impeachment · bargaining

Shared Power

Appointments and Confirmations

The president nominates many judges and executive officials, but the Senate confirms. This forces strategic nominations and creates leverage for senators.

Foreign Policy

Treaties vs. Executive Agreements

Treaties require Senate ratification by two-thirds. Executive agreements bypass that step, making them easier to conclude but often more vulnerable politically.

Presidential Limits

Youngstown and Nixon

Youngstown Sheet & Tube v. Sawyer shows presidents are weakest when acting against Congress. U.S. v. Nixon shows executive privilege is limited in a judicial proceeding.

Removal Power

Impeachment

The House impeaches by majority vote; the Senate removes by a two-thirds vote. Impeachment is a constitutional remedy, but it also unfolds as a political process.

Key Statutes

Institution-Building and Governance Laws

Pendleton · Hatch · War Powers · Budget Act · FOIA · NCLB

Civil Service

Pendleton and Hatch

The Pendleton Act moved federal hiring toward merit and away from the spoils system. The Hatch Act limited partisan activity by federal employees to preserve bureaucratic neutrality.

Congressional Control

War Powers and Budgeting

The War Powers Resolution tried to limit unilateral presidential military action. The Budget and Impoundment Control Act strengthened Congress against presidents refusing to spend appropriated funds.

Transparency and Implementation

FOIA, Voting Rights, NCLB

FOIA expanded access to government records. The Voting Rights Act and No Child Left Behind show how statutes can increase federal implementation burdens and oversight over states and agencies.

FRQ Skills

Building High-Scoring Branch Interaction Answers

Claim · mechanism · interaction · evidence

Paragraph Model

Power → Mechanism → Check

Start with the branch power, explain how it operates, then show how another branch can limit or influence it. That structure keeps the answer causal instead of definition-heavy.

Case Use

Do More Than Name-Drop

Name the case, state the principle, and apply it to the scenario. Example: U.S. v. Nixon means executive privilege exists but can be overcome in a criminal proceeding.

Common Error

Do Not Confuse Strategy with Power

“Going public” is an informal strategy, not a constitutional power. A president can pressure Congress politically, but cannot legislate unilaterally.
