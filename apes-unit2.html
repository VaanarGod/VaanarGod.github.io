Core Frame
What Unit 2 Is Really About

Unit 2 zooms in on biodiversity — what it is, why it matters, how it's measured, what threatens it, and how ecosystems respond to disturbance. The thread connecting every section is that biodiversity underpins the ecosystem services humans depend on for free.

The big organizing idea: biodiversity at the genetic, species, and ecosystem levels all contribute to resilience. Lose diversity, lose stability. Every FRQ mechanism you write should trace a cause-effect chain from biodiversity loss to a concrete ecological or human consequence.

Exam Strategy
FRQs here love mechanism questions: don't just name a threat — explain the chain. "Habitat fragmentation → increases edge effects → specialist species decline → food-web simplification → reduced ecosystem services." Never stop at naming; always explain how.

  • Distinguish genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity — don't mix up levels.

  • Define keystone species and explain the trophic cascade mechanism.
  • Apply island biogeography to fragmented habitats (not just literal islands).
  • Distinguish primary vs. secondary succession using soil presence.
  • Explain why invasive species succeed using enemy release and generalist traits.
  • Describe how wildfires, floods, and volcanic eruptions can both harm and benefit ecosystems.
  • Distinguish resistance vs. resilience in disturbance-recovery scenarios.

Common Mistakes

Avoid These

  • Treating biodiversity as only species richness — it has three levels, and evenness matters too.
  • Calling nutrient cycling a regulating service — it's a supporting service. Water purification is regulating.
  • Assuming keystone species are always top predators — they can be ecosystem engineers or critical mutualists.
  • Defining deserts by heat — deserts are defined by low precipitation, not temperature.
  • Saying tropical rainforest soil is nutrient-rich — most nutrients are stored in biomass, not soil.
  • Calling any introduced species "invasive" — invasive requires non-native + rapid spread + harm.
  • Calling biological control always safe — biocontrol agents can harm non-target species.
  • Confusing resilience (recovery speed) with resistance (magnitude of change).
Study Order

Best Way to Review

  • Start with ecosystem services: learn all four categories with at least two examples each.
  • Master biodiversity levels: genetic (MVP, bottleneck), species (richness vs. evenness), ecosystem.
  • Study keystone and indicator species: know specific examples and the mechanism of impact.
  • Cover island biogeography and habitat fragmentation — apply it to forest patches, not just islands.
  • Learn terrestrial biomes and aquatic systems from climate graphs — temperature + precipitation.
  • Study ecological succession — primary vs. secondary, pioneer species, r vs. K strategists.
  • Finish with invasive species and disturbance — mechanisms, management trade-offs, and fire ecology.

01 · Ecosystem Services & Why Biodiversity Matters

What Ecosystems Do for Free

Ecosystem services are the benefits people obtain from ecosystems. Healthy ecosystems do work "for free" — when they are damaged, society often pays to replace those services with technology, which is usually more expensive and less effective.

Four Categories of Ecosystem Services

Provisioning Services
Tangible goods: food (fish, crops), fresh water, timber, medicinal resources, fiber (wool), meat, milk. These are the most visible and easily valued services.
Regulating Services
Natural processes that regulate conditions: water purification, flood control, climate regulation (carbon storage), erosion control, disease regulation, natural pest control — predators keep pest populations in balance, reducing pesticide need.
Supporting Services
Underlying processes that make other services possible: nutrient cycling, soil formation, primary production (photosynthesis). If these collapse, provisioning and regulating services collapse too. A common trap: nutrient cycling is supporting, not regulating.
Cultural Services
Nonmaterial benefits: recreation, aesthetic value, spiritual or educational value. Sustainable fisheries, recreational fishing, ecotourism. These are real economic values even if harder to quantify.

Biodiversity → Ecosystem Stability

Biodiversity supports ecosystem services because different organisms play different ecological roles. Systems with greater functional variety tend to maintain productivity under changing conditions, resist disturbances (drought, pests, storms), and recover faster afterward. Think of biodiversity as a diversified team: if roles are shared across many species, losing one doesn't collapse the whole system.

Pollination example: Many flowering plants depend on animal pollinators (insects, birds, bats). Pollinator diversity stabilizes pollination — if one pollinator declines due to disease or pesticide exposure, others can fill the gap. Assuming "it's just bees" is a common mistake.

Wetlands act as natural water filters: water slows, sediments settle, plants and microbes absorb nutrients and pollutants. Remove wetlands → need engineered water treatment + greater flood vulnerability.

Trade-off chain (FRQ structure): Clearing a forest → Short-term: ↑ provisioning (timber, farmland) → Long-term: ↓ regulating (carbon storage, flood control) ↓ supporting (soil formation, nutrient cycling) → Net: ecosystem services lost > economic gain from clearing
02 · Biodiversity: Levels, Patterns & Measurement

Three Levels of Biodiversity

Genetic Diversity
Variation in DNA within a species. Larger gene pools increase the chance some individuals survive disease, climate shifts, etc. Population bottleneck: catastrophic reduction shrinks the gene pool, increasing inbreeding risk. MVP: minimum viable population — smallest size at which a population can persist without extinction risk from random events.
Species Diversity
Species richness: number of different species in an area. Species evenness: how evenly individuals are distributed among species. Both matter — a community dominated by one species has low diversity even with many species present.
Ecosystem Diversity
Range of habitats/ecosystems in a specific area. More ecosystem types support more species and provide a broader set of ecosystem services. Confusing this with species diversity is a frequent exam error.

Richness vs. Evenness: Classic Exam Scenario

Community A: 100 individuals, 10 species → 91 from one species Community B: 100 individuals, 10 species → ~10 each Same richness. Community B has HIGHER diversity because greater evenness. → Community A is more vulnerable if the dominant species is harmed.

03 · Island Biogeography & Habitat Fragmentation

Islands Are Anywhere Habitat Is Isolated

In APES, an island can be any suitable habitat surrounded by unsuitable habitat — a forest fragment surrounded by farmland functions as an island for forest species. Island biogeography theory predicts that species richness on an island reflects a balance between immigration (new species arriving) and extinction (species disappearing).

Island Size Effect
Larger islands → more habitats, larger populations, lower extinction rates, easier target for migrants → higher species richness.
Isolation Effect
Closer islands → higher immigration rates → higher species richness. Distance = barrier. Fragmented habitats isolated by roads or fields behave like distant islands.
Habitat Fragmentation
Habitat broken into pieces by development, logging, roads. Reduces total area, increases isolation between patches (less immigration, more local extinction), and increases edge habitat proportion.
Edge Effects
Conditions near habitat boundaries differ: more light, wind, temperature fluctuation, easier access for predators and invasive species. Interior specialists decline; edge-adapted and invasive species may expand.
FRQ Application: Forest reserve design Larger reserve → lower extinction risk (more habitat, larger populations) Connected reserves (corridors) → higher immigration → more resilience More edge relative to core → ↑ invasive species, ↑ predation on nest-nesters → Interior specialist birds (neotropical migrants) especially vulnerable
04 · Ecological Tolerance & Adaptations

Law of Tolerance & How Species Adjust

The Law of Tolerance states that the existence, abundance, and distribution of species depend on each species' tolerance level to physical and chemical factors. Each organism has a minimum, maximum, and optimum range for each factor. Outside the range, survival and reproduction decline. Limiting factors are those closest to tolerance extremes.

Behavioral Adaptations
Instincts, mating behavior, vocalizations, migration to avoid extreme temperatures. These are often the most flexible and fastest responses.
Physiological Adaptations
Internal temperature regulation (ectotherm vs. endotherm), digestive enzymes, camouflage coloration, altered metabolic rate in extremes.
Structural Adaptations
Physical features: thick fur, waxy leaf coatings, spines, root depth, conical tree shapes that shed snow, succulents for water storage.
Short-term vs. Long-term
Short-term: temporary, not inherited, no DNA change, not evolutionary. Long-term: involves DNA changes over generations via natural selection. Calling any short-term adjustment an "evolutionary adaptation" is a grading error.
05 · Terrestrial Biomes

Temperature + Precipitation → Biome

A biome is a large ecological region characterized by climate and typical plant/animal communities. The two organizing variables are temperature and precipitation, including their seasonality. Focus on: water availability, growing season length, and disturbance patterns (fire, frozen soil). Biomes blend through ecotones — transition zones that can have high biodiversity from both neighboring regions.

Tropical Rainforest
Warm year-round; high precipitation. Highest species richness. Soils often nutrient-poor — nutrients are in biomass, not soil. Rapid decomposition. Deforestation here has long-term productivity consequences.
Tropical Seasonal / Savanna
Warm with distinct wet and dry seasons. Savanna = grasses with scattered trees, shaped by seasonal rain, fire, and grazing.
Subtropical Desert
Defined by low precipitation (not temperature). Hot or cold. Adaptations: succulents (water storage), nocturnal animals, reduced leaf area, CAM photosynthesis, deep/shallow root systems, waxy coatings.
Woodland / Chaparral
Mild wet winters, hot dry summers. Fire is a common natural disturbance. Many plants resprout after fire or have seeds that germinate after heat/smoke cues. Fire suppression → fuel buildup → hotter fires.
Temperate Grassland
Moderate precipitation, not enough for trees. Very fertile soils (deep roots, organic matter). Fire and grazing maintain grass dominance. Most converted to agriculture globally.
Temperate Deciduous Forest
Warm summers, cold winters, moderate precipitation. Trees drop leaves to reduce water loss. Fertile soils from leaf litter. Often heavily modified by human land use.
Temperate Rainforest
Cooler than tropical but high precipitation. High biomass, significant carbon storage. Dense vegetation, epiphytes common.
Boreal Forest (Taiga)
Largest terrestrial biome. Long cold winters, short summers. Evergreen conifers. Slow decomposition. Stores massive carbon. Needle leaves reduce water loss; conical shapes shed snow.
Tundra
Cold, low precipitation, short growing season. Permafrost limits roots and slows decomposition, affecting nutrient cycling. Low NPP. Warming → permafrost thaw → methane release (positive feedback).
06 · Aquatic Biomes

Salinity · Depth · Light · Nutrients

Aquatic biomes are organized by salinity, water depth, light availability, nutrient levels, and water movement. The photic zone has enough light for photosynthesis; the aphotic zone does not. Light strongly shapes aquatic productivity since food webs begin with photosynthetic producers.

Freshwater Systems

Streams & Rivers
Upstream: colder, clearer, faster, higher dissolved oxygen. Downstream: warmer, slower, more sediments and nutrients. Upstream land use affects communities far downstream — important for FRQs on pollution.
Lake Zones
Littoral (shallow, rooted plants) → Limnetic (open water, plankton) → Profundal (deep, low light, decomposition dominates). Thermal stratification affects oxygen distribution and nutrient cycling.
Freshwater Wetlands
Marshes and swamps. Store water, mitigate flooding, provide habitat, filter water. High plant productivity. Degraded by agriculture, drainage, and development.

Marine & Coastal Systems

Estuaries
Freshwater meets saltwater → brackish, highly variable. Highly productive from river nutrients and shallow well-lit water. Nurseries for fish/shellfish. Buffer storms. Vulnerable to nutrient runoff.
Intertidal Zone
Exposed at low tide, submerged at high tide. Organisms tolerate drying, wave action, temperature swings, and salinity changes. High biodiversity despite physical stress.
Coral Reefs
Warm, shallow, clear water. Highest marine biodiversity. Corals + zooxanthellae (photosynthetic algae). Need clear water for light — excess nutrients fuel algal growth that harms reefs. Sensitive to temperature (bleaching) and acidification.
Open Ocean
Covers most of Earth's surface. Often nutrient-limited at surface → low productivity. Productivity concentrated where nutrients are supplied: upwelling zones (cold nutrient-rich water rises) and coastal delivery.

Eutrophication

Full FRQ chain (never stop early): Fertilizer runoff (N, P) → enters water body → Algal bloom (excess nutrients fuel rapid algal growth) → Algae die → decomposers break down dead algae → Decomposers consume dissolved oxygen → hypoxia / dead zone → Fish kills, loss of aquatic biodiversity → Reduced ecosystem services (fisheries, water recreation, water quality)
07 · Ecological Succession & Disturbance

How Communities Change Through Time

Ecological succession is the gradual, directional process of ecosystem development as community composition changes through time, often after disturbance. The key distinction on exams is whether soil is present.

Primary Succession
Starts on barren areas with no soil (new volcanic rock, land exposed by retreating glaciers). Pioneer species (lichens, mosses) break down rock, add organic matter, slowly form soil. Very slow: 1000+ years. Soil must develop before other organisms can colonize.
Secondary Succession
Starts after disturbance that removes organisms but leaves soil intact (fire, storms, abandoned farmland). Faster (50–200 years) because soil, seeds, roots, and nutrients remain. Fast-growing grasses first, then shrubs, then trees.
Pioneer Species
Early successional organisms; often r-strategists: rapid maturity, short-lived, high population size, niche generalists. They modify conditions to allow later species to establish (facilitation).
Late Succession / K-strategists
Slow maturity, long-lived, lower population size, niche specialists, greater biodiversity. Late-stage communities have higher biomass, more complex nutrient cycling, and greater ecosystem stability.
Resistance vs. Resilience
Resistance: how much an ecosystem changes when disturbed. Resilience: how quickly it returns to prior state. Biodiversity supports resilience — if one species declines, others performing similar roles can partially compensate.
Species Interactions in Succession
Facilitation: one species modifies environment to benefit another. Inhibition: one species modifies environment unfavorably for another. Tolerance: species are not affected by others' presence.

Natural Disturbances: Not Always Harmful

Wildfires
Kill wildlife and remove vegetation → erosion risk. But also: clear dead vegetation, increase light for survivors, ash adds nutrients. Some plants require fire. Fire suppression allows fuel buildup → hotter, more destructive fires later.
Flooding
Kills plants (roots need oxygen), destroys burrows/nests. But: floodplain species are often adapted; floods deposit nutrient-rich sediment along banks. Increases surface runoff and nutrient transport.
Volcanic Eruptions
Remove vegetation, expose soil to erosion. But: over time, volcanic rock weathers into rich soils. Volcanic sulfur gases form atmospheric droplets that cool the troposphere ~2–3°C for years.
Process Timescales
Episodic: irregular intervals (El Niño/La Niña). Periodic: repeated intervals (tides). Random: no pattern (meteorite impacts). Scale and pattern affect which species adapt and how ecosystems recover.
08 · Invasive Species

Why Invaders Win & How to Stop Them

A species is invasive when it is: (1) non-native, (2) spreads rapidly, and (3) causes harm (ecological, economic, or health). Not all introduced species become invasive — "invasive" requires harmful spread. Invasive species are a major driver of biodiversity loss, especially combined with habitat fragmentation.

Enemy Release
Key mechanism: in the native range, predators/parasites/pathogens keep the species in check. In the new range, those enemies are absent — the invader escapes natural control and can reproduce unchecked.
Generalist Advantage
Many successful invaders are generalists: broad diet, wide habitat tolerance, high reproductive rate. Native specialist species may lack defenses or competitive strategies because they never coevolved with the invader.
Introduction Pathways
Ballast water (ships), hull attachments, firewood transport, ornamental plant escapes, pet releases, intentional pest-control introductions. Prevention targeting these pathways is most cost-effective.
Impacts on Biodiversity
Predation (prey lack defenses), competition for light/nutrients/space, habitat alteration (fire regimes, hydrology, soil chemistry), disease introduction. Simplifies food webs, degrades ecosystem services.

Management Strategies & Trade-offs

StrategyMethodTrade-off / Drawback
PreventionInspections, quarantines, transport restrictionsMost cost-effective, but requires international cooperation; hard to catch everything
Mechanical ControlPhysical removal: pulling plants, trapping animalsEffective for small invasions; very labor-intensive; rarely eradicates established populations
Chemical ControlHerbicides, pesticidesEffective but can harm non-target species; pollution concerns; resistance can develop
Biological ControlIntroduce natural predator/parasite/pathogenCan be effective long-term but biocontrol agents can become new invasives or harm non-target species; requires careful testing
