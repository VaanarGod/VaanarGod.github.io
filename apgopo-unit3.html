01 · Civil Liberties and Civil Rights

Foundations and Incorporation

Unit 3 starts with the difference between limits on government and guarantees of equal treatment. Civil liberties protect individuals from government interference, while civil rights focus on discrimination, equal citizenship, and access to public life.

The Bill of Rights originally restrained the national government. The major shift came after the Civil War, when the Fourteenth Amendment gave the Court a way to protect people from state governments through due process and equal protection.

Think of this unit as a map of constitutional claims. First ask who acted. If the actor is government, a constitutional right may apply directly. If the actor is private, the answer may depend on a civil rights statute. Then ask what right is involved, what legal standard applies, and whether the government can justify the restriction or classification.

Selective Incorporation
The Court applies most Bill of Rights protections to states one right at a time through the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause. Gitlow v. New York is a key early speech example. Later cases incorporated criminal procedure rights, such as Mapp, Gideon, and Miranda, and the Second Amendment through McDonald.
State Action
Constitutional claims usually require government action. A public school, police department, state legislature, or city election office is clearly government. A private employer, restaurant, or university may be regulated by statute, but its unfair action is not automatically unconstitutional.
Scrutiny
Rational basis is easiest for government, intermediate scrutiny is commonly tied to sex discrimination, and strict scrutiny applies to race classifications and many fundamental-right burdens. The level of scrutiny often predicts the outcome, so naming the right test is a major FRQ skill.

What Is Not Fully Incorporated

Incorporation is not a perfect copy-paste of the whole Bill of Rights onto the states. AP courses commonly highlight the Fifth Amendment grand jury indictment requirement, the Seventh Amendment civil jury guarantee, and the Third Amendment quartering protection as examples that are not usually treated like the major incorporated rights. The takeaway is that incorporation happened gradually, case by case.

How to Read a Scenario

If a state arrests someone, restricts protest, searches a home, leads school prayer, segregates schools, or closes polling places, begin with government action. If a private business discriminates, think about statutes. If a law classifies people by race or sex, move to Equal Protection and scrutiny. If a law limits speech or religion, move to First Amendment doctrine.

02 · First Amendment Freedoms

Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition, and Association

The First Amendment is a big Unit 3 bucket because it protects how people believe, speak, publish, gather, and challenge government. The AP skill is not just listing the freedoms. You need to identify the type of restriction: is government sponsoring religion, burdening religious practice, targeting a viewpoint, regulating the logistics of a protest, censoring a newspaper, or punishing speech after it happens?

The religion clauses can pull in different directions. The Establishment Clause limits government sponsorship, endorsement, coercion, or official favoritism toward religion. The Free Exercise Clause protects religious belief and practice, but it does not make every religiously motivated action immune from law.

Public-school prayer is a classic setting. A student praying privately is usually protected private expression. A teacher or school official leading prayer is more likely to be government-sponsored religious activity.

Engel v. Vitale
Government-written or government-led prayer in public schools violates establishment principles, even if participation is framed as voluntary.
Lemon and Kennedy
The Lemon test asks about secular purpose, primary effect, and entanglement. Newer cases place more weight on history, tradition, and coercion.
Smith and Yoder
Neutral, generally applicable laws do not always require religious exemptions. Some cases and statutes still protect religious accommodation.

Speech and Press

Speech cases usually ask whether government is attacking the message or regulating the circumstances. Content-based restrictions target ideas, subjects, or viewpoints and are very suspicious. Content-neutral restrictions regulate time, place, and manner, like permits, sound limits, or traffic rules. Those can be constitutional if they are reasonable, applied fairly, and leave open other channels for expression.

The Court gives political expression especially strong protection. Brandenburg v. Ohio protects advocacy unless it is intended and likely to produce imminent lawless action. Texas v. Johnson protects flag burning because offensive political expression is still expression. Tinker v. Des Moines protects student symbolic speech unless school officials can show material and substantial disruption.

Prior Restraint
Prior restraint means censorship before publication. New York Times Co. v. United States makes it extremely hard for government to stop publication, even when government claims national security concerns.
Defamation
False statements that damage reputation receive less protection, but New York Times v. Sullivan requires public officials to prove actual malice: knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth.
Obscenity
Miller v. California created a three-part obscenity framework involving prurient interest, patently offensive depiction, and lack of serious value.
Assembly and Petition
Peaceful gathering, protest, lawsuits, lobbying, and formal petitions are protected. Government can still regulate crowds for safety and order if the rules are neutral and evenly applied.

Compelled Speech and Association

The First Amendment also protects people from being forced to express a government-approved message. West Virginia v. Barnette is the key example: students could not be forced to salute the flag. Speech and assembly also imply freedom of association, meaning government usually cannot punish people simply for joining a group unless there is a serious and specific national security concern.

Common Trap

Do not write that religion is banned from public schools, or that offensive speech is automatically unprotected. The question is usually who is speaking, what government is doing, and whether the rule targets the message.
03 · Due Process and Criminal Procedure

Rights of the Accused

Rights of the accused limit government power from investigation through trial and punishment. The basic fear is that an unchecked state could search, arrest, interrogate, convict, or punish without fair procedures.

The Fourth Amendment focuses on searches and seizures; the Fifth protects against compelled self-incrimination and double jeopardy; the Sixth protects fair trial rights and counsel; the Eighth limits excessive bail, fines, and cruel punishment.

This section is about procedure: even if government is trying to enforce a legitimate criminal law, it must follow constitutional rules. Courts often ask whether police had enough evidence for a stop or search, whether a confession was coerced, whether a defendant had counsel, and whether punishment is constitutionally excessive.

Fourth Amendment
The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. Warrants usually need probable cause and must be specific about the place searched and items seized. Exceptions include consent, plain view, search incident to lawful arrest, some vehicle searches, and exigent circumstances.
Mapp and Terry
Mapp applies the exclusionary rule to states, meaning illegally obtained evidence is generally excluded. Terry allows limited stop-and-frisk based on reasonable suspicion, a lower standard than probable cause.
Miranda and Gideon
Miranda requires warnings during custodial interrogation: silence, counsel, and awareness that statements may be used in court. Gideon requires states to provide counsel for indigent felony defendants.
Eighth Amendment
Capital punishment debates often turn on procedures, limits, and evolving standards of decency, not a simple yes-or-no rule.

Fifth, Sixth, Seventh, and Habeas

The Fifth Amendment protects against self-incrimination and double jeopardy, includes a takings rule, and requires due process. The Sixth Amendment covers speedy and public trial, impartial jury, notice of charges, confrontation of witnesses, compulsory process, and assistance of counsel. The Seventh Amendment covers civil juries in some federal civil cases and is commonly taught as not incorporated against states. Habeas corpus lets a person challenge unlawful detention and is tied to the Constitution’s broader fear of illegal imprisonment.

Evidence Exceptions

The exclusionary rule has limits. Evidence may sometimes come in through good-faith reliance on a warrant, inevitable discovery, or other exceptions. So an AP answer should not just say "illegal search means automatic freedom." Say what happens to the evidence and whether an exception might apply.

Common Trap

Miranda is not triggered by every police conversation. Look for both custody and interrogation.
04 · Privacy and Other Liberties

Second Amendment, Third Amendment, Ninth Amendment, and Privacy

Some Unit 3 rights are not only about speech or criminal procedure. The Court also decides whether a liberty is fundamental, whether it binds states, and how much room government has to regulate.

The Second Amendment protects an individual firearms right, while privacy doctrine grows from a mix of amendments and the concept of substantive due process rather than one explicit privacy clause.

This topic can feel scattered because it connects several doctrines: incorporation, unenumerated rights, substantive due process, and individual liberty. The unifying question is whether the Constitution protects a personal choice strongly enough to limit government regulation.

Heller and McDonald
Heller recognizes an individual right to possess firearms for lawful purposes such as self-defense in the home. McDonald incorporates that right against states through the Fourteenth Amendment.
Ninth Amendment
The Ninth Amendment signals that people retain rights not specifically listed, which connects to privacy and unenumerated-rights arguments.
Privacy Cases
Griswold recognizes marital privacy, Dobbs overrules Roe and Casey, and Obergefell protects same-sex marriage.

Third Amendment and Historical Context

The Third Amendment forbids quartering soldiers in private homes without consent. It rarely appears as a modern case rule, but it helps show how the Bill of Rights responded to colonial experiences with arbitrary government power.

Privacy Is Not One Clause

The Constitution does not contain one sentence saying "right to privacy." Instead, privacy developed from several amendments and from the Fourteenth Amendment’s protection of liberty. Griswold is often taught as the beginning of modern privacy doctrine. Roe and Casey once protected abortion access, but Dobbs overruled those cases and returned most abortion regulation to states. Dobbs did not automatically erase every privacy case, so be precise about what changed.

FRQ Move

Separate policy arguments from constitutional doctrine. The question is what the Constitution protects, not whether the policy is good.
05 · Civil Rights and Equal Protection

The Struggle for Equality Under Law

Civil rights questions focus on whether government is treating people equally and whether legal protections against discrimination are being enforced. The Reconstruction Amendments created the foundation: the Thirteenth abolished slavery, the Fourteenth created citizenship and equal protection, and the Fifteenth protected voting rights from racial discrimination.

Equal Protection analysis asks whether a classification exists, what kind it is, which level of scrutiny applies, and whether the government satisfies that standard.

Unlike many civil liberties questions, civil rights questions often involve both constitutional and statutory paths. The Equal Protection Clause is constitutional. Laws like the Civil Rights Act of 1964, Voting Rights Act, Title IX, ADA, and Fair Housing Act are statutes. A strong AP answer knows which one fits the facts.

Plessy and Brown
Plessy upheld separate but equal. Brown rejected school segregation and became the key constitutional turning point, though implementation required later court orders, executive action, and federal legislation.
Intent and Impact
Constitutional Equal Protection claims often need proof of discriminatory intent. Disparate impact means a policy has unequal effects, but unequal effects alone may not prove a constitutional violation without evidence of purpose.
Statutory Rights
The Civil Rights Act, Fair Housing Act, Equal Pay Act, Title IX, ADA, IDEA, Age Discrimination Act, and Lilly Ledbetter Act all expand anti-discrimination protections beyond direct constitutional claims.
Affirmative Action
Bakke, Grutter, and Students for Fair Admissions show strict scrutiny in race-conscious admissions.

Reconstruction to Jim Crow

After the Civil War, the Reconstruction Amendments promised a new constitutional order. But federal enforcement weakened, and many states used Jim Crow laws, poll taxes, literacy tests, and grandfather clauses to preserve racial hierarchy. The Court’s narrow reading of federal civil rights power in the late 1800s made it harder to challenge private discrimination until later civil rights legislation.

Women and LGBTQ+ Rights

Women’s civil rights expanded through the Nineteenth Amendment, Equal Pay Act, Civil Rights Act of 1964, Title IX, Civil Rights Restoration Act, and Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act. In constitutional law, United States v. Virginia is the key sex-discrimination case and shows intermediate scrutiny. LGBTQ+ rights often combine due process and equal protection reasoning, especially in Lawrence v. Texas and Obergefell v. Hodges.

Affirmative Action

Affirmative action policies try to expand opportunity for groups that have faced discrimination. Critics often describe them as reverse discrimination. Because these policies use race, the Court applies strict scrutiny. Bakke rejected quotas but allowed some consideration of race, Grutter upheld limited race-conscious admissions, and Students for Fair Admissions sharply restricted race-conscious admissions policies.

Common Trap

Unequal outcomes are not automatically unconstitutional. Ask whether there is classification, intent, scrutiny, and a constitutional or statutory hook.
06 · Voting Rights

Political Equality and Access to the Ballot

Voting rights are both civil rights and democratic structure. Election rules can be neutral administration, but they can also become tools of exclusion. AP questions often ask whether a rule burdens voters, whether it has a discriminatory purpose or effect, and whether a constitutional amendment or statute applies.

Voting rights disputes are rarely just about whether a state may regulate elections. States can regulate registration, ballot design, election timing, ID requirements, and polling places. The constitutional question is whether the regulation unfairly burdens voting or discriminates. The statutory question is often whether the rule violates the Voting Rights Act.

Voting Amendments
The Fifteenth protects race-based voting rights, the Nineteenth protects women’s suffrage, the Twenty-Fourth bans federal poll taxes, and the Twenty-Sixth sets the voting age at 18.
Voting Rights Act
The VRA attacked literacy tests and other racial barriers. Shelby County weakened preclearance by striking the coverage formula.
Representation
Baker v. Carr opened redistricting challenges to federal courts. Reynolds v. Sims supports one person, one vote.

Preclearance and Shelby County

Preclearance required certain jurisdictions with histories of voting discrimination to get federal approval before changing election rules. Shelby County v. Holder did not erase the Voting Rights Act, but it struck down the coverage formula that decided which jurisdictions needed preclearance. The effect was to weaken a major federal oversight tool.

Modern Access Debates

Modern cases and AP scenarios may involve voter ID, registration deadlines, polling-place closures, district maps, or majority-minority districts. A strong answer identifies the state’s claimed interest, such as preventing fraud or orderly administration, and then weighs the burden on voters. For race-related claims, look for evidence of discriminatory intent, racial effects, unusual procedure, or Voting Rights Act relevance.

FRQ Move

For polling-place closures, voter ID, or registration rules, weigh state interests against voter burdens and look for evidence of racial discrimination or VRA relevance.

Foundations

Civil Liberties, Civil Rights, and Incorporation

Bill of Rights · state action · due process · equal protection

Two Questions

Liberties vs. Rights

Civil liberties ask whether government can restrict an individual: speech, searches, counsel, worship. Civil rights ask whether government treats people or groups equally, especially under the Fourteenth Amendment.

14th Amendment

The Bridge to the States

Originally, the Bill of Rights restrained the national government. After the Fourteenth Amendment, the Court used the Due Process Clause to apply most protections to state and local governments through selective incorporation.

Threshold Issue

State Action Matters

Most constitutional rights claims require government action. Private discrimination is often addressed through statutes such as the Civil Rights Act of 1964, while state discrimination may trigger direct Fourteenth Amendment review.

Court Tests

Scrutiny, Balancing, and Legal Standards

Rational basis · intermediate scrutiny · strict scrutiny · time/place/manner

Default

Rational Basis

The government usually wins when the law is rationally related to a legitimate government interest. This is the lowest level of review and is used for most ordinary classifications.

Middle Tier

Intermediate Scrutiny

Often used for sex discrimination, this asks whether the law is substantially related to an important government interest. United States v. Virginia is a useful anchor.

Hardest Test

Strict Scrutiny

Race classifications and burdens on fundamental rights usually trigger strict scrutiny: the law must be narrowly tailored to a compelling government interest. The government usually has a difficult defense.

First Amendment

Religion: Establishment and Free Exercise

School prayer · Lemon · history and tradition · neutral laws

Establishment

Government Sponsorship

The Establishment Clause bars government from establishing, endorsing, or coercing religious practice. Engel v. Vitale shows why government-led prayer in public schools is unconstitutional.

Historic Test

Lemon and Its Shift

Lemon v. Kurtzman asked about secular purpose, primary effect, and excessive entanglement. Newer cases, including Kennedy v. Bremerton, emphasize history and tradition more heavily.

Free Exercise

Belief vs. Conduct

The Free Exercise Clause protects religious belief and some practice, but religious claims do not automatically override neutral laws that apply generally.

Neutral Laws

Smith and Exemptions

Employment Division v. Smith held that neutral, generally applicable laws do not always require religious exemptions. Legislatures may still create protections through laws like RFRA.

First Amendment

Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition, and Association

Content regulation · symbolic speech · prior restraint · defamation · protest

Speech

Ideas Get the Most Protection

The Court is most suspicious when government targets ideas or viewpoints. Brandenburg v. Ohio protects advocacy unless it is directed to and likely to produce imminent lawless action.

Symbolic Speech

Conduct Can Communicate

Tinker v. Des Moines protects student armbands absent substantial disruption. Texas v. Johnson protects flag burning as political expression.

Press

Prior Restraint Is Rarely Allowed

New York Times Co. v. United States creates a heavy presumption against censorship before publication. New York Times v. Sullivan protects criticism by requiring public officials to show actual malice in defamation cases.

Public Order

Time, Place, and Manner

Government can use content-neutral rules for permits, noise, traffic, and safety, but it cannot deny a protest permit because officials dislike the message.

Criminal Procedure

Rights of the Accused and Due Process

Searches · exclusion · counsel · interrogation · punishment

Fourth Amendment

Searches and Seizures

Search warrants generally require probable cause and must state where police may search and what may be seized. Exceptions include consent, plain view, legal arrest, vehicle searches, and emergencies.

Evidence

Mapp and Terry

Mapp v. Ohio applies the exclusionary rule to states. Terry v. Ohio permits limited stop-and-frisk based on reasonable suspicion, not a vague hunch.

Fifth and Sixth

Silence, Counsel, and Fair Trial

Miranda v. Arizona requires warnings during custodial interrogation. Gideon v. Wainwright requires states to provide counsel for indigent felony defendants.

Eighth Amendment

Bail, Fines, and Punishment

The Eighth Amendment bars excessive bail and fines and cruel and unusual punishment. Death penalty debates often turn on procedure, limits, and evolving standards of decency.

Liberty and Privacy

Second Amendment, Third Amendment, and Substantive Due Process

Heller · McDonald · unenumerated rights · Griswold · Dobbs · Obergefell

Second Amendment

Individual Right and Incorporation

District of Columbia v. Heller recognized an individual right to possess firearms for lawful purposes such as home self-defense. McDonald v. Chicago incorporated that right against the states.

Unenumerated Rights

Privacy as a Doctrine

The Constitution does not explicitly say "privacy," but cases such as Griswold v. Connecticut connect privacy to multiple amendments and substantive due process.

Modern Privacy

Roe, Dobbs, Lawrence, Obergefell

Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization overruled Roe and Casey on abortion. Lawrence and Obergefell remain central to due process and equal protection arguments about LGBTQ+ rights.

Civil Rights

Equal Protection and the Struggle for Equality

Reconstruction · segregation · intent · civil rights statutes · affirmative action

Reconstruction

13th, 14th, and 15th

The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery, the Fourteenth created citizenship, due process, and equal protection, and the Fifteenth barred racial discrimination in voting.

Segregation

Plessy to Brown

Plessy v. Ferguson upheld "separate but equal." Brown v. Board of Education rejected school segregation and became a constitutional turning point.

Proof Problem

Intent vs. Impact

Equal Protection claims often require evidence of discriminatory intent. Unequal effects may matter, but disparate impact alone is usually stronger under statutory frameworks than direct constitutional claims.

Race-Conscious Policy

Affirmative Action

Bakke, Grutter, and Students for Fair Admissions show how strict scrutiny governs race-conscious admissions policies.

Civil Rights Statutes

Law as an Enforcement Tool

CRA 1964 · VRA · Title IX · ADA · fair housing · equal pay

Race and Public Life

1964, 1965, 1968

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 banned discrimination in public accommodations and employment. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 attacked racial barriers to voting. The Fair Housing Act banned racial discrimination in housing.

Women and Education

Equal Pay, Title VII, Title IX

The Equal Pay Act, Title VII of the 1964 Act, Title IX, and the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act all expand statutory protection against sex discrimination.

Disability and Age

IDEA, ADA, and Age Protection

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act protects educational access, while the Americans with Disabilities Act requires broad anti-discrimination and accessibility protections.

Voting Rights

Political Equality and Access to the Ballot

15th · 19th · 24th · 26th · preclearance · one person, one vote

Expansion

Voting Amendments

  • 15th Amendment: race cannot be used to deny voting rights.
  • 19th Amendment: women's suffrage.
  • 24th Amendment: bans poll taxes in federal elections.
  • 26th Amendment: lowers voting age to 18.
VRA

Federal Oversight

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 enforced the Fifteenth Amendment with tools against literacy tests and discrimination. Shelby County v. Holder struck down the coverage formula for preclearance.

Representation

One Person, One Vote

Baker v. Carr opened federal courts to redistricting disputes. Reynolds v. Sims advanced the principle that districts should be roughly equal in population.

Building Rights and Liberties Answers

Paragraph Model

Actor -> Right -> Test -> Result

Start by proving there is government action, identify the constitutional right or statute, apply the right standard, and explain the likely outcome with one case or clause.

Case Use

Cases Are Rules, Not Decorations

Name the case only after you know the legal principle. Example: Tinker is useful for public-school student expression, not every speech dispute.

Common Error

Constitutional vs. Statutory

Do not call every unfair private action unconstitutional. If the actor is private, your best answer may be a statute like the Civil Rights Act, ADA, Title IX, or VRA.
