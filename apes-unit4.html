Core Frame
What Unit 4 Is Really About

Unit 4 connects the physical Earth — its moving plates, dynamic soils, and layered atmosphere — to environmental science. The thread: Earth's systems are interconnected. Plate movement shapes where minerals and fertile soils form; soils determine agricultural productivity; the atmosphere's energy balance drives climate.

Master three scales: the deep-Earth scale of plate tectonics, the surface scale of soil formation and degradation, and the atmospheric scale of energy balance and greenhouse gases. The exam ties each to human impacts and policy.

Common Mistakes

Avoid These

  • Assuming all convergent boundaries produce volcanoes — continental collision doesn't; subduction does.
  • Placing the ozone layer in the troposphere — it's in the stratosphere.
  • Confusing porosity with permeability. Clay has high porosity but low permeability.
  • Thinking clay holds less water than sand — clay holds more, drains more slowly.
  • Treating greenhouse effect and ozone depletion as the same problem.
  • Saying soil forms quickly — productive topsoil takes centuries.
  • Calling salinization a fertilizer problem — it's a water-balance and drainage issue.
  • Proposing windbreaks for water erosion on steep slopes — match strategy to erosion type.
Formulas & Key Numbers

Cheatsheet

  • Porosity = Vw ÷ Vt  (1 cm³ = 1 mL)
  • Albedo = reflected ÷ incoming radiation
  • Loam = balanced sand + silt + clay + humus
  • Soil particle size: gravel > sand > silt > clay (≤ 2 mm inorganic)
  • Troposphere: 0–10 km; temp decreases with altitude; weather here
  • Stratosphere: 10–50 km; ozone layer; temp increases with altitude
  • Volcanic aerosols persist 1–3 years in stratosphere

01 · Plate Tectonics and Geologic Processes

Earth's Moving Surface

Plate tectonics holds that Earth's lithosphere is broken into plates moving atop the ductile asthenosphere, driven by mantle convection. In 1915, Alfred Wegener proposed all continents were once joined as Pangaea, supported by matching fossils, rock types, coastline fit, and paleoclimate evidence. Mechanism came later via seafloor spreading — confirmed by magnetic stripe patterns and increasing rock age away from mid-ocean ridges.

Plate Boundary Types

Boundary Motion Features Examples
Divergent Apart New crust, shallow quakes, volcanism, rifts Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East African Rift
Convergent / Subduction Together (one sinks) Deep trenches, volcanic arcs, powerful quakes; island arcs (2 oceanic) Cascades, Aleutians
Convergent / Collision Together (both buoyant) Mountain ranges, quakes — NO volcanoes Himalayas
Transform Slide past Earthquakes, minimal volcanism San Andreas Fault
Hot Spots
Mantle plumes pierce mid-plate lithosphere. As the plate moves, a chain of volcanoes forms — strong evidence for plate motion direction and rate. Example: Hawaiian island chain.
Earthquake Damage Factors
Magnitude, depth (shallow = more surface damage), distance from epicenter, local geology (soft sediments amplify; saturated soils liquify), and building codes. Two equal-magnitude quakes can have vastly different impacts.
Volcanic Effects
Effusive (lava) = slow, destroys property. Explosive (ash, pyroclastic) = acutely deadly. Long-term: ash weathers to fertile soils; large eruptions inject stratospheric aerosols, temporarily cooling climate.
Exam Key
Volcanoes occur at subduction zones — NOT at continent-continent collisions. Plates ride atop a ductile asthenosphere, not a global ocean of liquid magma.
02 · The Rock Cycle

How Rocks Transform & Why It Matters for Resources

Type Formation Key Features APES Examples
Igneous Cooled magma/lava Intrusive (slow, coarse-grained) vs Extrusive (fast, fine-grained); classified by silica content Granite (intrusive), Basalt (extrusive)
Sedimentary Compacted sediments or chemical precipitation Fossils form ONLY here; often contain fossil fuels Limestone, Sandstone, most Coal
Metamorphic Heat + pressure (no melting) Associated with collision zones and deep burial Diamond, Marble, Slate, Anthracite coal
Resource Connection
Sedimentary basins concentrate fossil fuels. Specific geologic settings concentrate mineral ores. Weathering of all rock types produces soil parent material. APES focuses on connecting rock type to hazards and resource distribution.
03 · Soil Formation, Horizons & Ecology

Soil Is a Living, Layered System

Soil forms through rock weathering plus accumulation and decomposition of organic matter. Productive topsoil can take centuries or longer to form — it is not quickly renewable on human timescales.

Five Factors of Soil Formation

Parent Material
Rock/minerals from which soil derives (local bedrock or transported by water, wind, glaciers). Determines mineral composition and initial nutrients.
Climate
Precipitation and temperature control weathering rates and organic matter decomposition. Tropics = rapid weathering; arid = slow formation.
Topography
Slope affects drainage, erosion, and depth. Steep slopes lose topsoil; low areas accumulate sediment and organic matter.
Organisms
Microbes, earthworms, roots, fungi decompose litter and cycle nutrients. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium) convert N₂ to usable forms. Earthworms mix humus with minerals.
Time
Well-developed fertile soils take thousands of years. Loss is fast, recovery is slow — the core reason soil conservation matters.

Soil Horizons

Horizon Name Description
O Surface litter Leaves and partially decomposed organic debris
A Topsoil ⭐ Most fertile; organic matter + organisms + minerals. Thick in grasslands. Key for agriculture — erosion here is most damaging.
E Zone of leaching Dissolved materials move downward; present in some soils
B Subsoil Accumulates iron, aluminum, clay leached from above; often yellowish/reddish
C Parent material Partially weathered inorganic material transitioning to bedrock
Soil Ecology
The soil food web — bacteria, fungi, insects, worms, roots — decomposes organic matter, releases plant-available nutrients, and improves aeration and water movement. Humus (decomposed organic matter) improves structure, water retention, aeration, and pH stability.
04 · Soil Texture, Porosity & Permeability

Particle Size Controls Water, Air & Nutrients

Type Pore Size Drainage Nutrient Holding Key Note
Sand Largest Very fast Low Low surface area; water moves too fast for many crops
Silt Medium Moderate Moderate-high Fertile; easily transported by water (erosion risk)
Clay Smallest Slow (low permeability) Highest (large surface area) Compacts easily; can waterlog; high porosity, low permeability
Loam Mixed Balanced — ideal High Best for most crops: sand + silt + clay + humus
Porosity
Total pore space. Formula: Porosity = Vw ÷ Vt (water volume to saturate ÷ total saturated volume; 1 cm³ = 1 mL). Clay has HIGH porosity (many tiny pores) but LOW permeability.
Permeability
How easily water flows through connected pores. Sand = HIGH permeability (large, connected pores). Clay = LOW permeability despite high porosity. Low permeability → more runoff, erosion, less groundwater recharge.
Infiltration
Water entering soil from the surface. Low infiltration → high runoff → flood risk, erosion, less groundwater recharge. Vegetation and organic matter improve infiltration.
Leaching
Downward movement of dissolved substances after saturation. Leaches nutrients into B horizon where roots may not reach — concern in high-rainfall regions and over-irrigated soils.
Porosity Calculation Example: Soil sample total volume = 400 mL Water needed to saturate = 160 mL Porosity = 160 ÷ 400 = 0.40 = 40% Remember: 1 cm³ = 1 mL Clay: high porosity, low permeability (pores tiny, poorly connected) Sand: lower porosity, HIGH permeability (pores large, well connected)
05 · Soil Degradation

Erosion, Desertification, Salinization & More

Water Erosion
Flowing water removes topsoil. Increases with bare soil, steep slopes, intense rain, downslope plowing. Carries sediment, nutrients, pesticides → turbidity, eutrophication downstream.
Wind Erosion
Common in dry, exposed fields. Removes fine fertile particles. Dust storms reduce soil quality. Prevented by vegetation, windbreaks, cover crops.
Landslides / Mudslides
Triggered by heavy rain, earthquakes, wildfires, or construction removing vegetation. Most likely on steep slopes with prior slide history or concentrated runoff channels.
Desertification
Dryland degradation from overgrazing, deforestation, unsustainable farming, drought. NOT simply natural desert expansion — reflects land use exceeding dryland limits.
Salinization
Salt accumulates as irrigation water evaporates; poor drainage prevents flushing. Arid/semi-arid regions. Water-balance problem, NOT too much fertilizer. Hard to reverse once severe.
Waterlogging
Soil becomes saturated, depleting oxygen for roots. Often co-occurs with salinization under poorly drained irrigation. Damages crops and structure.
Accelerated by Poor Practices
Improper plowing, monoculture, overgrazing, and removing crop residues all accelerate erosion and nutrient depletion. Over-tilling breaks soil structure, reduces infiltration, and creates a runoff feedback loop.
06 · Soil Conservation Strategies

Matching Solutions to Degradation Causes

Contour Plowing
Plow along elevation contours, perpendicular to slope. Creates ridges that hold water and slow runoff. Best for hilly agricultural land — water erosion.
Terracing
Level steps on steep slopes drastically reduce runoff speed. High initial cost, very effective long-term. Water erosion on steep hillsides.
Strip Cropping
Alternating rows of crops and grass/cover strips. Grass strips slow runoff and catch sediment. Water erosion.
Riparian Buffers
Vegetation along waterways traps sediment and nutrients before they reach streams. Protects water quality and aquatic habitat.
Windbreaks (Shelterbelts)
Rows of trees perpendicular to prevailing winds reduce wind speed and protect topsoil. For wind erosion — NOT water erosion on slopes.
No-till / Cover Crops
No-till: minimal disturbance, preserves structure, reduces erosion dramatically (but may increase herbicide use). Cover crops: keep soil rooted between seasons, add organic matter, can fix nitrogen.
Practice Mechanism Tradeoff
No-till Preserves structure; reduces erosion up to 90% May require more herbicide for weed control
Crop rotation Breaks pest cycles; legumes restore nitrogen; varied roots improve structure More planning; market constraints on crop choice
Compost / Manure Adds organic matter; improves water retention, structure, microbial life Labor, cost; excess phosphorus runoff risk
07 · The Atmosphere: Composition & Structure

Earth's Protective Gaseous Shell

Nitrogen (N₂) ~78%
Most abundant. Essential for amino acids and nucleic acids. Enters ecosystems via nitrogen fixation and lightning; returns via denitrification and combustion.
Oxygen (O₂) ~21%
Accumulated significantly ~2.5 billion years ago (Great Oxidation Event). Produced by photosynthesis; used in cellular respiration. GOE oxidized methane to CO₂, weakening greenhouse effect → global cooling.
Water Vapor (H₂O) 0–4%
Highly variable; highest near equator and tropical oceans; lowest in deserts and poles. Sources: evaporation, transpiration, combustion, volcanic eruptions.
CO₂ (trace but critical)
Small fraction, large climate influence as key greenhouse gas. Increasing from fossil fuel combustion and deforestation. Early Earth had high volcanic CO₂ enabling earliest life.

Atmospheric Layers

Layer Altitude Temperature Trend Key Role
Troposphere 0–~10 km Decreases with altitude ~75% of atmosphere; all weather; almost all water vapor; pressure decreases with height
Stratosphere ~10–50 km Increases with altitude Contains protective ozone (O₃) that absorbs UV. Do NOT place ozone in troposphere.

A temperature inversion occurs when a warm air layer caps cooler surface air, preventing vertical mixing. Pollutants accumulate near the ground — worsening smog in valleys and basins. Classic scenario: calm winter morning, city in a valley, cold air trapped under warm cap.

08 · Energy Balance, Greenhouse Effect & Albedo

How Earth Heats and Cools

Albedo
Fraction of incoming sunlight reflected. Ice/snow = high albedo (~80%). Ocean/forests/asphalt = low albedo. Melting ice (replacing high albedo with low) creates a warming positive feedback. Land-cover change affects local and global temperatures.
Natural Greenhouse Effect
H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, N₂O absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, warming the troposphere. Without this, Earth would be ~33°C colder. Essential for life.
Enhanced Greenhouse Effect
Human-driven increases in greenhouse gases intensify warming. CO₂ from fossil fuels, CH₄ from agriculture/landfills, N₂O from fertilizers add to the atmospheric "blanket."
Ozone Layer — SEPARATE Issue
Stratospheric O₃ absorbs UV radiation. Ozone depletion (CFCs) → more UV at surface → skin cancer, harm to phytoplankton. Different gases, different radiation (UV not IR), different layer from greenhouse effect.
Volcanic Aerosols
Sulfur-rich eruptions inject aerosols into stratosphere → reflect sunlight → tropospheric cooling + stratospheric warming. Persist 1–3 years. Can also accelerate ozone destruction.
Convection & Weather
Warm air rises, cool air sinks → pressure differences → wind. Unequal solar heating (tropics receive more than poles) drives global circulation patterns and weather systems.
Energy Balance Summary: Incoming solar radiation (shortwave) → Some REFLECTED by clouds, ice, land (albedo) → Some ABSORBED by surface and atmosphere Earth emits longwave (infrared) radiation outward → Greenhouse gases ABSORB & RE-EMIT → warms troposphere Equilibrium: Incoming = Outgoing ↑ Greenhouse gases → less outgoing → warming ↓ Albedo (less ice, more asphalt) → more absorption → warming Volcanic aerosols → more reflection → temporary cooling (1–3 yrs)
09 · Water Systems

The Hydrosphere: Freshwater, Oceans, and Global Patterns

Water covers ~71% of Earth's surface, but only ~2.5% is freshwater, and most of that is locked in glaciers. The hydrologic cycle — evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, and infiltration — continuously redistributes water. APES questions on water systems connect physical geography (where water goes) to ecological and human consequences (what happens when it's disrupted).

Hydrologic Cycle
Evapotranspiration: water leaving Earth's surface via evaporation + plant transpiration. Infiltration: water soaking into soil → groundwater recharge. Runoff: water flowing over land surface → streams → rivers. Impervious surfaces (pavement, buildings) reduce infiltration and increase runoff, raising flood risk and reducing aquifer recharge.
Watersheds
A watershed (drainage basin) is the area of land that drains to a single outlet. Anything that happens in the watershed — agriculture, urbanization, deforestation, mining — can affect water quality and quantity downstream. Watershed management is a key conservation strategy.
Groundwater & Aquifers
An aquifer is an underground layer of permeable rock saturated with water. Recharge zones are areas where water infiltrates and replenishes aquifers. Overpumping causes the water table to drop, land subsidence, saltwater intrusion in coastal aquifers, and eventually aquifer depletion (Ogallala Aquifer is a key APES example).
Ocean Currents
Surface currents are driven by wind and the Coriolis effect; they redistribute heat globally and moderate coastal climates. Thermohaline circulation (deep ocean conveyor belt) is driven by differences in temperature and salinity — cold, salty water sinks and drives global ocean circulation. Climate change could slow thermohaline circulation by adding freshwater from melting ice.
El Niño / La Niña (ENSO)
El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is a periodic warming (El Niño) and cooling (La Niña) of the central/eastern Pacific Ocean. El Niño: weakened trade winds → warm water moves east → disrupts precipitation globally (droughts in Australia/SE Asia, flooding in South America). Affects fisheries (disrupts upwelling), agriculture, and wildfire risk.
Upwelling
Upwelling occurs when wind pushes surface water away from a coastline, drawing cold, nutrient-rich deep water up. Creates some of Earth's most productive fisheries (Peru, Pacific Northwest). El Niño suppresses upwelling → fish populations crash → seabird and marine mammal die-offs.
Freshwater Availability
~97.5% of Earth's water is saltwater. Of freshwater, ~69% is in glaciers and ice caps, ~30% is groundwater, and less than 1% is in surface water (lakes, rivers, wetlands). Uneven global distribution means water scarcity is a growing crisis — affecting ~2 billion people. Climate change is shifting precipitation patterns and accelerating glacial melt.
Water Use Sectors
Agriculture accounts for ~70% of global freshwater withdrawals (irrigation). Industry uses ~20%, and municipal/domestic use accounts for ~10%. Irrigated agriculture often uses consumptive use — water that evaporates or is transpired and does not return to the local water source — unlike cooling water that is returned.

Rain Shadow Effect

When moist air rises over a mountain, it cools and drops precipitation on the windward side. The leeward (downwind) side receives dry air — creating a rain shadow desert. Examples: Sierra Nevada creates the Great Basin Desert; Andes creates the Atacama. APES connects this to biome distribution and why deserts appear next to coastal mountains.

Coriolis Effect & Global Precipitation Patterns

Earth's rotation deflects moving air and water (rightward in Northern Hemisphere, leftward in Southern). This creates Hadley cells (tropical convection cells that produce equatorial rain and subtropical deserts at ~30° latitude), prevailing westerlies, and polar easterlies. Understanding these explains why deserts cluster at 30° N/S and why tropical rainforests cluster near the equator.

APES Exam Focus

Common question types: trace water from precipitation through a watershed to identify pollution sources; predict the effect of removing riparian vegetation on stream temperature and erosion; explain why El Niño reduces upwelling and collapses fisheries; explain why paving a watershed increases flooding; calculate consumptive vs. non-consumptive water use.
10 · Global Wind Patterns

Atmospheric Circulation, Wind Belts, and Climate

Earth's atmosphere circulates in predictable cells driven by unequal solar heating and Earth's rotation. These circulation patterns determine where precipitation falls, where deserts form, and how ocean surface currents move. The Coriolis effect deflects moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere, creating the characteristic wind belts and ocean gyres.

Hadley Cells (0°–30°)
Warm, moist air rises at the equator (the ITCZ — Intertropical Convergence Zone), cools, drops heavy precipitation → tropical rainforests. Air moves poleward, descends at ~30° latitude as dry air → subtropical deserts (Sahara, Arabian, Sonoran). This is why deserts cluster at 30° N and S latitude.
Ferrel Cells (30°–60°)
Mid-latitude cells where air rises at ~60° (creating the temperate rainy zone — home to temperate forests) and sinks at ~30°. Generate the prevailing westerlies — winds that blow from west to east across North America and Europe. These carry weather systems and moisture.
Polar Cells (60°–90°)
Cold, dense air sinks at the poles and flows equatorward, creating the polar easterlies. Where polar and temperate air masses meet is the polar front — a region of frequent storms and precipitation that drives much of mid-latitude weather.
Trade Winds
In the tropics (0°–30°), surface air flows from subtropical highs toward the equator, deflected by the Coriolis effect → northeast trade winds (NH) and southeast trade winds (SH). Historically used by sailing ships. Drive equatorial ocean surface currents. Their weakening during El Niño disrupts upwelling.
Coriolis Effect
Earth's rotation causes moving air (and water) to deflect: rightward in the Northern Hemisphere, leftward in the Southern Hemisphere. This creates the curving of wind belts, the spiral of cyclones/hurricanes (counterclockwise in NH, clockwise in SH), and ocean gyres. NOT a force — it's an apparent deflection due to Earth's rotation beneath moving objects.
Ocean Gyres
Large circular ocean surface current systems driven by wind patterns and Coriolis deflection. Clockwise in NH, counterclockwise in SH. Western boundary currents (like the Gulf Stream) are warm and fast; eastern boundary currents are cool and slow. Gyres concentrate marine debris in "garbage patches" where currents converge.

ITCZ and Seasonal Migration

The Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) migrates north and south following the sun's direct rays, causing monsoons — seasonal reversals of wind direction that bring predictable wet and dry seasons across South Asia, West Africa, and parts of the Americas. When the ITCZ shifts north in summer (NH), it draws moist ocean air over South Asia → heavy monsoon rains. This is why tropical regions don't have four seasons like temperate zones — they have wet and dry seasons.

Rain Shadow Effect

Moist prevailing winds rise over mountains → cool → drop precipitation on the windward side. Air descends dry and warm on the leeward (downwind) side → rain shadow desert. Examples: Sierra Nevada → Great Basin Desert; Cascades → Columbia Plateau; Andes → Atacama (world's driest non-polar desert).
11 · Solar Radiation and Earth's Seasons

Why Seasons Exist and How Insolation Drives Climate

Earth's seasons are caused by its axial tilt of ~23.5° relative to its orbital plane — NOT by its distance from the Sun (Earth is actually closest to the Sun in January). As Earth orbits, the hemisphere tilted toward the Sun receives more direct sunlight, longer days, and higher solar intensity — causing summer. This tilt also determines which latitudes receive direct vs. angled sunlight year-round, which is the primary driver of biome distribution.

Direct vs. Angled Sunlight
At the equator, sunlight strikes nearly perpendicular year-round — concentrated energy → high temperatures. At higher latitudes, sunlight strikes at an angle, spreading over a larger area → less energy per m² → cooler climates. This is also why polar regions are cold: same amount of solar energy spread over a larger surface, AND light passes through more atmosphere (more scattering).
Solstices and Equinoxes
Summer solstice (~June 21): NH tilted maximally toward Sun; longest day, most intense solar radiation in NH. Winter solstice (~Dec 21): NH tilted away; shortest day. Equinoxes (~Mar 20, Sep 22): neither hemisphere tilted toward Sun; 12 hours day/night everywhere. Key: Southern Hemisphere seasons are opposite to Northern Hemisphere.
Solar Intensity (Insolation)
Insolation = incoming solar radiation. Measured in W/m². Factors reducing insolation at surface: (1) angle of incidence — oblique angles spread light over more area; (2) atmospheric path length — light passes through more atmosphere at high latitudes, increasing scattering and absorption. Tropics receive highest insolation year-round → drive atmospheric and oceanic circulation.
Albedo and Surface Absorption
Albedo is the fraction of solar radiation reflected by a surface. Fresh snow: ~80–90% reflected. Ocean: ~6% reflected (absorbs most). Forests: ~10–15% reflected. As ice melts due to warming, darker land/ocean is exposed → less reflection → more absorption → more warming → ice-albedo positive feedback. This amplifies warming at the poles (polar amplification).
Latitude and Biome Distribution
The combination of solar angle (temperature) and atmospheric circulation (precipitation) determines biome location. Tropical rainforests at 0°–10° (high insolation + ITCZ rain). Subtropical deserts at 25°–35° (high insolation + descending dry air). Temperate forests at 40°–60° (moderate sun + prevailing westerlies bring rain). Tundra/boreal forest at 60°–70° (low sun angle, cold).
Seasons and Productivity
Longer days in summer → more hours of photosynthesis → peak NPP. Deciduous forests shed leaves in winter to conserve water and energy when light is insufficient. Many animals migrate or hibernate in response to seasonal shifts in food availability driven by insolation changes. Phenology (seasonal timing of biological events) is shifting as climate change alters seasonal patterns.

APES Exam Focus

Questions often ask: why are tropical regions warmer year-round (sun angle, not distance); why are polar regions cold (oblique light + long atmospheric path); why do seasons occur (axial tilt, not distance); how does albedo change with ice loss; how does solar radiation interact with greenhouse gases and the atmosphere. Do NOT confuse axial tilt with distance from the Sun as the cause of seasons.

Soil Horizon Memory Aid

O-A-E-B-C Top to Bottom

  • O = Organic litter (surface debris)
  • A = topsoil — most fertile, most critical for agriculture
  • E = Eluviation / leaching zone
  • B = Buildup (iron, clay, minerals washed down)
  • C = Crumbled parent rock → bedrock below
Atmosphere

Two Problems, Two Layers

  • Greenhouse effect: IR radiation + CO₂/CH₄/H₂O/N₂O → troposphere warming
  • Ozone depletion: UV + CFCs → stratospheric ozone loss → more UV at surface
  • Temperature inversion: warm cap traps cool air + pollutants near surface
  • Albedo: high = more reflection = cooler; low = more absorption = warmer
Soil Texture Shortcut

Sand · Clay · Loam

  • Sand: large pores → fast drain, LOW nutrient/water retention
  • Clay: tiny pores → slow drain (LOW permeability), HIGH surface area → holds MORE water than sand
  • Loam = best: balanced drainage + nutrient holding
  • Porosity = Vw ÷ Vt; clay porosity HIGH but permeability LOW
