Unit Overview

Earth's Changing Systems

Unit 9 examines how human activity alters Earth's atmosphere and climate at a planetary scale. Two headline topics dominate: climate change driven by greenhouse gas accumulation, and stratospheric ozone depletion caused by CFCs and halons. These are related but distinct problems with different causes, mechanisms, and solutions.

Why It Matters

Cause → Effect → Response

The core reasoning pattern for global change is always: identify the cause (emissions, land use), trace the physical/chemical mechanism (greenhouse effect, catalytic ozone destruction), predict the impacts (warming, sea-level rise, UV increase), and evaluate mitigation or adaptation responses.

Big Picture
Energy budget · Greenhouse gases · Feedback loops · Climate impacts · Adaptation · Mitigation · Ozone layer · CFCs · Montreal Protocol · Biodiversity · Invasive species
Energy & Greenhouse Effect

Earth's Heat Balance

Shortwave in, longwave out. Greenhouse gases trap outgoing infrared. Albedo controls reflection. Understanding the energy budget explains all of climate change.
Evidence & Drivers

Climate Science Basics

Temperature records, ice cores, sea-level rise, and ecosystem shifts all point the same direction. Human emissions are the primary modern driver; natural factors don't explain recent warming.
Climate Impacts

Sea Level, Heat & Ecosystems

Thermal expansion and land-ice melt raise seas. Species shift ranges or face extinction. Crops, infrastructure, and public health are all affected — unevenly by region and wealth.
Adaptation

Reducing Harm

Adaptation adjusts to unavoidable change: seawalls, drought-tolerant crops, heat warning systems, managed retreat. Distinct from mitigation, which cuts emissions.
Mitigation

Cutting Emissions & Sinks

Decarbonize electricity, electrify transport, protect forests. Policy tools include carbon taxes, cap-and-trade, fuel standards, and international agreements like the Paris Agreement.
Ozone Layer

CFCs & Stratospheric Depletion

CFCs are stable enough to reach the stratosphere, where UV releases chlorine that catalytically destroys ozone. Strongest over Antarctica. The Montreal Protocol reversed the trend.
Ozone Impacts & Solutions

UV-B, Health & Policy

Ozone thinning increases UV-B reaching Earth: more skin cancer, cataracts, immune suppression, damage to phytoplankton and crops. Montreal Protocol = major policy success story.
Biodiversity

Invasive Species & Endangerment

Climate change shifts habitats, blocking migration corridors. Invasive species traits, endangered species characteristics, and strategies to protect biodiversity are all high-yield exam topics.

01 · Earth's Energy Budget & the Greenhouse Effect

How Greenhouse Gases Warm the Planet

Global change refers to large-scale shifts in Earth's climate system driven by human activity over decades to centuries. The two headline topics are climate change (greenhouse gas accumulation) and ozone depletion (CFC chemistry). They are different problems — different causes, mechanisms, and solutions.

Earth's Energy Budget

Incoming: shortwave solar radiation (visible + UV) Some reflected by clouds, ice, aerosols (high albedo = more reflection) Remainder absorbed by surface and atmosphere → warms the planet Earth emits: longwave infrared (heat) radiation back to space Greenhouse gases intercept outgoing infrared → re-emit in all directions → surface warms If absorbed energy > outgoing energy → net warming until new balance
Albedo
Fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected. High albedo (ice, snow, light clouds) = more cooling. Low albedo (oceans, forests, asphalt) = more absorption and warming. Formula: Reflected energy = albedo × incoming energy.
Greenhouse Effect (Natural)
Sunlight → surface absorbs and warms → emits infrared → greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit infrared in all directions, including downward → Earth is warmer than it would be without GHGs. Not inherently bad — without it, Earth would be frozen.
Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)
Sources: fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, cement production; also natural (respiration, volcanoes). Accumulates in atmosphere because it persists for centuries. Primary long-term driver of modern warming.
Methane (CH₄)
Sources: livestock digestion, rice paddies, landfills, fossil fuel extraction, wastewater. More potent warming effect per molecule than CO₂ but shorter-lived. Reducing methane gives faster short-term benefits.
Nitrous Oxide (N₂O)
Sources: agricultural soils (especially from nitrogen fertilizer), manure management, industrial processes. Very high warming potential per molecule. Primarily from agriculture.
Water Vapor
Most abundant greenhouse gas, but primarily a feedback, not the original long-term driver. Warm air holds more moisture → strengthens greenhouse effect → amplifies CO₂-initiated warming.
Fluorinated Gases
Industrial processes, refrigeration, consumer products. Includes HFCs, PFCs, and sulfur hexafluoride (SF₆). Very high warming potential per molecule; though small in volume, very significant pound-for-pound.
Not Greenhouse Gases
Oxygen (O₂) and nitrogen (N₂) do NOT absorb infrared radiation effectively. Black carbon (soot) is not a gas — it's a particle that warms by absorbing sunlight and darkening ice surfaces (lowers albedo).

Key Positive Feedback Loops

Ice–Albedo Feedback
Warming → melts ice/snow → exposes darker surfaces → lower albedo → more solar absorption → more warming → more melting. Explains polar amplification (poles warming faster than global average).
Water Vapor Feedback
Warming → more evaporation → more atmospheric water vapor → stronger greenhouse effect → more warming. Amplifies initial CO₂-driven change.
Arctic Methane Feedback
Warming → thaws permafrost → frozen organic matter decomposes → releases methane (and CO₂) → more warming → more thaw. An important tipping-point concern.
Exam Focus
Greenhouse effect traps OUTGOING infrared, not incoming sunlight. Water vapor is a feedback, not the original cause. O₂ and N₂ are not GHGs. Black carbon is not a gas. Positive feedbacks amplify change — they are NOT the original cause. Albedo change is crucial: ice loss → lower albedo → more warming.
02 · Evidence for Climate Change & Its Drivers

Multiple Lines of Evidence & Human vs. Natural Causes

Climate is the long-term pattern of weather over decades; weather is short-term. A cold week does not contradict a long-term warming trend. Climate change conclusions emerge from many independent lines of evidence ("consilience") that all point the same direction.

Instrumental Temperature Records
Long-term trends in global average surface temperature from weather stations and ocean buoys show clear, sustained warming since the Industrial Revolution, with the most rapid warming in recent decades.
Ocean Heat Content
Oceans absorb most of the excess heat entering the climate system. Even when surface air temps vary year-to-year, ocean heat content rises consistently — a powerful long-term indicator of warming.
Glacier & Ice Sheet Mass Loss
Glaciers worldwide have retreated significantly since the late 1800s. Ice cores from Antarctica and Greenland preserve bubbles of ancient atmosphere, showing CO₂ and temperature have historically moved together over hundreds of thousands of years.
Sea-Level Rise
Sea level has risen about 120 m since the last ice age peak (~18,000 years ago). Modern rise is accelerating, driven by thermal expansion and land-ice melt — measured at roughly 3 mm/year in recent decades.
Phenology & Range Shifts
Earlier flowering, breeding, and migration times. Species moving poleward or to higher elevations. These biological responses track temperature changes independently of physical records.
Paleoclimate Proxies
Ice cores, tree rings, sediment cores, and corals show climate history over millennia. These confirm modern warming is rapid relative to natural variation and tightly linked to CO₂ concentrations.

Anthropogenic vs. Natural Drivers

Human (Anthropogenic) Drivers
Fossil fuel combustion (CO₂) · Deforestation/land-use change (less carbon sequestration, more CO₂ release) · Agriculture (CH₄ from livestock/rice, N₂O from fertilizers) · Industrial processes (cement produces CO₂; some industries emit fluorinated gases) · Waste management (landfill CH₄).
Solar Variability
Solar output changes in cycles but typical temperature influence is small (~0.1°C between maxima/minima). Cannot explain the rate or magnitude of modern warming. Direction of forcing also doesn't match recent temperature rise.
Volcanic Eruptions
Large eruptions inject aerosols into the stratosphere that reflect sunlight → temporary cooling, not sustained warming. Significant for short-term effects only; not the cause of modern trend.
Milankovitch Cycles
Long-term changes in Earth's orbital shape, axial tilt, and wobble. Operate over thousands to tens of thousands of years. Way too slow to explain rapid recent warming.
Exam Focus
One cold event ≠ climate trend. Volcanoes usually COOL short-term. Solar cycles can't explain the rate of modern warming. Correlation + mechanism + multiple lines of evidence = strong case. Deforestation affects CO₂ by removing sinks AND releasing stored carbon. Ocean CO₂ uptake affects both heat storage and ocean chemistry (acidification).
03 · Impacts of Climate Change

Heat, Sea Level, Ecosystems & Human Health

Climate change shifts averages and changes the probability of extremes. Effects are uneven by region, latitude, and socioeconomic status — a core environmental justice dimension. Communities with fewer resources face higher risks and have less capacity to adapt.

Heat & Precipitation
Heat waves become more frequent and intense. Precipitation shifts: some regions face more flooding, others more drought. Warmer oceans increase potential energy for stronger tropical cyclones. Regional patterns diverge — not "everywhere hotter and wetter."
Sea-Level Rise: 2 Mechanisms
1. Thermal expansion — water expands as it warms. 2. Melting land ice — glaciers and ice sheets add water to ocean. Melting floating sea ice does NOT significantly raise sea level (already displaces water), but strongly affects albedo and ecosystems.
Antarctica & Ice Facts
Antarctica holds ~90% of world's ice and ~70% of its freshwater. If all Antarctic ice melted, sea level would rise ~60 m (~200 ft). Glacier retreat already affects freshwater availability in regions relying on seasonal meltwater.
Permafrost Thaw
Thawing permafrost releases previously frozen organic carbon as methane and CO₂ → positive feedback. Also damages infrastructure (roads, buildings) built on frozen ground. Especially significant in Arctic regions.
Ocean Acidification
CO₂ + seawater → carbonic acid → lower pH → reduced carbonate availability → corals and shellfish struggle to build shells/skeletons. Distinct from acid rain. Driven by CO₂ uptake, not temperature alone.
Ecosystem & Biodiversity Impacts
Species shift poleward or upslope. Phenology changes (earlier flowering, migration). Species unable to move or adapt fast enough face higher extinction risk. Habitat fragmentation blocks migration corridors, compounding climate stress.
Agriculture & Food Security
Heat stress, shifting rainfall, increased pests, and extreme-event risk reduce crop yields. Some regions gain longer growing seasons, but benefits are often constrained by water availability and heat extremes.
Human Health Pathways
Heat-related illness/mortality · Air quality degradation (higher ground-level ozone, wildfire smoke) · Vector-borne diseases expanding (mosquitoes, ticks carrying malaria, dengue, Zika, yellow fever) · Waterborne disease risks from warmer water · Coastal flooding damage.
Displacement Risk
Estimates suggest hundreds of millions may need relocation due to coastal flooding, shoreline erosion, and agricultural disruption — often cited as ~150 million people by 2050. Disproportionately affects low-income coastal communities.
Exam Focus
SEA ICE melt ≠ major sea level driver (land ice is key). Thermal expansion AND land ice melt both matter. Ocean acidification ≠ acid rain. Regional impacts vary — not uniform globally. Connect each impact to its climate variable (temperature, precipitation, sea level, or extremes). Environmental justice: who is most vulnerable, who caused the problem?
04 · Adapting to Climate Change

Reducing Harm & Building Resilience

Adaptation means adjusting human systems to reduce harm from climate impacts that are already occurring or unavoidable. It is not a substitute for mitigation. The key distinction:

Mitigation = reduce the SIZE of climate change (cut GHG emissions, increase sinks) Adaptation = reduce the DAMAGE from climate change (prepare for flooding, heat, drought)
Infrastructure & Engineering
Elevating buildings, redesigning stormwater systems, improving drainage. Seawalls, levees, and surge barriers protect coasts but are expensive and can create "lock-in" — failure under extreme conditions is catastrophic.
Nature-Based Solutions
Restoring wetlands and mangroves reduces storm surge and erosion while providing habitat. Urban tree canopy reduces heat islands and improves air quality. Often cheaper and more resilient than hard engineering.
Water & Agriculture Adaptations
Drought-tolerant crops, drip irrigation, adjusting planting dates. Reduces crop losses under hotter/drier conditions. Connects land management to food security.
Public Health Preparedness
Heat warning systems, cooling centers with targeted outreach for vulnerable populations, disaster planning for floods/hurricanes/wildfires. Reduce mortality and economic disruption without large capital costs.
Managed Retreat
Moving infrastructure away from high-risk coastal zones. Most sustainable long-term option where risk is extreme. Socially and politically difficult when culture, livelihoods, and property are tied to place.
Equity in Adaptation
Resilience is stronger with reliable infrastructure, social networks, healthcare, financial resources, and good governance. Low-income communities face higher risks but have fewer adaptation resources — a core environmental justice concern.
Exam Focus
Solar panels are MITIGATION, not adaptation. Seawalls can harm wetlands (unintended consequence). Air conditioning as adaptation can increase emissions (tradeoff). Explain mechanism: how does the adaptation reduce risk? Consider equity: adaptations must be realistic for low-income communities, not only expensive engineering. Managed retreat is a legitimate option — acknowledge social tradeoffs.
05 · Mitigating Climate Change

Cutting Greenhouse Gases & Increasing Carbon Sinks

Mitigation aims to limit future climate change by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and enhancing carbon sequestration — capturing carbon in biomass, soils, or geologic formations. Connect each strategy to a specific emission source.

Decarbonizing Electricity
Solar, wind, hydroelectric, geothermal: low direct CO₂ emissions. Nuclear: low direct CO₂ with tradeoffs (radioactive waste, cost, public perception). Energy efficiency: same output with less electricity (LEDs, insulation, efficient motors).
Electrifying End Uses
If electricity is cleaner, shift cars, heating, and some industry from direct fuel combustion to electricity. Electric vehicles reduce tailpipe emissions; total benefit depends on the electricity mix (still helps if grid is cleaner than gasoline).
Reducing Transportation Emissions
Public transit, biking, walkable city design (reduces vehicle miles), higher fuel efficiency, and alternative fuels. Multiple strategies stack — efficiency × cleaner fuels × reduced travel distance.
Cutting CH₄ & N₂O
Capture methane from landfills or manure lagoons. Reduce leaks from natural gas systems. Improve fertilizer management (right source, rate, time, place) to reduce N₂O from soils. High warming potential per molecule → quick relative benefits.
Carbon Sinks & Land Use
Reforestation and afforestation increase carbon storage. Soil conservation increases soil organic carbon. Key nuance: forests store carbon but become sources if burned or degraded — long-term protection is required.
Renewable Tradeoffs
"Renewable" ≠ no environmental impact. Hydroelectric dams alter ecosystems. Wind and solar require materials and land. APES rewards recognizing tradeoffs while still comparing carbon benefits — don't treat renewables as perfect.

Policy Tools

Carbon Tax
Fee proportional to carbon content of fuels or CO₂ emissions. Makes high-carbon choices more expensive → incentivizes low-carbon alternatives. Broad and simple price signal. Political resistance; equity concerns if not redistributed.
Cap-and-Trade
Sets a total emissions limit (CAP) and issues tradeable allowances. The CAP guarantees total limit; TRADE is the market mechanism letting reductions happen where cheapest. Don't confuse: the cap is the environmental limit, the trade is the flexibility mechanism.
Regulations & Standards
Fuel economy standards, emissions performance standards, renewable portfolio standards, building codes, appliance efficiency standards. Reduce emissions through rules rather than prices.
Kyoto Protocol (2005)
International agreement under UN framework with binding GHG reduction targets for developed (Annex I) countries. First major international climate commitment. Limited reach — U.S. did not ratify.
Paris Agreement (2016)
Goal: keep warming well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels. Each country sets its own plans (Nationally Determined Contributions). Broader participation than Kyoto. Non-binding targets but with transparency and review framework.
Exam Focus
"Recycling more" is NOT a primary GHG mitigation strategy. Cap-and-trade does NOT mean each firm reduces equally — trading allows flexibility. Carbon sequestration in forests is only reliable with long-term protection. Connect each mitigation strategy to the sector and gas it targets. Kyoto = binding; Paris = nationally determined contributions.
06 · The Stratospheric Ozone Layer

CFCs, Catalytic Destruction & the Ozone Hole

Ozone (O₃) has opposite roles depending on altitude. The critical distinction: stratospheric ozone is protective; tropospheric ozone is a pollutant. "Ozone is good up high, bad nearby."

Stratospheric ozone (15–40 km up): absorbs harmful UV-B radiation → beneficial, protective shield Tropospheric ozone (near ground): major component of photochemical smog → lung damage, plant harm Same molecule, opposite roles → altitude matters
UV Radiation Types
UVA: closest to visible light; associated with tanning. UVB: causes blistering sunburns; linked to skin cancer; ozone shields most of this. UVC: largely absorbed in upper atmosphere; involved in ozone formation reactions.
Natural Ozone Balance
UV splits O₂ → atomic oxygen. Atomic oxygen combines with O₂ → O₃ (ozone). Ozone also breaks back to O₂. Concentration depends on balance of formation and destruction — naturally maintained.
CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons)
Nonflammable compounds (carbon, chlorine, fluorine). Were used in aerosol propellants, refrigeration, and foam-blowing. Chemically stable in lower atmosphere → persist long enough to reach stratosphere → UV breaks them apart → releases chlorine atoms.
Halocarbons / Halons
Organic molecules with at least one carbon and one or more halogens (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine). Used in fire suppression. Bromine is even more effective at catalytic ozone destruction than chlorine.
Catalytic Destruction
Chlorine (or bromine) atoms from CFC breakdown destroy ozone in a catalytic cycle — the halogen atom is NOT consumed and can destroy thousands of ozone molecules. One CFC molecule → huge ozone damage.
Antarctic Ozone Hole
Ozone depletion is most severe over Antarctica because extreme cold creates polar stratospheric clouds. Reactions on cloud surfaces convert chlorine to forms that rapidly destroy ozone when spring sunlight returns. The polar vortex isolates Antarctic air, intensifying the effect.
Exam Focus
The "ozone hole" is thinning, NOT a literal hole. Ozone depletion ≠ global warming (different layers, chemicals, effects). CFCs work because they are STABLE (reach stratosphere), then UV breaks them. Catalytic = one chlorine atom destroys many ozone molecules. UVB causes skin cancer and cataracts — ozone depletion increases UVB reaching surface.
07 · Ozone Depletion Impacts & Solutions

UV-B Effects & the Montreal Protocol

When stratospheric ozone decreases, more UV-B radiation reaches Earth's surface. UV-B damages tissues and DNA. These impacts are about ultraviolet exposure, not heat — do not confuse with climate change impacts.

Human Health Impacts
Higher risk of skin cancer · Increased cataracts and eye damage · Immune suppression (reduced effectiveness of immune system) · More sunburns and skin damage. These are UV-B effects, not temperature effects.
Ecosystem & Agriculture Impacts
Reduced phytoplankton growth → cascading effects through aquatic food webs. Damage to plant tissues → reduced crop yields. Increased mutations (UV changes DNA structure). Deleterious effects on animals.
Stratospheric Cooling
Ozone depletion can cool the stratosphere (less UV absorbed there). This is separate from greenhouse warming of the lower atmosphere. Keep causal chains distinct.
Materials Degradation
Increased UV accelerates breakdown of certain plastics and paints. Less obvious but real economic impact of ozone depletion.
Montreal Protocol (1987)
International treaty phasing out CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances. Worked because causes were concentrated in a narrow set of chemicals/industries, alternatives were developed, and costs/benefits were clearer than for climate change. Major policy success — ozone layer slowly recovering.
Policy Tools for Ozone
Tariffs on products from countries allowing CFC use · Tax credits/rebates for turning in old refrigerators and air conditioners · Product regulations on aerosols, inhalers, fire extinguishers · Coolant alternatives: helium, ammonia, propane, or butane where appropriate.
Exam Focus
Ozone depletion impacts = UV-B exposure, NOT temperature increase. Montreal Protocol = ozone; Paris Agreement = climate change. Recovery takes decades even with CFC phase-out. Phytoplankton damage ripples through entire marine food webs. Replacement chemicals (HFCs) have their own tradeoffs (still potent GHGs) — solutions can introduce new impacts.
08 · Biodiversity, Invasive Species & Endangered Species

Species Responses to a Changing World

Climate change threatens biodiversity by shifting habitats, altering life-cycle timing, and increasing stress from extremes. These impacts compound habitat loss and fragmentation, which can block migration corridors that would allow range shifts.

Migration, Adaptation & Acclimatization

Migration (Organism Movement)
Success depends on access routes/corridors, magnitude and rate of change, organism's ability to travel, and availability of suitable new habitats. Habitat fragmentation is the biggest barrier.
Adaptation (Genetic, Population-Level)
Ability of a population/species to survive across generations in changing conditions. Depends on birth rate, genetic variability, population size, generation length, and gene flow between populations. Slow — requires generations.
Acclimatization (Individual Physiology)
An individual organism adjusts to gradual environmental change to maintain performance. Depends on physiological/behavioral limits and rate of change. NOT genetic — occurs within one organism's lifetime.
Plants' Vulnerability
Plants cannot migrate as individuals, cannot seek water or nutrients, seedlings must survive in degraded conditions, seed dispersal can be slow. Often more immediately susceptible to habitat loss than mobile animals.

Invasive Species

Definition & Traits
Species transported to areas where it does not naturally live, then spreads. Common traits: broad diet, high dispersal, high reproduction, short generation times, wide environmental tolerance, close association with humans, genetic variability.
Dutch Elm Disease
Transmitted by elm bark beetles; fungal pathogen killed over half of elm trees in northern United States. Demonstrates how a combination of invasive vector + invasive pathogen can devastate a dominant native tree species.
European Green Crabs
Introduced to San Francisco Bay 1989; threaten commercial fisheries and native invertebrates. Competitive exclusion of native species, disruption of food webs.
Water Hyacinth
Introduced from South America; forms dense mats that reduce sunlight for submerged plants, crowd out native aquatics, and clog waterways and water intake pipes. Economic and ecological damage.
Zebra Mussels
Attach to hard surfaces: clog water intake/discharge pipes, attach to boat hulls, docks, native mussels, and crayfish. Economic damage through infrastructure impacts in addition to ecological disruption.

Endangered Species

Definition & Factors
Species facing very high risk of extinction in the wild. Assessed by breeding success, known threats, population trend, and number remaining. Factors increasing risk include low reproductive rates, limited range, specialized diet, and habitat loss.
Traits Leading to Endangerment
Low reproductive rates (whales, elephants) · Limited range (pandas) · Specialized diet (pandas) · Large territory needed (tigers) · Long/fixed migration routes (Pacific Northwest salmon) · Commercially valuable (elephants, rhinos) · Cannot adapt quickly (polar bears).
Why Protect Endangered Species?
Genetic diversity · Keystone and indicator species preserve ecosystem function · Aesthetic, ecological, educational, historical, recreational, scientific value · Potential undiscovered uses (medicines, etc.) · Intrinsic ethical value.
Biodiversity Strategies
Wildlife sanctuaries and corridors · Captive breeding programs · Habitat management and monitoring · Protective laws (Endangered Species Act) · Land trusts · Species reintroduction · Ecosystem restoration · Reducing invasive species.
Exam Focus
Adaptation (genetic, over generations) ≠ acclimatization (individual, within lifetime). Invasive species cause BOTH ecological AND economic/infrastructure damage (zebra mussels clog pipes). Habitat fragmentation blocks climate-driven range shifts — corridors are critical. Explain mechanism when proposing biodiversity strategies: how do sanctuaries or corridors reduce extinction risk?

Exam Prep

Common Mistakes & Traps

High-yield patterns · Things the AP loves to test

Trap #1

Ozone ≠ Climate Change

These are two different problems. Ozone depletion = CFCs destroying O₃ in the stratosphere → more UV-B. Climate change = GHGs trapping infrared → warming. Different chemicals, different atmospheric layers, different consequences, different solutions. Never conflate them.
Trap #2

Sea Ice vs. Land Ice

Melting sea ice does NOT significantly raise sea level (it already displaces ocean water). Melting LAND ice (glaciers, Greenland, Antarctica) adds new water to the ocean. For sea-level rise questions, land ice + thermal expansion are your two mechanisms.
Trap #3

Adaptation vs. Mitigation

Solar panels = MITIGATION (cuts emissions). Seawalls = ADAPTATION (reduces harm from sea-level rise). Cooling centers = ADAPTATION. Reforestation = MITIGATION (carbon sink). The exam loves to blur these — always ask: does the strategy reduce emissions (mitigation) or reduce harm (adaptation)?
Trap #4

Water Vapor Confusion

Water vapor is the most abundant greenhouse gas but is a FEEDBACK, not the original long-term driver. It amplifies CO₂-initiated warming because warm air holds more moisture. CO₂ drives long-term trend; water vapor amplifies it. Don't say "water vapor is the main cause of climate change."
Trap #5

Volcanoes Cool, Not Warm

Large volcanic eruptions inject aerosols into the stratosphere that REFLECT sunlight → temporary COOLING, not sustained warming. Students often say volcanoes cause warming. They don't explain the modern trend — they work in the wrong direction and the wrong timescale.
Trap #6

Adaptation vs. Acclimatization

Adaptation = genetic change at the POPULATION level across generations. Acclimatization = physiological adjustment in an INDIVIDUAL within its lifetime. One cold summer doesn't give polar bears new genetics. A plant adjusting stomatal opening to heat is acclimatization. These are very different biological processes.
Trap #7

Montreal vs. Paris

Montreal Protocol (1987) = phased out CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances → stratospheric ozone recovery. Paris Agreement (2016) = climate change, greenhouse gases, 2°C target, nationally determined contributions. Don't apply one to the other's problem.
Trap #8

Greenhouse Effect Mechanism

The greenhouse effect traps OUTGOING infrared radiation — it does NOT block incoming sunlight. Students often write "greenhouse gases block the sun." Wrong. Sunlight passes through freely; GHGs intercept the heat radiating outward from the warm surface.
